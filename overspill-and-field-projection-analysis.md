# Overspill and Field Projection in *The Faith of Beasts*

An analysis of the two interlocking concepts that frame the novella's depiction of
Carryx–deathless-enemy warfare: **field projection** (the weapon) and **overspill**
(the collateral effect bystanders feel when countermeasures can't fully dampen it).

---

## 1. Field Projection

### What it is

Field projection is the dominant form of interstellar warfare used by *both* the
Carryx and the "deathless enemy." It is a weapon, a sensor footprint, and a
signaling device all at once. Vaudai, the slug-like strategic analyst, explains its
physics succinctly:

> "Field weaponry propagates as would light or gravity. If we can see them, the
> battle has in essence already begun."

Projected fields travel at the local maximum speed — minutes to hours between
planets — so combat is conducted as a long-range exchange of expanding waves
rather than as ship-to-ship dogfights. Vaudai's tactical display shows them as
"strange projection fields that crossed, sometimes whole swaths of the vacuum
between planets."

### Physical form

The Carryx field projectors are described in biological-crystalline terms:

> "the field projectors woke on four of the ships, their crystal petals blooming
> slantwise in the void and the strange energies they commanded glowing in
> complex and symphonic patterns of the spectrum."

On approach to the target, the fields manifest visibly as a "dancing color" or
"killing aurora" — coral-to-pink-to-green-to-gold light that walks across a
planet's surface, behind which cities are reduced to "a dull red light. Fire
through smoke." At close range inside a ship it appears as a "filthy aurora,"
slicing beams that "glittered with minute, angry sparks" where they touched
matter. Matter caught in the beam is *unmade* — Campar throws his sample case
into a wave and "the terrible light unmade it."

### A taxonomy of field weapons (as named in the text)

- **Projection fields** — the general-purpose medium; both sides deploy them.
- **Resonance exclusions** — a Carryx attack form tuned to hit specific targets;
  at Ashtin-Kah they "focused on [the enemy's] command structure" instead of
  the broadest kill count.
- **Fundamental decoherence zones** — zones of collapsed quantum coherence
  "still evaporating" hours after the battle.
- **Field suppressors** — defensive tech the Jurupe defenders "arc through the
  skin of [their] atmosphere" to cancel inbound fields.
- **Fields of stealth** — used to obscure ships; tellingly, the Carryx at
  Jurupe *don't* use them, because they want the prey to scream.

### Strategic purpose

Field projection is not only destructive; it is designed to be *heard*. The
subjugator-librarian at Jurupe uses its field projectors as bait:

> "The animals of the planet… would hear the field weapons like a footstep in
> the dark. Because they heard, they would look, because they looked, they
> would see, and because they saw they would scream out in fear and anguish,
> begging their protectors for aid. It was the screaming that the Carryx
> required."

The whole Jurupe operation is a provocation to draw the deathless enemy's
reinforcements into a killing ground. Field projection is thus inseparable from
the Carryx's preferred mode of war — one fought through sensor-signatures, bait,
and ecological annihilation, not through occupation.

## 2. Overspill

### What it is

Overspill is the residue of a field attack that gets past the defender's
countermeasures. The coinage is Vaudai's, translating to Rickar after the first
attack aboard the Carryx ship:

> "The enemy set a trap with projection fields…. the masters put countermeasures
> in place. We suffered what little couldn't be dampened out. Overspill."

A ship's shielding, dampers, and hardened safe-rooms absorb most incoming field
energy. What leaks through is still enough to injure, derange, or kill any
crew who aren't inside hardened sections.

### What it feels like

The text gives overspill a consistent phenomenology — it is not just radiation,
it is a perceptual and metaphysical assault:

- **Quantum-scale prickling:** "A sensation like a thousand invisible claws
  gently pricking at him passed over his skin."
- **Time and perception breakdown:** "Blank spots appeared in his field of
  vision. The chaos of the room seemed to stutter like time had bits cut out of
  it and the broken ends jammed together."
- **Metaphysical dread:** "a deep sense of nightmarish wrongness, the universe
  slipping off its mask and letting him see the monstrous face underneath."
- **Convulsion and burning:** "the vacuum in the hallway seemed to fizz, and
  Campar felt his muscles twitching like a billion tiny electrodes"; later, it
  blisters his bare skin to "a white-and-red mass of blisters."
- **Environmental damage:** "The overspill seemed to have knocked out a few of
  the ship's lights and set others to an uneven strobing."

Vaudai's dry summary: "It is like painful defecation and leaves me with an
ache."

### Overspill vs. direct hit

An important distinction: a direct field strike is lethal and near-instant
(the Jurupe surface under the aurora, or the unmade sample case). Overspill is
the *attenuated* version — painful, damaging, sometimes fatal, but survivable
inside shelter. The narrative concern of the ship-bound chapters is always
timing the gaps between overspill waves to move between hardened rooms.

## 3. Species and Characters Involved

### The Budon of Luus ("fluting-stink-sacks")

Bird-like singers whose organic biology makes them living quantum-flux sensors:

> "The fluting-stink-sacks sense variations in quantum flux a thousand times
> better than the best half-mind. It is why they are here. They sang, it raised
> the alarm, the masters put countermeasures in place."

Their song is the ship's early-warning system for incoming field attacks;
every overspill sequence in the novella is prefaced by Budon singing. They pay
for it — two are killed in the first attack. Their placement on Carryx ships is
explicitly about detecting field projection before it can be seen.

### Vaudai ("giant slug" — analyst of violence)

The Carryx's embedded strategic analyst. Vaudai is a slug-like being —
"a vast, blue-gray mass of flesh roughly the size of a side of beef." Its
species is not named in the text; Rickar notes "It called itself Vaudai, but
Rickar didn't know if that was its name or its species." It is *not* a Sinen
(the goat/cuttlefish-like overseers who run moieties and field camps), nor is
it itself a "half-mind" — in this novella "half-mind" refers to the
translation/computational device worn on the body (or mounted in ships and
weapons), not to a species. Vaudai *has* a half-mind through which it speaks
("Vaudai's translation half-mind said"; "its half-mind spoke"; "the
translation half-mind didn't recognize their interest until Campar reached
out and touched the couch-sized slug thing on its side"). What Vaudai does
tell us about its kind is functional: "The gift of my kind is to ponder the
patterns of violence." Vaudai:

- Names the phenomenon ("Overspill") and explains the physics to the humans.
- Secretes a hard white resin to anchor itself to the deck during waves —
  its species-specific adaptation to surviving overspill.
- Reads the tactical display showing projection fields stretched between
  planets and predicts enemy moves from their geometry.
- Explicitly frames its purpose: "The gift of my kind is to ponder the patterns
  of violence."

Vaudai is the reader's tutor for every technical concept — resonance
exclusions, decoherence zones, propagation speed — all arrive through its
mouth.

### The Carryx and their clients (subjugators, subjugator-librarians, Soft Lothark, Sinen)

Operators and beneficiaries of the system. The Carryx subjugator-librarian
commands the field projectors at Jurupe. The Soft Lothark — a client soldier
species — brace on the deck during overspill; the detail that even they are
afraid of it ("their tiny black eyes wide and limbs trembling") is one of the
few moments the reader sees fear on the winning side. Sinen overseers (a
goat/cuttlefish-like client species that staffs moieties and field camps)
treat the survivors' blister wounds afterward.

### The Deathless Enemy

Also deploys projection fields — the opening attack and the Jurupe defensive
barrage are theirs. The novella's horror sequence inside the abandoned
command ship features a three-tentacled deathless-enemy remnant that *also*
knows enough to seek hardened rooms during overspill:

> "It took the other safe room. It seems disoriented by its amputations, but
> it knew what it meant when the overspill came. It still thinks, somehow."

Vaudai classifies this as typical of "the army of the dead."

### Rickar

The human POV for observing overspill as a passenger. He experiences the
full perceptual breakdown during the first attack, recovers, and by the
Jurupe chapter has habituated enough to eat mush while watching a planet die.
His arc tracks the normalization of atrocity: "I made myself forget what the
Carryx are… Now I remember why I made myself forget."

### Campar and Ghati

The human POV for overspill as a *tool*. Trapped in an evacuated Carryx
command ship with Vaudai, hunted by a wounded deathless-enemy shape-shifter,
Campar weaponizes the incoming overspill cycle itself: he lures the creature
through unshielded corridors so the next wave will catch it in the open. It
works — the enemy is unmade in the aurora, Campar is burned nearly to death,
and he throws his Carryx sample case into the light as a final act of
refusal. This is the single most extended *use* of overspill as a plot
mechanism in the book.

## 4. How Overspill and Field Projection Impact the Story

### They define what "war" looks like in this universe

Every battle in *The Faith of Beasts* is fought through fields, not bullets.
Ships don't close — they project. Planets don't fall to invasion — they fall
to a walking aurora. This gives Corey's war a specific, alien texture: it is
long-range, nearly invisible until too late, and measured in propagation times
of light-minutes. It also makes the war legible only through translation —
humans can't see it directly, so Vaudai reading a tactical display becomes the
only access point.

### They are the mechanism of the Jurupe genocide

The novella's atrocity set-piece — the extermination of billions on Jurupe —
is executed entirely by field projectors. Rickar and Dervan watch the
"killing aurora" walk across the night side of the planet in real time:

> "Where the aurora had been, there was no green on the land, no blue in the
> water. Only a slowly swirling darkness dimming a fiery red glow."

The fact that the weapon is beautiful ("God, it's so beautiful") before it is
revealed as genocidal gives the sequence its moral weight. Field projection is
what makes the Carryx's casual planetary erasure *possible* and aesthetically
unbearable.

### They make the humans into captive witnesses

Because overspill is what humans feel and field projection is what the masters
deploy, humans in this book experience war as pure passivity: perceptual
collapse, blistered skin, survivor's guilt. The recurring line "We're mice on
the fire team. We go where they take us" is a direct consequence of the
physics. You cannot fight a field weapon with your body. You can only
shelter, witness, and survive — or, like Campar, use the wave as a weapon of
last resort.

### They reveal the Budon's and Vaudai's purpose

Both species are on the ship *because of* field projection. The Budon detect
it; Vaudai analyzes it. Understanding what field projection is retroactively
explains every cross-species detail of the Carryx's mixed-ecology warships — a
Carryx ship is assembled around the problem of surviving and returning
field-fire.

### They set up Campar's sacrifice

The novella's emotional climax — Campar leading the deathless remnant on a
corridor loop until the next overspill wave catches them both — only works if
the reader has absorbed three chapters of overspill as ambient environmental
hazard. By the time Campar makes his plan, the weapon is familiar enough to
be turned on an enemy. Overspill is the mechanism; it is also the theme: the
story argues that in a war this big, the residue is the only thing clients
ever get to touch, and the only thing they can ever use.

### They frame the deathless enemy as a mirror, not an "other"

Both sides use projection fields. Both sides suffer overspill. The
three-tentacled remnant on the abandoned ship seeks a hardened safe room for
the same reason Campar does. This symmetry — subtle, buried in the
technology — undercuts the Carryx's framing of the deathless enemy as
wholly alien. They are playing the same game with the same tools. The
only asymmetry is that the Carryx keep their client animals on board to
witness it.

## Summary

**Field projection** is the physics of this war: crystal-petal projectors
blooming on ships, propagating at light-speed, manifesting as killing
auroras, and named variously as projection fields, resonance exclusions,
decoherence zones, field suppressors, and fields of stealth. **Overspill** is
what those fields do to you when you're a passenger on the ship that survived
the attack — the attenuated leak of the weapon past the dampers, experienced
as seizure, vision loss, metaphysical wrongness, and burned skin.

The Budon sense the incoming fields. Vaudai explains them. The Carryx wield
them. The deathless enemy trades them blow for blow. The humans — Rickar,
Campar, Ghati, Dervan — endure the overspill and, once, turn it into a weapon
of their own. The Jurupe chapters show what field projection looks like from
the giving end: a planet of billions, unmade by a walking light.
