# The Five-Limbed Captives and Their Relationship to Humans

A comprehensive textual evidence compendium drawn from *The Mercy of Gods* (Book 1) and *The Faith of Beasts* (Book 2) of *The Captive's War* by James S. A. Corey.

The "captured five-limbed enemies" introduced in *The Mercy of Gods* are the **fivefold captives of Ayayeh** — also called the **deathless**, **pentapus / pentapods**, or "**starfish octopuses**." This compendium traces every reference to them and the gradually unfolding revelation that humans and the deathless enemy are related — that the human moiety on Anjiin is biochemically, genetically, and culturally the same species as the Carryx's "great enemy."

---

## I. The Captives in *The Mercy of Gods*: First Appearance

### Capture at Ayayeh (Chapter 14)

The Carryx subjugator-librarian Ekur-Tkalal arrives at the planet **Ayayeh** to subjugate its apparent native species, the hexapod **Eelie**. The conquest goes catastrophically wrong:

> "In the planet's northern hemisphere, a collection of the Eelie in the valley of a vast plain drew the attention of the prime. It directed one hundred Carryx and two thousand animals of violence to descend on the indigenous population for acquisition and subjugation. Half of their animals of violence died, and a third of the Carryx."
> — Chapter 14

The prime reports back the truth that triggers the rest of the plot:

> "The species is not as the probes and void tendrils reported. We have only collected a few of them intact, but they are not from this world… They are unrelated to the planetary ecosystem. **Manufactured life-forms intended to trick us into believing they are the indigenous species. This is a trap.**"
> — Chapter 14

The Eelie are an artificial decoy ecosystem; the actual enemy emerges from rifts in space:

> "From the three rifts, ships began to spill out into normal space, blinking into reality from whatever non-dimension the enemy employed to undo the limits of the local universe."
> — Chapter 14

### The "Deathless" Behavior in Battle (Chapter 14)

The first description of the enemy soldiers' uncanny resilience comes through the Carryx's combat reports:

> "Ekur-Tkalal knew that the enemy was virtually deathless, that their animals of violence could be riddled with injuries and flow forward like a tide. **The heat and pulse of the living organism could fade without ending its assault.**"
> — Chapter 14

> "The enemy bled black and red and clear. Their voices were a mesh of radio signals that danced to evade all the Carryx efforts to drown it out."
> — Chapter 14

> "Out of more than a thousand species, this enemy was the most recalcitrant…"
> — Chapter 14

### Capture and Their Fate of Choice (Chapter 14)

Through an unexpected breach in the battle, hundreds of escape pods are netted by the Carryx:

> "Traveling in the skin of that detonation, hundreds of escaping enemy wrapped in shells of titanium and deep copper fled death and found captivity. The webwork of void tendril and magnetic force gathered the escape pods like a net hauling in fish."
> — Chapter 14

> "**The deathless could still be annihilated, and the enemy would rather slip into oblivion than be yoked.**"
> — Chapter 14

> "The disabled enemy ships flickered into knots of nuclear fire and faded to ash. Whether that was the nature of the enemy or a choice to deny information to the Carryx wasn't clear."
> — Chapter 14

### First Physical Description (Chapter 14)

> "The captives were thin-bodied animals with **fivefold symmetry in their hard, crusted epidermis**. The half-mind had been observing them carefully, but had not yet gained context enough to communicate with them beyond the simplest levels."
> — Chapter 14

## II. The Captives in *The Mercy of Gods*: Interrogation (Chapter 24)

After weeks in asymmetric space, Ekur-Tkalal is reassigned to interrogate the prisoners — a humiliating posting from subjugator-librarian to interrogator-librarian.

### Physical Description Refined

> "The interrogator-librarian decided that **the metallic threads in the captives' five arms** were of interest, the white-eyed Sinen relayed the command to vivisectionists."
> — Chapter 24

> "The subject was one of twenty who had survived the process. It was in a dry tank under the ultraviolet light that the captives seemed to find noxious. **Its communication style was a combination of chemical release and electromagnetic waves**, so when they conversed, it remained silent apart from the half-mind's translation."
> — Chapter 24

The translator describes the linguistic interface:

> "The translation half-mind pulsed once as it passed this on in terms the captive could comprehend — **a mist of esters and cyclic terpenes, a burst of radio waves on a combination of frequencies**."
> — Chapter 24

### The Captives Self-Identify as Designed/Manufactured

The interrogation reveals their nature as artificial pilots:

> "Identify the star, planet, or system in which your species evolved."
> "**No such place exists. My kind are not the products of evolution.**"
> — Chapter 24

> "Identify the star, planet, or system in which your species resided before you achieved the ability to move between stars."
> "**No such place exists. We were created as tools of the war, and did not exist before the ships we pilot.**"
> — Chapter 24

> "We are designed life… **We are like half-minds made from life.**"
> — Chapter 24

### Carryx Categorization: A Perversion

> "A thrill of disgust ran through the librarian's body. The Carryx had come across manufactured life-simulacra before. Stick-flowers on Ursin-Qin, the Stone-Mind of High Lothark, the Ambients of Cahl and Deáphan. They were perversions. Imitations of mind doomed to degradation and death like an animal fed nothing but its own waste. **To build a monstrosity like that out of living flesh was depraved in a way that the librarian had never considered.**"
> — Chapter 24

### The Aunjeli Claim

The captive identifies its "creators":

> "They call themselves **Aunjeli. Their flesh is made from semi-stable plasma, and they build their cities in the coronas of stars.**"
> — Chapter 24

The Carryx librarian doubts this — and notably, the Carryx **never verify the claim**. As of the end of *Faith of Beasts*, no Aunjeli have been observed by anyone.

### Captive Defiance

> "**Go inseminate your Sovran, we aren't going to tell you feces eaters anything.**"
> — Chapter 24

### A Key Detail: The Distress Signal Reaches the Swarm

While Ekur-Tkalal interrogates, the captive transmits a distress radio signal that the human-occupying swarm intercepts. This is the first cross-species "enemy" contact:

> "It knows a distress call when it hears one."
> — Chapter 24

> "*The pause lasts less than a moment, and then a flood of information, all encrypted in ways that the swarm knows without knowing, like hearing words in a language it had forgotten it could speak. The pilot replays the battle, the trap that was sprung at Ayayeh and the damage that the Carryx sustained.*"
> — Chapter 24

The swarm — itself "a tool of war" sent to Anjiin from elsewhere — recognizes the captive's protocols **as something it already knows but does not consciously remember**. This is the first hint that the swarm and the deathless are aligned weapon systems from the same source.

## III. The Captives in *The Mercy of Gods*: Death (Chapter 34)

### All Captives Die in Custody

> "The last of the fivefold captives of Ayayeh system died reciting a series of concepts in a loop, and Ekur of the cohort Tkalal debated with itself whether the repetition was a symptom of mental collapse or a chosen death ritual. Reflex or prayer. Either way, **its five limbs spasmed, curled upward, shivered, and ended their biological activity**."
> — Chapter 34

> "The interrogator-librarian settled in its niche isolated from the animals, distilled its conclusions, and prepared to pass a report up to its superior… *The organism claimed to be artificial: a half-mind built from living tissue. The truth of the claim is ambiguous as evidence exists to both support and refute it.*"
> — Chapter 34

### The Pivotal Revelation — The First Connection to Humans

In the same chapter, immediately after reporting the captives' deaths, Ekur-Tkalal is summoned to the Sovran. The regulator-librarian announces Ekur's reassignment with this fateful sentence:

> "**There is a subject species that appears to be related biochemically to the pilot captives you brought.** We have come to honor its keeper-librarian and address an incident. Your service to the Carryx will involve these."
> — Chapter 34

That subject species is **the human moiety**. Ekur-Tkalal — the Carryx who interrogated the deathless captives — is then promoted to **keeper-librarian of the human moiety**, replacing the previous keeper, who is publicly executed on the dais by the Sovran's guards. The regulator-librarian announces the biochemical link and Ekur's reassignment in a single speech. The text does not state the reassignment is *because* of Ekur's experience with the captives, but the juxtaposition invites that reading: a librarian who studied pentapus anatomy is now in charge of a "subject species… related biochemically to the pilot captives." Critically, the Carryx have noticed the biochemical resemblance but have not connected it to their galactic war — they treat humans as a useful client population worth studying, not as the deathless enemy itself. (See Section VII below for Clae's later framing — "the enemy's tools. Their possessions" — of how the Carryx tend to read species in this position.)

This is the first textual hint that **humans are the enemy's species**, and the chapter ends with Ekur-Tkalal introducing itself to Dafyd Alkhor:

> "I am Ekur of the cohort Tkalal, and I have been made the new keeper-librarian of the human moiety."
> — Chapter 34

## IV. The Captives in *The Faith of Beasts*: The Black Things on the Dead Ship

In *Faith of Beasts*, Campar, Ghati, and the slug-alien Vaudai are sent aboard a captured "deathless enemy" command ship floating dead in Ashtin-Kah system. They examine the corpses inside.

### Five-Limbed Pilots Among Human Crew

> "In among the corpses of men and women, alien flesh lay dead as well. **Thin silver limbs that glistened in the light like salmon skin and met in a small central node. Something like octopuses, but with only five tentacles.**"
> — Chapter (the "second exploration of the dead ship" sequence)

> "The five-armed alien nearest her was next. It didn't seem at first to be wearing clothing, but there were complicated rings near the base of each tentacle that might have been keratin shell that had grown there or artifacts put in place."
> — *Faith of Beasts*

Critically, the five-armed pilots are found **side by side with human corpses**:

> "Campar pulled himself to them. The nearest was a human woman. Death bloat had stretched the lines of her face, but he could still see the crow's feet at the corners of her eyes, the laugh lines at her mouth. Her hair was white and tied back in a bun. She wore a jumpsuit with writing at the collar in a script Campar didn't know."
> — *Faith of Beasts*

> "**That's clearly a human,**" Ghati said. "**You can't tell me these aren't people.**"
> "**She is,**" Campar said. "But look over there."
> — *Faith of Beasts*

> "**Do they really not know?**" Ghati said, asking the question out loud.
> — *Faith of Beasts*

### The Ship Is Built for Humans

> "**The design looks familiar,**" Ghati said. "Don't you think?"
> "Compared with what?"
> "Everything… This looks like someone dug up a bunch of service tunnels, put an engine on one side, and shot them into space."
> "You think the design looks human."
> "**Of course it looks human,**" Ghati hissed. "Don't tell me that you don't see it too."
> — *Faith of Beasts*

> "Now that he knew humans had used these hallways, it was obvious. The doors were slightly different ratios than the ones he was used to from Anjiin. The lights were pleasant and warm to a human eye."
> — *Faith of Beasts*

### The Black "Regrowing" Soldier — A Surviving Deathless

A third "five-legged" body, blackened, refuses to be cut. Vaudai later identifies it:

> "Campar pulled himself to the black, misshapen thing and prodded it… The skin would not cut. Campar braced himself against the nearest workstation and pressed with the full mass of his body, but all he managed to do was bend the scalpel's blade. The black thing showed a thin, livid line where he'd attacked it, but that was all."
> — *Faith of Beasts*

When the ship powers back on, the black thing reactivates and pursues them:

> "The deathless enemy is on the ship with us. I am not certain how it was damaged, but as it was sessile until the ship regained power, I believe it was very badly compromised. This is to our advantage."
> — Vaudai, *Faith of Beasts*

> "**If the deathless has been reactivated by the proximity of its allied fleet, it may very well attempt to reach us here. We are, after all, enemy combatants.**"
> — Vaudai, *Faith of Beasts*

The reanimated soldier hunts them through the ship corridors:

> "At the far end of the corridor, the thing swam toward him. Its tentacles flowed with an eerie grace, twirling as it moved through the space. Only the two missing limbs gave it a sense of wrongness and unbalance."
> — *Faith of Beasts*

> "It took the other safe room… it seems disoriented by its amputations, but it knew what it meant when the overspill came. It still thinks, somehow."
> "**The army of the dead often displays this behavior,**" Vaudai replied. "Intriguing to see firsthand."
> — *Faith of Beasts*

The captive sample Campar takes — a glass jar of black material — comes alive and tries to escape:

> "In the glass jar containing the sample he'd taken from the black thing, **the specimens of dust had come together, organizing into a mechanism like a tiny drill. The tapping they'd felt was the sample throwing itself against the wall of the jar. Trying to escape.**"
> — *Faith of Beasts*

### Identification Recap (Later Conversation)

Months later, Dafyd questions Campar about what he saw:

> "The one that came back alive," Dafyd said. "Tell me about it."
> "I'm fairly certain it was one of the **starfish octopuses. Pentapus?** The remaining arms were the right length and conformation, and when it moved, it kept acting like it expected missing limbs to be there."
> — *Faith of Beasts*

This chapter introduces "**pentapus / pentapods**" as the human nickname for the five-limbed pilots.

## V. The Captives in *The Faith of Beasts*: Jessyn Discovers the Secret

While Vaudai/Campar/Ghati explore the dead ship, **Jessyn Kaul** — sent on a separate expedition to the planet of Ashtin-Kah — independently uncovers the truth from the ground up.

### A Pear-Like Tree Tested

> "The pear-like thing was a pear. The result bars listed DNA, chloroplasts, mitochondria, cellulose. If she had taken the sample from the corner grocer at Dyan Academy, the results would have looked the same. **She and the trees in this orchard were closer cousins than any of the inhuman things she'd been traveling with. More related to her than half the life on Anjiin.**"
> — *Faith of Beasts*

### The Logical Conclusion

> "It was a truism that human life and its biome hadn't originated on Anjiin… The species from the city had been human height. Had painted its sign in the spectrum visible to humans. **Because they had been human.** This planet had been inhabited by the great and deathless enemy of the Carryx. The opposite side of a war that stretched out past the edge of history. **Humans were the enemy. She was the enemy.** And somehow — how was it possible? — the Carryx didn't know."
> — *Faith of Beasts*

### Strategic Caution

> "If Third Gardener and their Carryx overlords figured out that **the human moiety and the deathless enemy were one and the same**… She didn't know what they'd do. Maybe the best they could hope for was quick death."
> — *Faith of Beasts*

### Meeting the Cousins

Jessyn is captured in a cave by survivors of the Carryx attack on the planet — a teacher (Manta), a knife man (Omco), a group of children, and a damaged "deathless" soldier in black armor named Corvall. Half of Corvall's body is partially regrown wire-thin reconstruction:

> "It was almost a human shape: a head, two arms, two legs. If its right side had matched its left, it could have passed for a slightly taller-than-average man in **black armor**. Instead, its right leg was a dark stick. Its right arm was less than skeletal — the idea of an arm complete with a simple, schematic hand and elbow, places where muscles might have attached, but made from wire."
> — *Faith of Beasts*

> "The black thing's helmet was strange. Featureless and smooth, but not quite opaque."
> — *Faith of Beasts*

The civilians are immediately legible as people; the soldier is human but armored/regrowing — confirming the "dust drill" pattern from Campar's sample:

> "These new captors were people. When they smiled, she knew what it meant. They couldn't speak to each other, but they could negotiate by gesture… Compared to the Night Drinkers or the Rak-hund or the Carryx, **these people were as explicable as a well-written warning.**"
> — *Faith of Beasts*

> "These were also her cousins, and the enemies of her enemies."
> — *Faith of Beasts*

> "**If the deathless enemy of the Carryx were humans, if the army standing against the empire were people like her, then maybe there was someplace to escape to.**"
> — *Faith of Beasts*

### Garral Pär's Linguistic Confirmation — Cultural Cousins

When Garral Pär is captured at the same site, he recognizes a tally-mark notation system on the cave floor:

> "**This is a first-generation math notation system.** You find these in the oldest settlements on Anjiin. It's great for certain kinds of math functions, so they still teach it in engineering programs. But it's old. Really old. I tried a few first-generation languages, and it seems like this guy knows something that's close to but not quite proto-Darsin… You don't care about that."
> — Garral Pär, *Faith of Beasts*

> "**These guys aren't just other human beings. This isn't just common descent. There's a cultural connection. These people are part of the same language system as the first humans on Anjiin. These are literally our cousins.**"
> — Garral Pär, *Faith of Beasts*

The implication: the humans of Anjiin are not just biologically related to the deathless enemy's client humans — **they share a deep cultural and linguistic ancestry**. Humans of Anjiin are a long-lost colony or branch of the same civilization that the Carryx have been fighting for millennia.

### Jessyn's Aborted Confession

Jessyn writes — then erases — a draft note to Dafyd:

> "And she would never go back to the world-palace. She'd never see her brother again. Never tell Dafyd Alkhor the secret she'd discovered: **The deathless enemy were just humans in weird black regrowing armor.**"
> — *Faith of Beasts*

### Plot With the Cousins

Jessyn agrees to smuggle a "deathless" weapon back to the Carryx ships and dispatch an Anjiin archaeologist (Garral Pär) to live with the cousins as a cultural-linguistic bridge. To sell her cover story to the Sinen overseer, she shoots herself with Corvall's gun. The plan rests on the conviction that **direct contact between Anjiin humans and the deathless will eventually transform the war**.

## VI. The Captives in *The Faith of Beasts*: Jessyn Tells Dafyd

After Jessyn returns to the world-palace, she finally delivers her bombshell to Dafyd Alkhor:

> "**This deathless enemy that the Carryx are so wound up about? When I was on assignment, I came across a bunch of them.** I sabotaged Carryx ships and assassinated a Sinen overseer to help them escape too. It's a long story. I'll tell you the whole thing, but the important thing is I sent one of us off with them."
> "You did what?"
> "There was an archaeologist on the team named Garral Pär. He went with them. He's teaching them about us and swapping languages. The Carryx don't know. They think he's dead. But that's not the big thing. You know how everyone back home was always clear that DNA-based life came from someplace besides Anjiin? Well, **I found out where we came from. The deathless enemy? It's us.**"
> — Jessyn to Dafyd, *Faith of Beasts*

## VII. Clae the Spy / Swarm: Theory of the Deathless

Dafyd discusses the revelation with Clae — the latest host of the swarm-spy that has been moving from human to human since the Anjiin invasion. (The swarm intercepted the captives' distress call back in Chapter 24 of *Mercy of Gods*.) The conversation refines the theory:

### Why the Carryx Did Not Recognize Humans

> "It didn't quite make sense when Jessyn said it. I mean, if humanity had been fighting this war back into the ages, wouldn't they have recognized you as their opponent when they took Anjiin? How would they have left any of you alive, much less brought you here?"
> — Clae, *Faith of Beasts*

### Evidence Both For and Against

> "They did fight humans, though. Campar saw them on the ship. Jessyn saw them on the planet. We have related languages. That's pretty strong evidence."
> — Dafyd, *Faith of Beasts*

> "**That humans are on the other side. That's not the same as being the great enemy. Not any more than I think these pentapods are the great enemy.**"
> — Clae, *Faith of Beasts*

### The "Animals as Tools" Reframing

> "The Carryx are going to see humans as animals. When they come across a species in war, they don't necessarily see them as the enemy so much as the enemy's tools. Their possessions. That's what they'd be if the Carryx had them. **That's what we are. I doubt the deathless see Rak-hund as their great enemy, right?**"
> — Clae, *Faith of Beasts*

> "So humans are a client species of this enemy?"
> "And Anjiin was like a pack of wild horses. Not tainted. Available to take and exploit for themselves."
> "Except we weren't."
> — *Faith of Beasts*

This reframes the whole Anjiin invasion: the Carryx assumed Anjiin was a feral, untouched species pool, when in fact **it was a forgotten outpost of the very enemy they have been hunting for millions of years**.

### The Archive Evidence

After Clae searches the Carryx archive:

> "I went back after we talked to Campar. The archive's too big. I can't be sure. But **I found references to what sounds like humans going back eight thousand years. The deathless enemy? The black things that keep fighting even after they're dead? They go back millions.** The earliest references I found, they were associated with something called the Logothetes of Shun and Sum, but even in those it seems like the Carryx had been fighting the deathless before that."
> — Clae, *Faith of Beasts*

So humans, as the Carryx recognize them, are an 8,000-year-old client species. The deathless are a force older than the Carryx empire's records. The two share biochemistry — but the deathless **predate humans**.

### The Lichen Theory — A Master Substrate

Clae proposes a model that reframes what the "deathless enemy" really is:

> "What do you know about lichens?"
> "I know they're primitive soil-generating life-forms that showed up in both trees of life on Anjiin."
> Clae picked up her empty tea mug and put it down again. Her fidgeting reminded him of Carryx feeding hands plucking at each other. "**A lichen has an algae-like function that can pull energy from light and a mineral decomposer like a fungus that can mine nutrients out of the environment. Together, they're what makes soil out of stone.** I did a focused study my last year in medrey."
> "Which you?"
> "Ameer," she said without missing a beat. "But the thing is, **they're different organisms. The algae and the fungal decomposer? Not genetically related. The same algae on a different substrate organism is a different lichen. You can mix and match. The deathless may be the same.**"
> — Clae, *Faith of Beasts*

### Dafyd's Restatement

> "You're thinking there's some other species out there like the Carryx that's been using animals like humans since the beginning of the war, whenever that was. Like a master enemy that's behind it all, and humans are pawns on both sides of the board."
> — Dafyd, *Faith of Beasts*

### Clae's Refinement: It's Not a Species — It's a Set of Instructions

> "**No, I don't think it's a species. I don't think it's alive. I think it's like me. Like I was.**"
> "I don't know what that means."
> "**What if it's just a set of instructions? Like a virus, but instead of how to make more viruses it shows how to make a substrate organism unkillable. How to take a body and make it into a soldier that's stronger, more robust, more effective than it would be without.** Then that idea or set of ideas or techniques gets passed on from species to species to species. For the Carryx, it's like they're seeing new aspects of the same enemy using the forms of different client species. They see it as a singular thing. But it isn't. **It doesn't have a self. It doesn't have any consciousness of its own. No will, no self beyond what it co-opts from the bodies it appropriates. No intention. No awareness.**"
> — Clae, *Faith of Beasts*

This reframes the relationship dramatically. The "deathless" is not a separate species from humans (or from any client of theirs). It is a parasitic / symbiotic technology — **an instruction set that turns a substrate body unkillable**, paralleling the swarm itself. Different client species + the deathless template = different "deathless soldiers." Humans + deathless = the human soldiers in black regrowing armor that Jessyn met. The pentapods + deathless = the five-limbed pilot captives.

The implication for the human moiety:
- The Anjiin humans share descent with the deathless army's human client species.
- The "deathless" isn't an enemy species but a weapon-making protocol.
- The protocol could, in principle, be applied to anyone — including the captive Anjiin humans.

### Campar's "Deathless Cousins"

Later, with weary fatalism, Campar names the relationship plainly:

> "I have it on authority I trust that **the war is going poorly for our deathless cousins**. There's another path here."
> — Campar, *Faith of Beasts*

The phrase **"our deathless cousins"** explicitly establishes the kinship: the deathless enemy of the Carryx are humans like the Anjiin moiety, just from a different and older branch of the species.

> "**Humans are not the deathless enemy.**"
> — Dafyd's opening of the lichen conversation, *Faith of Beasts*

The line is the formal puzzle: humans (Anjiin) are not, on their own, the deathless. But humans + deathless protocol = part of the deathless army. Anjiin humans are ethnically the same as the deathless's client humans, but functionally peaceful and "untainted."

## VIII. The Vaudai Perspective: An Outsider Sees the Stakes

The slug-alien Vaudai, a member of the Carryx's research moiety, frames the strategic value of unmasking the deathless:

> "For us, no. But if we hurry, we will learn things no one has ever told the Carryx. **The key to unmaking the deathless would justify our moiety. Our children and their children and theirs for generations.** We are insignificant, the war is all that matters! Plus which, it is interesting and I am bored!"
> — Vaudai to Campar, *Faith of Beasts*

> "**The enemy is in retreat. Their holdings are collapsing in five of the seven essential strategic zones. The war has stretched over eons, and the enemy has never been in a worse position than it is now.**"
> — Vaudai, *Faith of Beasts*

> "**War is what they are. Enslavement is what they are. A state with no conflict is incomprehensible. Before the deathless enemy arrived, there was war. When the deathless die, there will be war. But the deathless never die. War will never end.** We will be here in its mouth forever."
> — Soft Lothark / Deep Lothark testimony, *Faith of Beasts*

The deathless are losing. They have been at war with the Carryx for eras. And the Anjiin humans — newly captured and freshly inserted into the Carryx empire — are biologically identical to a key client species of the very enemy that is now losing.

## IX. The Coda: Garral with the Deathless Cousins

The Coda of *Faith of Beasts* shows Garral Pär in the Blue Room, an interrogation chamber on the deathless side. He is being debriefed by a human "interrogator" named Carlon.

> "The hallways in his new prison were the white of eggshell with bands of color painted on them with matching words that he was starting to understand: Medical, Cafeteria, Administration."
> — *Faith of Beasts* Coda

> "I would like to show you something," the interrogator said, and the screen of bird dots stuttered and changed. In its place, an image appeared. A hallway of the Carryx world-palace. He even knew the one, just outside the human moiety's new quarters and heading toward the big room they called the cathedral.
> "Yes," Garral said. "That's where they're keeping us…"
> "**This is your homeworld?**"
> "No," Garral said. "That's the Carryx palace. That's where they took us. How did you get this?"
> "You don't know?"
> "I don't."
> "It was transmitted to us using a secure protocol during a recent battle. **You don't know who might have sent it?**"
> — *Faith of Beasts* Coda

This closes the loop. The deathless cousins receive intelligence via "secure protocol" — almost certainly the swarm's transmissions. They have a reciprocal interrogation infrastructure that mirrors the Carryx's interrogation of the five-limbed captives in *Mercy of Gods* Chapter 24. The Carryx don't know who their captives' allies are; the deathless cousins don't know who their packets are coming from. Garral is now positioned as the bridge.

## X. The Web of Connections — A Synthesis

Bringing together every datum: **the relationship between the captured five-limbed enemies and humans is multilayered.**

### 1. Biochemistry

The Carryx Sovran's regulator-librarian observes that the Anjiin humans appear "**related biochemically to the pilot captives you brought**" (*MoG* Ch. 34). The Sovran's Carryx have not connected this to the larger war. They only see "subject species near to enemy tools." But the kinship is real — the metallic-thread anatomy of the five-limbed pilots is the same general design language as the black-armored regrowing soldier Jessyn meets, and that soldier is unmistakably human inside.

### 2. Ecology and Origin

DNA-based life, including humans, did not originate on Anjiin (a known truism in their world). Jessyn's fieldwork on the planet of Ashtin-Kah confirms that Anjiin's pear trees, microbes, and humans share an ancestor with the deathless enemy's planets. The deathless army's footprint goes back **millions of years**; the human moiety on Anjiin only **eight thousand**. Anjiin is a forgotten colony of the deathless's larger civilization — the wild-horse cousins.

### 3. Culture and Language

Garral Pär identifies the cousins' tally-mark notation as a first-generation Anjiin math system, and recognizes their language as adjacent to proto-Darsin. This is not just biology — there is shared cultural inheritance going back to the deepest layer of Anjiin history.

### 4. The Five-Limbed Pilots Are Not Humans

The pentapus pilots themselves are not human — they have hard fivefold-symmetric epidermis, metallic threads in their arms, communicate via radio and chemoreception, and (they claim) were "designed life," "made from semi-stable plasma" beings called the Aunjeli. They are **the enemy's manufactured pilot caste**, not the enemy's full citizens. Humans (Anjiin and otherwise) appear to be a different, organic client/citizen population.

### 5. The Black Armor / Regrowing Soldiers Bridge the Two

The "black thing" Jessyn meets is human inside black armor that regrows and resists damage. The "black-shelled" pentapus pilot Campar samples is the same regrowth/unkillability technology applied to a five-limbed body. The dust scraped from it self-organizes into a tiny machine. The same set of instructions has been applied to two different substrate species.

### 6. The Lichen Model

Per Clae: the "deathless" is not a species at all but **an instruction set** — algae-and-fungus style — that pairs with whatever substrate organism is convenient and renders it deathless. Pentapods + deathless = silver-skinned pilots. Humans + deathless = black-armored regrowing soldiers. This is exactly parallel to what the swarm itself is: not alive, not conscious before encountering its host, an instruction set that takes over a body and turns it into a tool.

### 7. The Swarm Is the Same Family of Technology

The swarm recognized the captives' radio distress call "**like hearing words in a language it had forgotten it could speak**" (*MoG* Ch. 24). It encrypted information back. Both are likely products of the same deathless protocol — the swarm being a stealth/intelligence variant rather than a combat variant. The black "regrowing" technology and the swarm's flesh-rebuilding technology are likely the same family of weaponized instruction sets.

### 8. The Strategic Picture

- The Carryx have been fighting the deathless for millions of years and are currently **winning** (the deathless are in retreat in five of seven strategic zones).
- The Carryx took Anjiin without realizing humans are biochemically and culturally part of the very enemy they are losing.
- The Carryx interrogated and killed every captured pentapus pilot without realizing the humans now living in their world-palace are kin to those captives' allied populations.
- The Anjiin humans now know the truth: they are **the deathless enemy** to the Carryx's eyes, just disarmed and naive.
- Garral Pär is on the deathless side learning their language and customs.
- The swarm/Clae has sent intelligence packets to the deathless cousins.
- Dafyd Alkhor proposes: **what if there were two Sovrans?** — i.e., a structural attack on Carryx hive-coherence rather than a decapitation strike.

### 9. The Tragic Irony

Ekur-Tkalal, the Carryx librarian who personally interrogated the five-limbed captives until they died and then was promoted to keeper-librarian of the human moiety **specifically because of the biochemical relationship**, still does not know it has the deathless enemy's cousins under its care. The Carryx noticed the kinship but read it only as "this subject species is near the enemy's tool," and assigned the librarian who knew the captives' biology to manage the new humans. The Carryx believe they are domesticating a useful animal. They are housing a population from the very civilization they have been at war with since before recorded history — a civilization the Anjiin humans never knew they came from.

## XI. Open Questions / Threads for the Next Book

- **Who or what really is the "deathless"?** Clae's lichen theory suggests an instruction set rather than a species. Is the Aunjeli claim (plasma beings in star coronas) real, a captive's deception, or a half-truth?
- **What is the relationship between the swarm-spy and the deathless?** Both are "designed life" with similar capabilities — flesh remaking, radio coordination, encryption that the other recognizes "without knowing." Are they two products of the same Aunjeli (or other) civilization?
- **Will Garral successfully unite the Anjiin humans with the deathless cousins?** The Coda shows him being interrogated; the cousins do not know yet that an entire forgotten branch of their species exists in Carryx custody.
- **What happens when the Carryx figure out the connection?** Both Jessyn and Clae fear immediate genocide. The Carryx famously cull useless or compromised moieties.
- **Can the human moiety itself become "deathless"?** If the deathless protocol is just an instruction set, in theory the Anjiin humans could be re-armed with it. The swarm already exists among them.
- **Why didn't the deathless protect Anjiin?** If Anjiin is a long-lost colony, why was it forgotten or unprotected when the Carryx came? The book hints at the deathless being so stretched thin that some outposts ("staying silent and small") rely on obscurity for security.
