# The Deathless — Analysis

*The Captive's War, Book 2: The Faith of Beasts — James S.A. Corey*

---

## Overview

"The deathless" (also "the deathless enemy," "the dark enemy," "the great and deathless enemy," "the army of the dead") is the Carryx's galaxy-spanning adversary. They have been at war for at least millions of years — long enough that the word for "war" in Carryx is the same as the words for "service" and "life." In *The Mercy of Gods* they were a rumor; in *The Faith of Beasts* they move from rumor to on-page presence. TFOB is the book where both the human moiety and the reader first see the deathless directly — and it is the book where the central revelation lands: **the deathless are, in part, humans.**

The revelation is delivered in four waves across three POV characters:

1. **Jessyn** finds an Earth-lineage orchard on World and identifies the local civilization's dead as human.
2. **Jessyn** then meets Corvall — a living human soldier in failing black living-armor — and confirms it.
3. **Campar** (with Vaudai and Ghati) boards a captured enemy command ship at Ashtin-Kah, finds a mixed crew of human corpses and five-limbed silvery alien corpses, and encounters a reactivated black-armored enemy soldier.
4. **Clae** — the swarm — puts the pieces together at book's end and delivers the correct abstraction: the deathless are NOT a species but an **instruction set**, a substrate-agnostic "design for how to make a body unkillable," passed from host species to host species across millions of years.

By book's end, Dafyd believes the deathless are "our cousins," but Clae's lichen theory has more explanatory power than that, and is likely the operative model for Book 3.

---

## Terminology

The book uses several overlapping labels. They matter:

- **"The deathless" / "the deathless enemy"** — default term, used by characters and narrator alike. The name refers to behavior, not metaphysics: they keep fighting after they appear to die. Corvall is called "the half-soldier"; the ship-board pursuer is "the three-legged thing" and "the black thing"; Vaudai calls the class of them "the army of the dead."
- **"The dark enemy"** — used by the subjugator-librarian's POV (*"The dark enemy itself could be on any of them or none..."*). An in-universe Carryx term.
- **"Our deathless cousins"** — Campar's coinage at the end of TFOB, after Jessyn's report.
- **"The pentapods"** — Dafyd and Clae's term, late in the book, for the five-limbed silvery form Campar sampled.

Vaudai, late in the book, offers an important distinction: *"Humans are not the deathless enemy."* Vaudai's species, which studies the patterns of violence, insists on keeping the categories separate even after Jessyn's discovery. Clae's lichen theory reconciles the two views.

## Physical Manifestations Seen in TFOB

TFOB shows the deathless in **four** distinct physical forms.

### 1. Corvall — humanoid, black living-armor, on World

Jessyn's first direct encounter (Ch 11–17). Corvall is the surviving soldier of a deathless army routed on World. His ship was forced down; his unit died; he alone remains, sheltering ten orphaned city-children with two tutors (Manta, Omco).

> "It was almost a human shape: a head, two arms, two legs. If its right side had matched its left, it could have passed for a slightly taller-than-average man in black armor. Instead, its right leg was a dark stick. Its right arm was less than skeletal — the idea of an arm complete with a simple, schematic hand and elbow, places where muscles might have attached, but made from wire. It walked forward as if its asymmetry were perfectly natural to it..."
> — TFOB Ch 11

> "The black thing's helmet was strange. Featureless and smooth, but not quite opaque. She felt like, in the right light, she'd have been able to see through the material to whatever was underneath. A face, a mechanism."
> — TFOB Ch 11

> "He's human. Like us. He's in... living armor? Something like that. He lost a leg and an arm in the crash, and the suit is rebuilding him, but there's not enough resources to do the job, so he's down to... you know. That."
> — TFOB Ch 16 (Garral to Jessyn)

So Corvall is a **human pilot** inside a living-armor substrate that is actively regrowing missing limbs in the background, but is metabolically starving: the suit has more to do than it has fuel for, so the regrown limbs are skeletal wire-frames rather than full flesh. Voice through the helmet is "harsh, buzzed at the edges like a bad speaker." Gender presumably male (Omco calls him *"Sauda-ut Corvall"*; he has apparent deferential gendered relationship with Omco).

He carries a handheld directed-energy pistol — *"a sharp, electric snap"* that blows a thumb-sized hole in cave stone. He can stand motionless as a statue for indefinite periods to conserve the suit's power.

Corvall's death-plan is to **accept his death as decoy**: fire a rescue beacon, let the Carryx hunt him while the kids escape with the rescue ships. He hands Jessyn a small ceramic cylinder of autonomous sabotage swarm to disable the Carryx ships from inside during the rescue. He also gives Garral a chance to "come with us" and be the first contact between the deathless civilization and humanity-from-Anjiin. Garral takes it. Corvall dies as planned.

### 2. Silvery five-tentacled — dead civilian form

Campar finds these on the bridge of the disabled enemy command ship at Ashtin-Kah (Ch 28), alongside dead humans in jumpsuits:

> "In among the corpses of men and women, alien flesh lay dead as well. Thin silver limbs that glistened in the light like salmon skin and met in a small central node. Something like octopuses, but with only five tentacles."
> — TFOB Ch 28

> "The five-armed thing was just as prone to postmortem swelling as humanity seemed to be."
> — TFOB Ch 28

They wear *"complicated rings near the base of each tentacle"* — Campar speculates these might be keratin that grew there, or tool-rings. He pulls one off with no adhesion; it has *"little keys that something might press"* on the inside — technology, not biology.

The command ship was manned by a **mixed crew** of humans and pentapods, neither wearing the "deathless" black armor. These appear to be civilian or technical personnel, not deathless soldiers.

### 3. Pentapod in black living-armor — the pursuer

Campar's second discovery on the same ship is a **different** body in the same corner:

> "One of the five-legged aliens, but this one without the silvery sheen. It was missing two of its limbs, though the stump of one still remained, and it was utterly blackened. Campar thought at first that it had been burned... But there was no heat contraction, no bubbling of flesh. The bodies that had fallen around it didn't have similar damage."
> — TFOB Ch 28

Its skin cannot be cut. The scalpel bends; a fresh blade bends; Campar can only scrape a few grains of dust from its exposed stump.

This black pentapod is **sessile** (Vaudai's word) because the ship is dead. When the ship's lights come back on, the pentapod **reactivates** and hunts Campar, Ghati, and Vaudai through the corridors. It is missing two of its five tentacles; it still tries to throttle Campar with the stump of a limb that isn't there:

> "The thing swam toward him. Its tentacles flowed with an eerie grace, twirling as it moved through the space. Only the two missing limbs gave it a sense of wrongness and unbalance."
> — TFOB Ch 28

It burns to white-hot ash in the Carryx field-weapon overspill but keeps moving long enough to nearly take Campar with it — which is the behavior that gives the class its name.

Vaudai identifies it on sight:

> "The deathless enemy is on the ship with us. I am not certain how it was damaged, but as it was sessile until the ship regained power, I believe it was very badly compromised. This is to our advantage."
> — TFOB Ch 28

> "The army of the dead often displays this behavior. Intriguing to see firsthand."
> — TFOB Ch 28

Vaudai's casual familiarity is a clue: the Carryx analytic community has studied this behavior pattern across many hosts.

### 4. The sample in the jar — the swarm form

Campar's scraped grains of dust from the dead pentapod later organize themselves in the sample jar:

> "the specimens of dust had come together, organizing into a mechanism like a tiny drill. The tapping they'd felt was the sample throwing itself against the wall of the jar. Trying to escape."
> — TFOB Ch 28

This is the single most important shot of the deathless on-page. The "living armor" is not a suit worn by a pilot — it is a substance that can, even in fragments, self-organize into tool-complexes with intent. The dust *wants out*. This is why Clae, later, proposes that the deathless are more like a "virus" / "set of instructions" than a species.

## Key Properties and Capabilities

- **Deathless-ness.** The name is literal behavior: they continue to act after the host body dies. Vaudai treats this as a well-documented property. Corvall's living armor can stand in for a mostly-gone human body; the pentapod on the command ship continues to hunt with two limbs missing and the ship dead; the sample in the jar continues to self-organize after being scraped off a corpse.

- **Sessile when unpowered.** Vaudai believes the armored pentapod *"was sessile until the ship regained power."* The deathless form requires an active energy substrate — ship power, fleet proximity, something — to stay animated. Corvall preserves this on the planetary surface by holding still for extreme durations.

- **Living armor / regrowing.** *"The suit is rebuilding him"* (Garral, Ch 16). The limb Corvall has regrown is wire-skeletal because his fuel reserves are exhausted. The implication is that at full power the armor regrows full-flesh replacements.

- **Command-ship self-destruct.** Standard doctrine. *"It is an enemy command ship. They do not permit them to be examined."* (Vaudai, Ch 18). This is the doctrine that **fails** at Ashtin-Kah, producing the disabled ship that Campar boards — *"an important insight into the enemy design philosophy. Also, it means they will be unable to detonate the ship remotely, though they will surely try. It will require direct attack."* (Vaudai, Ch 28).

- **FTL alternative.** The deathless do not use Carryx-style asymmetric space. Their ships emerge *"from flaws in spacetime that they used in place of the purity of asymmetric space"* (Ch 26). This is a different translation technique, not a weaker one: they can arrive in-system through routes the Carryx can't see.

- **Field weapons.** Decoherence zones, projection fields, resonance exclusion, void tendril probability arcs. The Carryx fight the same physics. Vaudai: *"Field weaponry propagates as would light or gravity. If we can see them, the battle has in essence already begun."* (Ch 24).

- **Handheld directed-energy weapons.** Corvall's pistol. Kinetic-plus-energy; blows a clean thumb-sized hole through limestone.

- **Sabotage swarm.** A ceramic cylinder containing self-directed "autonomous sabotage swarm" that disables Carryx ship engines and power systems for a short window when released inside the defense perimeter. This is what Corvall gives Jessyn for the rescue operation. The swarm inside Else/Jellit/Clae and the cylinder's swarm appear to be the same class of technology.

- **Secure communications protocol.** The deathless have at least one "secure protocol" for receiving data bursts, including Clae's single-packet transmission via Rickar's death-burst at Jurupe. But they have no protocol for *acknowledging* receipt, and no bidirectional channel: *"there's no protocol for them to acknowledge it"* (Clae, Ch 33).

- **Client / allied species.** The command-ship bridge had a mixed non-armored crew of humans and pentapods. On World, Corvall's "army" had at least the two species represented in his downed ship (described as *"a group of other soldiers"* — not further specified). Garral, on being shown the corridor image in the final scene, is being interrogated in a facility with a human population, clean architecture, signage in a language using first-generation Anjiin-cognate mathematical notation, and recognizable "Cafeteria / Administration / Medical" zones — suggesting the deathless civilization has standard biological infrastructure for its substrate populations.

## Clae's Lichen Theory (the central revelation)

Delivered in Ch 36 over tea in Dafyd's garden.

**The archive records:**

- Humans: references go back ~8,000 years.
- The deathless ("black things that keep fighting even after they're dead"): references go back **millions of years**.
- Earliest deathless references associate the phenomenon with "the Logothetes of Shun and Sum," but the Carryx-vs-deathless war predates even that.

**The incompatible timescales force the lichen analogy:**

> "A lichen has an algae-like function that can pull energy from light and a mineral decomposer like a fungus that can mine nutrients out of the environment. Together, they're what makes soil out of stone... they're different organisms. The algae and the fungal decomposer? Not genetically related. The same algae on a different substrate organism is a different lichen. You can mix and match. The deathless may be the same."
> — Clae, Ch 36

> "I don't think it's a species. I don't think it's alive. I think it's like me. Like I was... What if it's just a set of instructions? Like a virus, but instead of how to make more viruses it shows how to make a substrate organism unkillable. How to take a body and make it into a soldier that's stronger, more robust, more effective than it would be without. Then that idea or set of ideas or techniques gets passed on from species to species to species. For the Carryx, it's like they're seeing new aspects of the same enemy using the forms of different client species. They see it as a singular thing. But it isn't. It doesn't have a self. It doesn't have any consciousness of its own. No will, no self beyond what it co-opts from the bodies it appropriates. No intention. No awareness."
> — Clae, Ch 36

**Key consequences of the lichen model:**

1. There is no "deathless homeworld" to destroy and no "deathless species" to exterminate. The Carryx's entire strategic frame is wrong.
2. Humans may be only the **current substrate** in an older rotation. The pentapods appear to be another current substrate. Prior substrates may have gone extinct, or moved on, or still exist somewhere.
3. The "Logothetes of Shun and Sum" is probably a still-earlier substrate — a species that carried the deathless instruction-set long enough to leave its name in Carryx records.
4. Clae herself is a member of the same **class** of thing: a self-organizing instruction set capable of inhabiting a biological substrate. (*"It's like me. Like I was."*) The swarm and the deathless are cousins, not identical. Clae's excitement in the scene — *"There might be other things out there like me"* — is the felt experience of a loneliness the book hasn't named.
5. Clae is explicit that the deathless instruction-set is **not conscious**. It is a design pattern, not a mind. Whatever intention and strategy the deathless displays in war is coming from its substrate hosts.

Dafyd's response to the theory is brutally practical: *"Whether you're the only thing in the universe like you or not doesn't matter to any of that."* He is not wrong. But it matters enormously for any Book-3 plan to "ally with the deathless." Jessyn, Garral, and Corvall believed they were allying with an older human civilization. Clae's theory says they were allying with a **design pattern wearing an older human civilization this week**.

## The Vaudai Parallax

In Ch 35, right before parting, Vaudai tells Campar flatly: *"Humans are not the deathless enemy."* Vaudai is the book's on-page specialist in the patterns of violence; it has studied the deathless across many battles; and it is unimpressed by Jessyn's "deathless-are-human" discovery.

Vaudai is **correct** in a way the book quietly honors. Clae's theory is the one that reconciles Vaudai's view with Jessyn's: the deathless armor/instruction-set is not human; the current substrate on worlds like World is human; so "humans are the deathless enemy" is true at the level of the body and false at the level of the phenomenon. Vaudai, whose species has watched this rotation for millions of Carryx years, doesn't make the beginner's mistake.

## What the Carryx Know, and Don't

**Know:**

- The deathless are behaviorally immortal and will keep fighting after apparent death.
- Their command ships self-destruct on capture as standard doctrine.
- They use non-asymmetric FTL ("flaws in spacetime").
- They have client species across many worlds.
- Their technology is in constant evolutionary arms-race with Carryx countermeasures. Every battle is *"an experiment to discover what will undo the last encounter's defenses"* (Vaudai, Ch 13).

**Do not know (as of TFOB):**

- That humans of Anjiin and the deathless "humans" on World are conspecific. The Carryx took Anjiin thinking it was a *"pack of wild horses"* (Clae, Ch 36) — an untainted world of fresh biomass to domesticate. Had they known Anjiin was a deathless client population, they would almost certainly have sterilized it as they did Jurupe.
- That the deathless is a substrate-agnostic instruction set rather than a species.
- That the swarm (now Clae) has placed a death-burst transmitter inside a departing human (Rickar) and successfully delivered one packet of internal Carryx intelligence to the deathless, including a surveillance image of the world-palace corridor outside the human moiety.

The Carryx **did** notice Rickar's evacuation pod failing "strangely" at Jurupe — the Sovran herself is informed that *"a pattern of anomaly clouds the Anjiin moiety."* Surur-Tlassen is directed to *"clarify if this outweighs their utility."* This is the only Carryx-side signal that the war has just crossed into the world-palace.

## The Human Moiety's Accumulated Intelligence on the Deathless (as of end of TFOB)

### Jessyn (direct contact, confirmed)

- Humanoid-substrate deathless soldiers exist.
- They die, but slowly, and their armor continues to animate what's left of them.
- Civilian populations (tutors, children) exist behind their lines; these are people, not soldiers.
- Tech: handheld directed-energy weapons, autonomous sabotage swarms, rescue ships, crash beacons.
- They did not previously know Anjiin existed.
- They are losing the current phase of the war (Corvall's unit was routed; rescue ships were sent reluctantly).

### Campar (direct contact, dead and reactivated)

- Command ships maintain a mixed crew of at least two substrates (human + pentapod).
- Armored deathless soldiers are sessile without ship power and reanimate with it.
- Their flesh cannot be cut by conventional blades.
- A small sample of their flesh can self-organize into tools.
- Ship interiors are "almost human-scale" — squared, rectilinear corridors; the ship feels built for bipeds.
- They fear Carryx field-weapon overspill (the pentapod broke off pursuit to reach a hardened shelter).
- Command-ship self-destruct is a failure mode, not a guarantee.

### Clae (archive work, synthesis)

- Humans in the Carryx archive: ~8,000 years.
- Deathless in the archive: millions of years.
- Deathless pre-date the Logothetes of Shun and Sum, an otherwise-unidentified prior civilization.
- Lichen model: the deathless is a substrate-agnostic instruction set, not a species, not a mind.
- Clae herself is of the same class of thing but a different instance.

### Dafyd (synthesis, as he understands it at book's end)

- The deathless include humans.
- They are currently losing five of seven strategic zones (Vaudai, Ch 35).
- An assassination of the Sovran + a deathless invasion force would *"do to these bastards what they did to Anjiin."* (Dafyd, Ch 33) — this is his working plan for Book 3.

### Garral (embedded, inside the facility)

- A deathless city / installation exists at the other end of his ride.
- The civilization uses a first-generation Anjiin-style mathematical notation — direct linguistic ancestry.
- Standard clinical / administrative infrastructure: "Medical / Cafeteria / Administration / Restricted."
- Human-occupied sofas and chairs.
- Interrogators are ordinary-seeming old men who speak a descendant of a language Garral can partially parse.
- The deathless received some intelligence packet from inside the Carryx world-palace *"during a recent battle,"* but do not know who sent it. That packet is almost certainly the broadcast from Rickar's body.

## Strategic State of the War

> "The enemy is in retreat. Their holdings are collapsing in five of the seven essential strategic zones. The war has stretched over eons, and the enemy has never been in a worse position than it is now."
> — Vaudai, Ch 35

> "The Carryx will be victorious, and then they will go on with the war at every edge of the empire. They have no choice. What is, is."
> — Vaudai, Ch 35

Vaudai's prognosis is the book's final word on the war proper. If Vaudai is right, the deathless are **losing**. The Jurupe sterilization, the capture-by-disablement of the Ashtin-Kah command ship, the accelerated raid pace (*"we're going to be at the planet much faster than when they came to Anjiin"*) all point the same way: the Carryx are pressing an advantage.

Against this, the book puts three weights on the deathless side of the scale:

- One intel packet has now reached them from inside the Carryx world-palace.
- They have rescued ten orphaned children and Garral — the first humans-of-Anjiin they have seen.
- The Ashtin-Kah command ship, though captured, revealed a physical (not remote) self-destruct failure point in their design philosophy, which the Carryx will also learn from — but which the deathless can now correct.

The deathless have been losing for thousands of Carryx-logged years. Book 2 ends with the first structural change in that trend in living memory — and with Vaudai still certain it doesn't matter.

## Open Questions (for Book 3)

- If the deathless are an instruction set, who or what **originally encoded it**? The Logothetes of Shun and Sum? Something older? Something not alive?
- Does the instruction set know it's an instruction set? Does it **propagate** itself, or is it propagated **by** its substrates? Clae believes it has no awareness, but Clae did not know she had awareness either until she had it.
- Why didn't the original-homeworld fall of the Carryx to the Epikainot cause the deathless to win? The Carryx lost a Sovran, lost an archive, lost a world — and rebooted from the private creche on this world. If the deathless have been losing that long, was the original Carryx collapse itself a deathless strategy that failed?
- How does the deathless technology relate to the swarm technology Clae is? Both are substrate-agnostic, both organize from fragments, both hide inside host biology. Are they branches of the same design?
- Is Anjiin itself a previous deathless substrate world that the instruction-set has gone **dormant** on? If so, waking humans up is the last thing the Carryx want — and may be exactly what Dafyd's conspiracy is poised to do without knowing it.
- What does the deathless interrogator do with Garral's account of Anjiin? Garral knows the location of the Carryx world-palace's moon signature. Corvall "got excited" when Garral described it. The deathless may now have a vector to the world-palace.
