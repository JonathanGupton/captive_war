# The Swarm's Physical Transformation of Jellit's Body

## Overview

In *The Faith of Beasts*, the nanomachine "swarm" (the deathless-enemy spy that originally inhabited Ameer Kindred, then Else Yannin, and then Jellit Kaul) demonstrates increasingly dramatic physical control over the body it inhabits. The novel establishes that the swarm is not merely a passenger in Jellit's flesh — it is a **superstructure** of self-propelled microagents that permeates the entire body, preserves the original cells and DNA, and can rearrange them in service of whatever function it needs. Over the course of the book, this capability progresses from invisible internal "tuning" (turning Jellit into a radio receiver and transmitter) to visible under-skin motion, to full remodeling of bones and soft tissues, and finally to a complete reconstruction into a new person ("Clae Audin") who no longer looks like Jellit at all.

This file collects all the textual evidence and traces the transformation from beginning to end.

---

## 1. What the Swarm Actually Is

The swarm is repeatedly described as a machine-based network — a phage or connective tissue running through a living human body.

- **"a network of machines that it is"** (line 3677)
- **"the swarm doesn't have a self. It isn't alive. It's a bunch of machines. It's a technique."** (line 4455)
- **"the original nanoparticles of the swarm as a kind of connective tissue and phage"** (line 5596)
- **"the microagents that are its peculiar body"** (line 1119)
- **"The swarm is a weapon of war. A spy sent to collect everything and transmit what it learns back to worlds it has never known. It is designed to survive for as long as it can, but it is not designed to survive."** (line 410)

When the swarm "takes" a body, it preserves the host's cells, DNA, scars, and structures, but interpenetrates them at a level that lets it reconfigure them on demand:

> "The cells and structures are, in most senses, Jellit. The scar on its rib from an accident Jellit had when he was a boy. The one on its leg from an act of violence that happened after they were all prisoners of the Carryx. **The DNA in its nuclei has the code, for the most part, that Jellit was born with. The superstructure of the swarm directs and permeates Jellit, allows the body to become what it needs to be: a listening device, a transmitter, a chemical sampling machine.**" (line 444)

That last sentence is the clearest statement in the book of the *mechanism*: the swarm is a distributed directing structure that permeates every tissue of Jellit's body and can repurpose it.

## 2. How the Swarm Took Jellit (Retrospective References)

The actual transfer happened in *The Mercy of Gods* and is referred to in *Faith of Beasts* only in summary. Jellit is dead; the swarm wears his body.

- **"Jellit is the only male it has taken, the only one who had already dedicated himself to violence, and the only one who carried the weight of that trauma on his soul. The swarm can't say if any of these differences explain why this body is more dedicated to asserting itself. To fighting the possession. To denying the death that has already come for him."** (line 394)
- **"Now it has the room Jellit had been assigned, but the assignment came after Jellit's death…"** (line 426)
- **"it had killed Else and taken Jellit"** (line 2426)
- **"There was no way to convince Jellit to cooperate… He was adamant. I knew you wouldn't tell the librarian if he didn't, and we had to tell. We didn't have an option. So we left Else's body behind and came to his. Only the body. Nothing else. Nothing that matters."** (line 1153)

Jellit's consciousness persists inside the swarm as one of its "voices" (along with Ameer and Else), fighting possession — which implies the nanomachine network captured not only his flesh but also a neural pattern / memory trace that continues to argue with the swarm's other component personalities throughout the book.

A key mechanical note: **the body reinforces the mind.** When Else lost her flesh, her distinctness in the swarm's mind began to decay:

> "with her flesh abandoned, Else Yannin has begun to lose some clarity and definition as well, bleeding at the edges like a watercolor left where the damp could reach it. **The body reinforces the mind in ways the swarm is only starting to catalog.** If it had known before, it might not have shifted to Jellit." (line 404)

So the flesh is not just a disguise — it's a substrate whose structure preserves the captured mind patterns.

## 3. The Catalog of Physical Capabilities

At line 3701, as the swarm contemplates its most ambitious transformation yet, the book lays out its complete toolkit in a single paragraph:

> "**It knows how to change its flesh in a thousand different ways.** It can become a **listening device aware of fields of force so finely tuned that it could pick up a whisper in a storm.** It can **remake the bones and muscles of its borrowed flesh in ways that give it ten times the strength of the original human.** It can **extrude hairs so fine that it can record the individual ridges of a fingertip from a handshake.** **The eyes can be tuned to see in spectra no human being has ever seen.**"

This is the author's central statement of what the swarm can do to Jellit. Every other transformation in the book is a specific application of this general capability.

## 4. Visible Surface Effects — "Ripples" and "Black Motes"

The first direct in-text observation of the swarm acting on Jellit's flesh comes when it reveals itself to Dafyd. Here the swarm deliberately *shows* the nanomachines moving under the skin:

> "it rolls up its sleeve, bares the thin skin at its wrist, and lets the microagents that are its peculiar body cohere—**black motes swirl under its skin like flakes in a snowstorm.** It discovers that it is holding its breath." (line 1119)

> "His gaze shifts from the **black motes of its skin** to its eyes and back again." (line 1125)

This is the swarm *voluntarily* clustering its agents near the surface so Dafyd can see proof of what it is. Under normal operation the agents remain dispersed and invisible.

When the swarm is working as a data transmitter for the Carryx archive, the nanomachine activity becomes involuntarily visible at the surface:

> "Something odd was happening just under his skin like the **shadows of waves on the bottom of a pool.** After a moment, he nodded. 'All right. I've got it.'" (line 2408)

> "The rippling under Jellit's skin was gone. If he hadn't known better, he'd have thought the spy was just a man searching the world for some familiar landscape." (line 2440)

> "it could move through an abstract space that was the Carryx archive. But to do it meant **subtle alterations to Jellit's body—a darkening of the eyes, a change to the texture of the man's skin.** Dafyd didn't know what was being adjusted or rearranged to let the swarm become its own node in the vast alien network, but it would have been hard to explain away." (line 3012)

> "**His skin was taut and shining in a way that reminded Dafyd of the swelling after an injury.** Jellit took a long, shuddering breath the way he did sometimes now. 'There's another bank in here. Every time I think I'm at bedrock, it opens up and there's more.'" (line 3020)

So the *observable signatures* of the swarm in Jellit's body are:
- **Rippling/wave motion under the skin** (data-intensive work)
- **Darkening of the eyes**
- **Changes in skin texture**
- **Taut, shiny, injury-swollen skin**
- **Black motes coalescing visibly near the surface** (when the swarm deliberately reveals itself)

The swarm also **sheds massive heat and energy** doing this: "I'm burning through a lot of energy doing this. Can you… bring me a meal?" (line 3024).

## 5. Chemical Manipulation: Pheromones

In addition to mechanical reshaping, the swarm manufactures and emits complex molecules from Jellit's body.

> "If it had known Dafyd was coming, it would have built a supply of pheromones. Would have done what it could to calm the man's mind until it could reason with him, influence him, bring him back to the place they had been when it had lived within Else Yannin." (line 2345)

> "The **pheromone cloud** it had filled the room with smells like a party banner ruined by rain. **It breathes in, reclaiming the complex molecules**, as it cries." (line 4479)

> "As she had when she met Brun, Clae **flooded the air around her with pheromones.** *Love me, believe me, want to be my friend.* While the room filled with the scent of her manipulations…" (line 5604)

> "**The air smelled vaguely of spice.**" (line 5646)

Brun also notices, pre-recognition: "**There was a strange smell around her. Not unpleasant, but peppery or something.**" (line 5568). Dafyd notes it later too: "**the air filled with a peppery incense odor**" (line 5636).

So the swarm can synthesize scent-chemicals in Jellit's/Clae's body (a built-in "chemical sampling machine," per line 444), release them as a deliberate persuasion tool, and even *reclaim* them by breathing them back in.

## 6. The Mid-Book Attempt: Rebuilding Jellit Into Else

After Dafyd rejects a kiss, the swarm impulsively decides to reshape Jellit's body into Else's — on the theory that Dafyd's revulsion was about Jellit's male form. This is the first *major* structural rebuild shown on the page.

> "The swarm walks to the mirror, strips off the tunic and the trousers, and looks again at the body it now inhabits. **It tilts its head—Jellit's head—and the image in the mirror tilts with it.**" (line 3699)

Then, directly after the catalog of capabilities:

> "**But this change, there is no guide for. This, it has to improvise. The skin and soft tissues are simple. The harder connective ones will be more difficult. It begins reshaping the heavy bones of its pelvis.**
>
> **The changes hurt.**" (lines 3703–3705)

Hierarchy of difficulty:
1. **Skin and soft tissues: simple.**
2. **Connective tissues: harder.**
3. **Heavy skeletal bones (like the pelvis): hardest, requires improvisation.**
4. **The process is painful.**

When the rebuild is done, Else's features are on Jellit's frame:

> "The familiar face looks back from the glass. **Eyes set a little wider than the species median. The bridge of the nose graceful and long.**" (line 4485)

It is explicitly described as **Else-styled features on Jellit's underlying body**:

- "Her hair tumbled across her shoulders the way it always had, even though **the color of the hair and the shape of the collarbones were subtly wrong.**" (line 4453)
- "Else's little crease between its brows, her beautiful mouth curving down." (line 4441)
- "Else lifted her hands in exasperation, just the way she used to do. It was eerie seeing the gesture again." (line 4453)

But Dafyd rejects even this form because he has realized the swarm killed Else. The swarm is left in a half-Else/half-Jellit state.

## 7. The Late-Book Rebuild: Becoming Clae Audin

The swarm's breakthrough — the moment it stops trying to reconstitute its dead hosts and accepts itself as itself — triggers a total, identity-driven reconstruction of the body. This is the most extensive transformation passage in the book.

### Trigger

At line 4501–4517, the swarm looks into the mirror and finally recognizes that Else, Jellit, and Ameer were never truly separate personalities it was preserving — they were patterns it was puppeting. A profound shift occurs:

> "Something profound shifts.
>
> The swarm stepped back from the mirror, its heart racing… **The room was the way it had always been. And also different. The swarm was different.**" (lines 4503–4505)

> "And it was there, all of it, but now the swarm was aware of a hand inside the puppets, **and the hand was it.**" (line 4511)

> "When it went back to the mirror, **the woman looking back out was it.** The face was the swarm's face. **It looked like Else had, more or less, but changed in a way that didn't have anything to do with shape or coloration.**" (line 4517)

### The full-body rebuild

The swarm now begins molding Jellit's body into a new form of its own design:

> "The swarm touched its own cheeks, felt the texture of its skin against its fingertips. Felt the bones of its skull. The weight of its tongue resting against its palate. **Skin tone was easy, so it shifted the color of its face, lightening it, then darkening.** The prospect of putting Jellit's features back in place seemed impossible now." (line 4519)

> "It shifted its eyes to a light hazel. To blue. To green. To gray. To a brown so dark it could have been black." (line 4521)

> "With something on the edge of vertigo, the swarm began **shifting the bones and the soft tissues, molding itself like living putty, searching for what it really looked like. For who it really was.**" (lines 4523–4525)

### The recovery period

The rebuild takes days and costs a physical toll that the swarm has to manage. From Clae's POV later:

> "For long, painful, hungry days, she had kept herself sequestered in Jellit's room while she **remade herself and then remade herself again**, looking for something in the mirror." (line 5586)

> "She **burned excess mass** because she couldn't leave the cell even to get food. And because no one brought food. Not even Dafyd." (line 5586)

> "And while she waited for her flesh to remake itself, for the **pain of reknitting bones** to subside and **the tide of blood displaced by her alterations to reabsorb**…" (line 5590)

So the structural rebuild has real physical consequences on Jellit's original biology:
- **Mass is consumed / burned** to provide raw material for restructuring.
- **Bones break and reknit, painfully.**
- **Blood volume redistributes** and has to resettle.
- It is "painful, hungry days."

### The psychological point of no return

Once the rebuild is far enough along, reverting is no longer possible:

> "She'd turned back more than once, intimidated by the risk and the enormity of the task. But **when she tried to remake Jellit, she'd felt a sense of revulsion she still didn't understand. Where once she had been a secret sharer of his life and experience, now becoming him felt like putting on his corpse as a costume. Even when she wanted to go back, she couldn't.**" (line 5588)

The deliberate design choices:
- "**softer cheekbones**" (line 5596)
- "**preference—lightly held—for existing as a woman's body and mind**" (line 5596)

## 8. How Clae Appears to Others

Once Clae emerges, she is no longer recognizable as Jellit to anyone who meets her — including Dafyd, who *knows* and must be told.

From Brun's POV, meeting her in the lab:

> "The new woman came in from the archway to the east like she was heading in from the legacy labs. **Gold-brown skin, light brown eyes, and black hair. Her build was thin, almost adolescent.** When she smiled, he felt bad for staring, but he didn't know who she was, didn't remember seeing her before. That was weird." (line 5562)

> "There was a **strange smell around her. Not unpleasant, but peppery or something. Like she'd been sitting in a temple space, and the incense hadn't quite loosed its hold on her.**" (line 5568)

From Dafyd's POV, meeting her in the garden:

> "When the woman arrived, he mistook her at first for Andermus. **She had the same slight frame and a confidence to the way she walked** around the Rak-hund curled in the shadow of the archway. **The hair was wrong, though, and the skin closer in tone to the Kaul family light bronze than Llian's nearly translucent white.** As she got closer **the air filled with a peppery incense odor**, and Dafyd found himself **struggling to place her. She was familiar. He should have known her, but he couldn't put a name to the face.**" (line 5636)

Even with Dafyd, who knows exactly what she is, his brain cannot latch on. The physical transformation is complete and convincing.

The pheromone-driven social manipulation then closes the loop: once she talks to someone, she plants memories to make herself fit into their history.

> "That had actually been a woman named Daan, but the incident had been years ago, and memory — as the swarm knew better than anyone — was fragile. There was maybe a flash of uncertainty as the other woman tried to remember the moment the way the swarm had prompted her to. And then the discomfort resolved as she did, and **Clae Audin's roots traveled back into time and memory. She became someone who had always been on the periphery, but always present.**" (line 5612)

## 9. The Outer Limits: Other Physical Things the Swarm Can Do

Three more extreme capabilities are demonstrated or alluded to in the text, all of which imply the swarm's total control over Jellit's flesh:

### a) Transfer to another host entirely

The swarm could abandon Jellit's body and take Dafyd's instead:

> "**I could have taken you too. I could have left Jellit behind and been in your body. I can do that.**" (line 4467)

This is consistent with the original transfer from Else → Jellit: the swarm can decant itself out of one human and into another, presumably by reshaping itself into a transmissible form and bridging across.

### b) Break off a self-sufficient piece ("the cyst")

The swarm can separate off a fragment of itself and implant it in another human's body, where it will act autonomously.

> "Tell him how you **broke off a piece of yourself and hid it in someone who was shipping out**? Tell him that as soon as it's in range of your buddies on the other side, **it's going to pop like a hand grenade**?" (line 486)

> "'It had to be someone. There's no reason it was you. **I just needed to put the cyst into one of the people who was leaving the world-palace.**'" (line 5055)

When Rickar reaches safe range, the cyst activates:

> "In the evacuation pod, **the cyst reshaped itself, reconfiguring the body of its host.** On fifty overlapping frequencies, everything that the swarm had learned between the time it had landed successfully on Anjiin to the day that it had placed the cyst in Rickar's body shouted out to its allies and creators. **The energy used for the broadcast unmade the complex molecules that had been Rickar Daumatin's flesh like he was a human bonfire.**" (line 5087)

So the swarm, operating inside a host body, can turn that body into a transmitting antenna powerful enough to convert its own flesh to energy — a one-shot broadcast burn. This is an extreme but consistent end-state of the same mechanism that lets it reshape Jellit at the other extreme.

### c) Full dissolution into a "cutting storm"

When threatened, the swarm contemplates letting Jellit's flesh disperse entirely and becoming a weaponized cloud:

> "It prepares itself for a final violence. **If it releases itself now, lets the human flesh that has carried it so far fall away, it can become a cutting storm. It can kill all the enemies before it and many, many more.**" (line 1199)

This is the only hint that the swarm's component microagents can operate as a free-floating, lethal aerosol without any human host at all.

## 11. Summary of the Mechanism

The book never gives a full technical explanation, but the stitched-together picture is consistent:

1. The swarm is a **distributed network of self-propelled nanoscale machines** ("microagents," "nanoparticles") that live within a host body as a kind of connective tissue and phage.
2. Inside Jellit, the original human cells, DNA, and structures are **preserved and reused**, but the swarm's superstructure **directs them** to express whatever tissue is needed.
3. The swarm can operate at **three different spatial scales**: (a) fine-grained tuning within an otherwise normal-looking body (eye darkening, skin texture, pheromones, internal "ripples"), (b) gross structural remodeling of bones, muscles, and features (the Else→Clae transition), and (c) whole-body dissolution into an independent cloud of agents.
4. The rearrangement has **costs**: it **hurts**, it **burns mass** (the body has to consume its own reserves for raw material), and it **displaces blood and other fluids** that then have to resettle.
5. **Skin/soft tissue is easy, connective tissue harder, heavy skeletal bone hardest.** A full remodel takes days of hungry, painful seclusion.
6. The captured mind — Jellit's personality — persists as a pattern within the swarm's consciousness, because **the flesh reinforces the mind.** Losing the flesh causes those patterns to fade (as happened with Else after the Else→Jellit transfer).
7. When the swarm abandoned the Else-shaped rebuild and finally constructed "Clae Audin" from scratch, the transformation was complete enough that **not even Dafyd — who knew exactly who he was looking at — could recognize Jellit in the face.** Pheromone manipulation and memory-seeding closed the last loop, letting "Clae" insert herself into the moiety's social fabric as someone who had simply always been there.

The swarm's transformation of Jellit's body is, in short, the running demonstration of what the deathless enemy's technology can do to a human — a slow unfolding from "there's something odd just under his skin" in Chapter 2 to a wholesale new person who emits incense, seeds memories, and synthesizes persuasion in a breath.
