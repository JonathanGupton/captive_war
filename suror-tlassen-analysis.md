# Surur of Cohort Tlassen — Character Analysis

*The Faith of Beasts* gives the reader its most sustained view *inside* the
Carryx empire through four POV sequences centered on a single character:
**Surur of the cohort Tlassen, regulator-librarian to the Sovran**. He is
the highest of the librarians in the empire, the single conduit between the
Sovran's will and everything the empire does. His arc — daily ritual,
anomaly-sighting, and final dissolution into a "remnant" — is the novella's
window into how the Carryx organism actually functions.

---

## 1. Identity and Role

### Name and rank

- **Surur of the cohort Tlassen** (also written "Surur-Tlassen"). "Cohort"
  appears to be his clan or hive-lineage designation, paralleled elsewhere
  in the text by "Moqid of the cohort Tlannan" and "Dalkin of the cohort
  Astur."
- **Regulator-librarian to the Sovran**. Introduced as "the highest of the
  librarians in the empire" (2925) and later as "regulator-librarian to the
  Sovran and first among her servants" (6272).
- **Gendered male** at the time of the novella — "through him—him—to the
  Sovran herself" (2384) — having re-earned his gender by ascending to the
  apex of the librarian caste. The text is explicit that Carryx gender is
  contingent on rank and role.

### Position in the hierarchy

The empire is a pyramid described in explicit layers (2384):

> "Ekur-Tkalal lived now at the bottom of a great chain of being. Keeper-
> librarian of the human moiety, it directed all things that touched upon
> the animals under its control, as it was itself directed by Moqid of the
> cohort Tlannan, who was directed by Dalkin of the cohort Astur, who was
> directed by the supervisor-librarian of animal assets. And so on, up to
> Surur-Tlassen, regulator-librarian, and through him—him—to the Sovran
> herself with whom Ekur was honored to share a planet."

So the chain runs (from the bottom): **keeper-librarian (e.g. Ekur-Tkalal)
→ Moqid → Dalkin → supervisor-librarian of animal assets → [intervening
layers] → Surur-Tlassen → Sovran.**

Below Surur-Tlassen sits a **council of eight high librarians** who report
to him and receive his disseminated version of the Sovran's will (1925,
6318).

### What the regulator-librarian *does*

Surur-Tlassen's function is compression and broadcast. The empire's entire
information flow passes through a single biological mind. His daily cycle
(1925):

1. **Sleep and dream.** "A third of his life now was spent dreaming, and in
   some ways the most important third." Dream-state consolidates the vast
   data stream into something presentable.
2. **Feed.** "Black amask flesh and sweet lorrith" at his private trough.
3. **Meditate on the product of his own mind.**
4. **Attend the Sovran.** Delivers the state of the empire; receives her
   directives.
5. **Meet with the eight high librarians.** Passes down her will;
   aggregates new reports from all corners.
6. **Return to sleep.**

The text frames this not as administration but as a somatic process:
"thousands upon thousands of worlds, hundreds of thousands of ships,
billions upon billions of Carryx that were the flesh of the one great and
developing body that was the empire" (1925). Surur is a neuron — the
bridge between the empire's nervous system and its single controlling
mind.

## 2. Biography — the Upward Spiral

Surur's personal history maps the Carryx concept of a life well-lived.
From his opening dream (1921):

- **Brood mother** of the sixty-fifth exploratory body. Ate stone and bone,
  produced blank eggs for the public creche. None of his own genetic
  material entered those eggs — "no mere brood mother would be permitted
  so great a representation in the generations she brought to life."
- **Challenger.** "The subterranean desire to fill some of the blank eggs
  with offspring of his own" drove him to "challenge the keeper-librarian
  of his brood, snapping its right feeding arm and claiming its place."
  Succession by violence is the Carryx norm.
- **Librarian.** At some point he gave up gender to enter the librarian
  caste.
- **Regulator-librarian.** Climbed to the apex, which returned him his
  gender and the right to contribute to offspring.
- **Future: keeper of the private creche.** "One day to hold the limb of a
  future Sovran and guide her to her place at the peak of their society"
  (1927). His eventual fate is to become a neuter remnant, breeding the
  Sovran's daughters.

Surur sees his own life as a fractal of the empire: "Not for the first
time, he thought of his own ambition as a metaphor for the ambitions of
the Carryx as a whole" (1927). Individual ambition and imperial expansion
share the same word in his language as "war" and "service."

## 3. Physical and Sensory Portrait

Scattered details across the four chapters build a reasonably complete
Carryx body:

- **Abdominal legs** in ceaseless motion (2923); can fold under him when
  he rests.
- **Fighting arms** — used to snap the feeding arm of his predecessor;
  can be spread wide in surrender (2911).
- **Feeding arms** — distinct from fighting arms.
- **Beak.**
- **Thorax** that grows "slick and warm" in the Sovran's presence (2949).
- **Joints** that loosen when the Sovran's scent is present (2923) — a
  satiety/deep-sleep response.
- **Pheromones** that "in any other Carryx would have commanded respect
  and obeisance" (2905); he releases them reflexively.
- **Hormones associated with loss** and "nerves and apt channels" that
  respond to rank-signals.

The body is as political as the mind. Every reaction — loyalty, fear,
submission, command — is a hormonal or postural event, not a decision.

## 4. Four Chapters, Four Functions

### Chapter 13 (line 1921) — Introduction as the empire's circulatory system

The first Surur chapter has no plot — by design. It is an unbroken
description of the daily cycle, the dreaming, and his self-conception as
someone whose "ambition as a metaphor for the ambitions of the Carryx as a
whole." The function is to show the reader that Carryx rule is not
delegated to bureaucrats; it is biologically embodied in one individual,
whose sleep patterns literally shape imperial policy. The chapter also
plants the seed of his future transformation — the brood-mother origin and
the eventual return to egg-creation.

### Chapter 19 (line 2896) — Anxiety, Urur-Atlak, and the first daily report

This is the first chapter where Surur does things with consequences.

- He is summoned by the **eerie, scentless thing that had once been
  Urur-Atlak**, formerly "the most important male in the empire." It is
  now a remnant of the private creche. Surur's reflexive pheromone-display
  provokes no response — "the sensation in Surur-Tlassen's core would have
  been fear if feeling fear were possible" (2905). The text shows the
  remnants are immune to the signals that govern every living Carryx.
- A line of private thought lands: *"And one day I may know them too"*
  (2917). He shudders. His eventual fate is already visible to him.
- Walking to the Sovran, he **crushes a service animal** that failed to
  move fast enough (2919). "The sense of a small virtue comforted him."
  The moment communicates Carryx ethics cleanly: "small corrections
  repeated over time would eventually lead the other animals to excellence."
- He delivers the day's report. Three items we hear named:
  1. Baan-wheat and deep-copper harvest results.
  2. The **enemy ship in Ashtin-Kah that failed to destroy itself** — the
     same ship Campar, Ghati, and Vaudai will end up exploring.
  3. The loss of manufacturing planets in Horol-Sa and Dakku.
- The Sovran's reply binds Surur's arc to the novella's plot: *"Bring the
  enemy's attention elsewhere to give the animals the fullest opportunity
  to examine it, the cost of this is not a concern"* (2953). This is the
  strategic directive that generates the Jurupe genocide — billions of
  deathless-enemy client animals will be exterminated as a *diversion*
  so that researchers can examine one disabled enemy ship.

Surur's ecstasy at the Sovran's command ("shuddered in ecstasy and in
awe," 2957) is the novella's clearest demonstration that Carryx atrocity
is spiritually fulfilling for those who execute it.

### Chapter 29 (line 5091) — The anomaly he doesn't want to pass upward

The second report chapter. Surur has another routine cycle: sixteen
life-bearing planets taken from the deathless enemy; plague culling in the
Chinic moiety; his new daughter ready to meet her.

But there is a single item he "didn't like" (5103) bringing to the
Sovran's attention unprocessed:

> "In the battle at Jurupe system, an evacuation pod failed strangely. It
> lost coherence without evidence of an enemy attack…. The explosion
> stuttered. There appeared to be a structure within it that is not
> congruent with Saren-hund death. The cargo was an animal of use from
> Anjiin system. The human moiety."

This is Rickar Daumatin's death. The reader already knows (5087) that a
deathless-enemy **cyst** implanted in Rickar's body has just broadcast
everything the enemy swarm learned about humanity before unmaking his
flesh. Surur recognizes only the anomaly, not its cause.

The Sovran's response is the single line that routes back down the entire
librarian chain and reshapes Dafyd's life:

> *"Challenging that which we do not understand yields unexpected insight.
> A pattern of anomaly clouds the Anjiin moiety. Clarify if this outweighs
> their utility."* (5119)

Surur's reluctant honesty — presenting unprocessed anomaly, "a fine meal
with a bit of gristle at its heart" (5115) — is what marks the Anjiin
humans for deeper scrutiny. He is the pivot point through which the
novella's deathless-enemy plot reaches the Sovran and bounces back as a
policy directive on Dafyd's moiety.

### Chapter 36 (line 6272) — The dissolution of Surur-Tlassen

The final Surur chapter is the quietest and most significant: he stops
being Surur-Tlassen.

The scene is the meeting of the Sovran with her new daughter — his sixth
such meeting. The remnants emerge from the private creche in order,
youngest to oldest, the oldest barely able to climb its bench (6278). The
Sovran enters; her pheromones overwhelm even the senior librarians (one
"lost control of his limbs for a moment, flailing like a newborn fresh
from the shell," 6280).

The daughter emerges. The ritual combat begins. The Sovran lands the
first major blow — shattering the daughter's right abdominal foreleg —
but the daughter pivots and strikes the Sovran's fighting-limb junction.
The daughter kills the Sovran.

Then comes Surur's transformation. The remnant that had been Urur-Atlak
**turns all its eyes on Surur** (6310). No previous daughter-meeting had
produced this focus. What happens next is described as liberation, not
grief:

> "Surur-Tlassen felt a wave of calm wash over him, and then a profound
> and beautiful falling away. His gender, his responsibility, the burden
> of his office. A banked and unacknowledged lust that had underlain
> everything he—it—had done since achieving its goal deflated in a kind
> of holy fatigue. It was as if a lifetime of torture it hadn't known it
> was suffering had suddenly stopped and left it at peace." (6312)

The shift in pronoun — *he → it* — is made in the middle of a sentence,
and from that point the text never uses "he" for Surur again. The new
Sovran's song calls every other Carryx to resonance. Surur's body does
not answer. *"The relief was unexpected and joyful."*

He walks across the chamber and takes his place with the remnants. His
successor is already implied — "the council of eight that had attended
Surur would be fighting each other for the honor of taking its place"
(6318). Urur-Atlak leans over and welcomes it home (6324). Surur-Tlassen,
the character, no longer exists. What remains is a neuter monk bound for
the private creche, there to craft the next Sovran's daughters.

## 5. What Surur Reveals About the Carryx

Surur is, functionally, the novella's exposition engine for how the Carryx
empire works. Specific things we learn *only* through him:

- **Rule is embodied, not delegated.** A single living mind compresses and
  broadcasts imperial will every day. The "empire" is a nervous system
  with one neuron at the crown.
- **Life progression is a spiral through genders and castes.** Brood
  mother → librarian (degendered) → regulator-librarian (re-gendered) →
  remnant (neuter monk). Ambition is a biological feature, not a character
  trait.
- **Succession is ritual combat at every level.** Surur snapped his
  predecessor's feeding arm; the new Sovran beats her mother to death in a
  choreographed duel; the council of eight below Surur will fight each
  other for his vacated seat.
- **The private creche is extra-political.** "The Sovran's will reached
  to the stars… but not to the private creche" (2915). Only the remnants
  know its workings. This is the novella's first suggestion that the
  Carryx have internal structures that even the Sovran does not command —
  a crucial hint for anyone reading the empire as monolithic.
- **Pheromone submission is universal but not total.** Urur-Atlak, as a
  remnant, is immune to rank-pheromones. The remnants are the one class
  of Carryx who can stand outside the ordinary somatic politics of the
  empire.
- **Atrocity is literally pleasurable.** Surur's "shuddered in ecstasy
  and in awe" after ordering a diversionary genocide is not irony — it
  is the Carryx affective response to well-executed imperial policy.
- **The empire's scale dwarfs individual death.** When Surur reports the
  Jurupe pod anomaly — a single Anjiini dying — the Sovran's gaze drifts
  away. "To speak of a single death felt like sacrilege" (5113). And yet
  this single death is the thread that unravels into the Anjiin
  directive.

## 6. The Private Creche — What It Is and Why It Matters

The phrase "private creche" recurs throughout Surur's arc and is the single
most important piece of worldbuilding his chapters deliver. It is not a
nursery in any ordinary sense. It is the Carryx empire's **succession
engine and survival backup**.

### What it is physically

- **A sealed chamber within every world-palace.** "The private creche was
  set deep in the flesh of the palace, surrounded by it, but not connected
  except for the meeting chamber" (2903). The only visible interface is
  the passageway that opens onto the ritual meeting room where Sovrans and
  daughters fight.
- **Entered only by remnants.** "No full Carryx had passed since the day
  the private creche had been constructed" (2913). Unseen controllers open
  and close the door. Full-caste Carryx — even Surur, the highest
  librarian in the empire — cannot enter.
- **Inhabited by neuter monks.** The residents are all former regulator-
  librarians ("all of them former regulator-librarians like himself,"
  2903), now described as "remnants" or "the monks of the private creche"
  (6282). Urur-Atlak — once "the most important male in the empire" — is
  one of them.

### What happens inside it

Three functions run in parallel:

1. **Sovran manufacture.** "The Sovran is born in the private creche,
   built and designed and selected there for perfection of function.
   There is always one at the ready should an accident befall the empire"
   (6421). The creche is a bioengineering facility that designs the next
   Sovran.
2. **Daughter production.** Periodically the creche produces a "new
   daughter" — a candidate Sovran — who emerges into the meeting chamber
   and fights her mother. "The survivor of that meeting carries on"
   (6421). The creche supplies the challenger every generation.
3. **Blank-egg stockpiling.** "There are always blank eggs at the ready
   to prepare another if the need arises" (6423). Redundancy is built in
   below the daughter level.

The remnants are the workforce. Former regulator-librarians — who spent
their lives compressing the empire into daily reports — spend their
afterlives crafting the next generation of Sovrans and the eggs that will
become future Carryx. Surur's own destination (6318): "Soon, its new
cohort would lead it to the private creche where it would spend the rest
of its life crafting new daughters for the new Sovrans."

### Why it is extra-political

The creche sits outside the empire's ordinary chain of command. "The
mysteries of the private creche were not subject to the empire. The
Sovran's will reached to the stars, to the complex surface of death and
violence that was the empire's edge, to the deepest regions of the Carryx
mind, but **not to the private creche**" (2915). The current Sovran
cannot command it, inspect it, or know what it will produce next. The
remnants' pheromone-immunity to living Carryx — Urur-Atlak ignoring
Surur's reflexive submission display at 2905 — is the somatic sign of
this autonomy.

### Why it matters: the novella's central strategic revelation

The private creche is not merely Surur's retirement home. Its existence
is the reason the rebellion Dafyd is planning **cannot succeed as
conceived**. In Chapter 37, Dafyd interrogates a Deep Lothark informant
through symbol-glyphs and learns the history of the Epikainot — an
enemy that once destroyed the Carryx homeworld with a field-weapon
resonance effect (6413). The expected outcome — empire collapse — did
not happen:

> "When the Epikainot destroyed the first world and the voice of the
> Sovran went silent, the creche on this world opened, and the Sovran
> emerged. The librarians here became the librarians that had been. The
> empire shifted its attention a few degrees, and all carried on as it
> had." (6427)

And the scale is crushing:

> "There is a private creche on all the world-palaces. **Hundreds, each
> in its order.**" (6425)

This reframes everything Dafyd has been working toward. Killing the
Sovran on the current world-palace would only trigger the local creche
to open and produce a replacement; hundreds of other world-palaces would
simultaneously detect the void and their own creches could supply
replacements. Jessyn's immediate reaction at 6449 — could they "locate
all the planets that had these private creches…? taking out the whole
Sovran class at once… I mean, it seems like that might actually work"
— is met with Campar's scorn: "Coordinated strikes to decapitate every
possible Sovran on at least several hundred heavily fortified potential
throne worlds?" (6451). The rebellion is not a decapitation problem. It
is a distributed-redundancy problem.

### Why Surur's arc makes it legible

Surur's four chapters are how the reader learns about the private
creche *before* the Epikainot history lands in Chapter 37. Without his
perspective the Chapter 37 revelation would be abstract exposition. With
it, the reader already knows:

- The creche is sealed, ancient, and untouchable by ordinary Carryx (Ch. 19).
- The remnants who live inside are immune to the ordinary somatic
  politics of the empire (Ch. 19).
- Daughters emerge from it on a schedule known to the keepers, not the
  Sovran (Ch. 19, 29).
- The ritual meeting always ends in the death of one party (Ch. 36).
- A regulator-librarian's career ends by walking into the creche and
  never returning as himself (Ch. 36).

When Dafyd learns that every world-palace has one, the reader already
understands what one *does*. The private creche is the mechanism by
which the Carryx empire is **individually mortal and collectively
immortal** — the reason the narrative framing "None are valued because
none are valuable" (6429) is not rhetoric but infrastructure.

## 7. Surur's Role in the Novel's Plot

Though Surur never meets a human, his chapters are structurally load-
bearing:

1. **He routes the Ashtin-Kah ship discovery to the Sovran.** Her order
   — "bring the enemy's attention elsewhere" — is what generates the
   Jurupe operation. Every overspill event the Rickar/Campar chapters
   depict is downstream of Surur's daily report.
2. **He routes Rickar's death to the Sovran.** Her response ("clarify if
   this outweighs their utility") is the order that lands on Ekur-Tkalal
   and ultimately on Dafyd. The Carryx's sudden increased scrutiny of
   the Anjiin moiety — and the command that Dafyd identify three hundred
   individuals for fieldwork (5194) — flow from Surur's reluctant
   honesty.
3. **His transformation signals a regime change.** The Sovran's death
   and the rise of the new Sovran ("the consonance," 6356) is what Ekur-
   Tkalal registers in Dafyd's meeting. Dafyd doesn't understand what
   has happened. The reader, through Surur, does. The empire has just
   transitioned at the top, and the policy continuity Dafyd depends on
   is now in question.

## 8. Thematic Resonance

Surur is the novella's answer to the question *what is it like to be
Carryx?* The answer has four parts, each mapped onto one of his chapters:

- **Ch. 13 — Identity as function.** Being Carryx means being a role in
  a larger body. Selfhood is caste and hormone.
- **Ch. 19 — Devotion as pleasure.** Loyalty is ecstatic, chemically
  induced, and fully sincere.
- **Ch. 29 — Honesty as risk.** Even the highest servant feels the
  cost of passing unfiltered information upward. The empire's neatness
  is a construction that its own conduits must labor to maintain.
- **Ch. 36 — The self as temporary.** The highest reward in the Carryx
  life cycle is the dissolution of individuality. "Holy fatigue" — a
  "lifetime of torture it hadn't known it was suffering" ending — is
  how Surur experiences the end of his own selfhood.

Crucially, nothing about Surur is villainous in the human register.
His crimes (the crushed service animal, the diversionary genocide) are
executed with the satisfaction of a job well done. The novella uses him
to make the argument that the Carryx empire does not need evil
individuals to produce monstrous outcomes — the outcomes fall out of
the ordinary operation of the biological hierarchy. Surur is the proof.

---

## 9. Summary

Surur of the cohort Tlassen is the closest the novella comes to a
Carryx protagonist: the regulator-librarian whose daily cycle of
dream, feed, report, and disseminate is the physical mechanism of
imperial rule. Across four chapters he is introduced as a biological
conduit, shown executing imperial strategy at its most ecstatic,
caught in the small act of honesty that routes the deathless-enemy
contagion into his Sovran's attention, and finally dissolved into a
remnant bound for the private creche — his successor already lining
up for the ritual combat that will claim his seat. He is the novella's
explanatory key for how the Carryx actually work, and his four
appearances frame every downstream event involving the Anjiin humans.
