# Uuya Tomos — Character Analysis

*The Captive's War, Book 2: The Faith of Beasts — James S.A. Corey*

---

## Snapshot

A small, thin, iron-gray-haired woman in her later years, walking with a rolling, hip-pained gait. A folklorist and social historian who — before Anjiin fell — reorganized the medrey system "more or less single-handedly" and wrote the *Wolf of the World* mythic novels and *The People of the Panopt* short stories that were ubiquitous in Dafyd Alkhor's youth. Introduced as one of Dafyd's first administrative conscripts in *The Faith of Beasts* and at first contemptuous of him, she is ultimately recruited as the conspiracy's "minister of propaganda" and becomes the architect of the embedded resistance curriculum for the Carryx-born children — the editor of the *Myths of Origin* cycle that serves as the book's six part-epigraphs. By book's end she is one of the last three living conspirators (with Dafyd and Jessyn) and the only one whose view of their project is mature: a game to be left "to the great-grandchildren of our great-grandchildren."

---

## Background Before the Fall

Uuya's pre-invasion credentials are given in pieces across the book:

- **Folklorist and social historian.** The only woman in the new governance team whose prior career is academic rather than scientific or administrative. (TFOB Ch 5)
- **Reorganizer of the medrey system.** The book's single line — "reorganized the medrey system more or less single-handedly" — is a very large claim in the Anjiin context; the medrey system is Anjiin's institution of communal research-and-education campuses, and Rickar's, Jessyn's, and Garral Pär's whole social world is structured through them. She redesigned the framework that produced most of the human moiety. (TFOB Ch 5)
- **Author of the *Wolf of the World* mythic novels and *The People of the Panopt* short stories.** Dafyd, as a child, read *People of the Panopt* "a hundred" times. *Wolf of the World* he read but did not understand. (TFOB Ch 9)
- **Author of an academic monograph on wisdom myths.** "I did a book on them, but it was during my academic phase. Dry. I wouldn't blame anyone for skipping it." She was also working on a follow-up "set of those around menopause. There's a lot of good stories around menarche and menopause" before Anjiin fell. (TFOB Ch 31)
- **"Retired the first time."** Mentions retiring at least once before — her adult life has had multiple phases, each deep enough to have its own vocabulary. (TFOB Ch 23)
- **Knew Dafyd's aunt Dorinda ("Dory")** from their early twenties. Not friends — "I came out of the egg an academic, and she was always a politician. We could never be friends, but I admired her for her ruthlessness." The comparison to Dorinda becomes load-bearing in her arc. (TFOB Ch 9)

---

## Physical Description

Tonner, at first meeting, reads her as monastic:

> "The older woman with the thin face and the wreath of iron-gray hair standing out from her skull like the halo of some Serintist saint was Uuya Tomos."
> — TFOB Ch 5

Dafyd, who gets her as an interlocutor, repeatedly registers scale against presence:

> "She was a small woman, but she didn't feel that way. Her presence seemed to fill the space around them, and her scowl was sharp enough to cut."
> — TFOB Ch 9

Later appearances consistently note: a rolling, bad-hip gait; one white ceramic replacement tooth brighter than the rest of her natural, bone-colored teeth; hair often sweat-damp and "wild as smoke"; a habit of scratching her chin "like she was feeling for a beard." She looks like a working old woman, not an intellectual in retreat.

## Arc by Appearance

### TFOB Ch 5 — Conscripted
Dafyd drafts her into the new governance layer alongside Bastien Korham and Llian Andermus. In the room she says almost nothing; Tonner registers her presence and professional biography but she doesn't yet speak up.

### TFOB Ch 6 — The Refusal
She speaks for the first time when Dafyd reveals the lamb-sack breeding plan. Her voice is "simultaneously amused and serrated as a knife," and she is the only person in the room who sees through the framing to the moral substance:

> "So. Minister of propaganda?"
>
> "Children. He's talking about children."
>
> "How exactly do you expect that to work? Are we going to require everyone under forty to be perpetually pregnant? Am I supposed to pick who pairs up with who? Is this one — " she gestured to Andermus, " — supposed to hold them down if they disagree?"
>
> "Birthing new slaves for the Carryx is an atrocity any way you slice it. Because it's not just kids, is it? Give people children that they love, and you put handles on their backs. What are we going to do when the bugs can threaten to kill our babies if we refuse?"
> — TFOB Ch 6

She walks out mid-meeting with her bad-hip gait. Tonner's reaction — "Probably it was the most eloquent way she could have put it. He found himself liking her." — tells us the novel considers her refusal ethically correct, even as the novel will then drag her into the atrocity anyway.

### TFOB Ch 9 — The Recruitment
The hinge scene. Andermus has had to threaten her to get her to agree to meet with Dafyd at all. Dafyd opens by naming her former colleague Dorinda; she returns the favor by telling him she can't see Dorinda in him. He tries the flattery of the childhood reader; she brushes it off and keeps walking. He only stops her by shifting to the children themselves:

> "These new children? I can't help them. They need you."
> — Dafyd, TFOB Ch 9

She turns. She delivers the test: "And whose fault is that?" And then Dafyd makes the pitch that earns her — not the lamb-sack pitch from Ch 6 but a new one, about curriculum as sabotage:

> "If I wanted to raise these children to revolt against the empire, I could do that. If I wanted to raise them to be good servants, I could do that too. But I need them to be both, and that's too hard for me."
>
> "Not just a curriculum. It's not just education. I need stories. I need songs about how we might suffer now, but that we'll get justice in the end. About how servants can get power over time — how they turn invisible. Stories about saying the right things, doing the right things, playing the right part up until the moment comes. And then throwing off the mask and fighting."
> — Dafyd, TFOB Ch 9

Uuya is the one who immediately supplies the formal template:

> "The Gallantists had a tradition like that. They built a whole body of folktales around Shavas Rey, the sleeping god, and how she would wake at the end of time to remake the world."
> — Uuya, TFOB Ch 9

Dafyd then closes the trap: "I have just inducted you into a secret conspiracy to destroy the Carryx. When you came out here, you didn't know about it. Now you do. So when you go back through that archway, you have two options. Either you go straight — and I mean straight — to Ekur-Tkalal, tell it everything I just told you, and have me killed, or else you can help me." She recognizes the Dorinda-style ruthlessness she had just said she didn't see in him. She taps his sternum:

> "There she is. Now I see Dory."
> — Uuya, TFOB Ch 9

Her conversion is not to Dafyd's cause but to his methods. She has been looking for Dorinda's lineage in him, and the moment he shows it, she joins.

### TFOB Ch 15 — Uuya as Producer
She brings Ver Cannedan, the choreographer who punched Dafyd in Ch 6, into a commissary meeting with her own proposal: reassign Ver to study Carryx body language and nonverbal communication and build a human movement vocabulary around it. The plan is a double win — it rehabilitates a disruptive high-status human by giving him a role that feels important, and it attacks a real Carryx blind spot. She also uses the meeting to probe Dafyd about Jellit's fall from grace ("Is that what happened with Jellit Kaul?"), needling him about how little time he's made for the actual conspiracy she's committing her life to:

> "Except for every now and again when you recruit someone into a conspiracy. How's that going for you, by the way? Not a lot of spare time for it? Not many hands on the rope pulling along with you?"
> — Uuya, TFOB Ch 15

### TFOB Ch 16 — Uuya Teaching
She supervises one of Ver's movement lessons, translating Ver's bodily insights into geopolitics on the fly:

> "The point Ver's making is they don't have their feeding arms out when they fight. They tuck them away. So if you're in feeding arm position, you're de-escalating."
>
> "Also why they killed an eighth of us on Anjiin instead of a third or a tenth or something. Eight limbs, one broken."
> — Uuya, TFOB Ch 16

This is a decisive moment for what she actually is in the conspiracy: not a story-teller hidden in a back room, but the interpreter who connects every domain — folklore, physiology, ritual, political theory — into a single working model of how to survive the Carryx. "Minds are something that bodies do. They're not just connected, they're different ways of looking at the same damn thing." She is the person who knows how to read the body of an empire.

Her post-lesson banter with Dafyd also establishes the comic-drag register of their working relationship:

> "I'm always right. You'll get used to it."
>
> "A terrible burden, always being right."
>
> "I'm just rubbing it in a little. Give a woman her little pleasures. There's no wine on this planet, so smugness is the only vice I've got left."
> — TFOB Ch 16

### TFOB Ch 22 — Dismissive of Tonner
A brief passing scene: she runs into Tonner in Dafyd's garden as she's leaving and he's arriving. He sneers. She returns polite-false sympathy — "The burdens we bear" — and walks past. Tonner dies shortly after. Her instinct, that Tonner is self-pitying past the point of usefulness, will prove correct and will shape how she manages his memorial.

### TFOB Ch 23 — The Memorial Strategy
Dafyd comes to her private quarters for help managing Tonner's memorial. Her room is the only human space in the book described as having been aesthetically made back into something — a mural by the artist Ferdan Bellenn, bartered for a month of dishes duty; lacework on the bed; incense. This is the only glimpse we get of the life she is protecting for the people who don't have her political role:

> "There's a lot of that in the rank and file, you know. People who dedicated their lives to something that doesn't matter anymore. Honestly, I'm shocked we haven't had more suicides."
> — Uuya, TFOB Ch 23

She immediately names what the memorial is actually for — not grief, but narrative control:

> "And what is it you're looking to achieve from the ritual?"
>
> "Acknowledging the passing of an old friend isn't enough?"
>
> "Not for you. Not in your position."
>
> "Give our brother Tonner's passing a story to edge out any other stories that might find their way in?"
> — TFOB Ch 23

She also tells him flatly that he cannot give the eulogy, because the community will just remember the old love triangle with Else; she'll write it, and Brun will deliver the first speech. The memorial is being authored, not held.

### TFOB Ch 24 — The Memorial Service
At the service she sits at Dafyd's side, pinches his thigh to cue him, and hands him the speech she has pre-written. After, she briefs him with the bluntest political weather report in the book:

> "They hate you. Of course they do. They hate the Carryx, and you're the Carryx's whip hand. Every shitty thing they have to do, every policy they have to follow, it comes through you. They hate Andermus too. That woman loves structure and law more than she loves people. They don't much like Korham either, but at least he's charming and he makes things that people use."
>
> "What about you?"
>
> "They love me."
>
> "And yet here you are, talking with me."
>
> "I have to. They know I tried to walk away, and you wouldn't let me. Oh, don't look like that. It's true. And the story will change. Maybe not in your lifetime, and maybe not into something you prefer, but heroes and villains change places in history all the time. Look at your friend Tonner. He was a very clever man with a very narrow view of the universe, and now he was an open-hearted, kind, supportive teacher who gave us all food and shelter. And babies pretty soon now."
> — TFOB Ch 24

This is her most important single speech in the book. It contains three things: (1) the affective map of the moiety — who is loved, who is hated, and why; (2) the public story she has constructed about her own recruitment, which Dafyd will have to accept in perpetuity as the frame for their partnership; and (3) her theory of historical memory — that the role of myth is to change who the hero is, and that roles rotate.

### TFOB Ch 31 — The Wisdom Myths Lesson
Rickar has just died, Dafyd has lost his composure badly enough to physically confront Ekur-Tkalal, and is breaking down. Uuya, working construction on the new school, talks him back. This is the most direct philosophical statement of her worldview in the novel:

> "You ever heard of wisdom myths? I did a book on them, but it was during my academic phase."
>
> "There are a lot of mythic and religious forms. The one you're commissioning from me is a justice story. God will punish the wicked and reward the just, even if it takes a long time. And then there's origin stories that place communities in the world. Explicatory stories that take a common experience and put a metaphoric context around it. I was working on a set of those around menopause. There's a lot of good stories around menarche and menopause."
>
> "But wisdom stories aren't there to give comfort. They tell the truth. The only consolation you get from a wisdom story is that someone else saw the same world you're seeing. They're the stories where sometimes the good get screwed and it's not made right. The evil prosper, and God lets them. The plague comes and decimates and leaves, and there's no rhyme or reason for who it kills and who it leaves standing. And they're very consistent about what you can do about it."
>
> "I'm guessing you're about to say 'nothing.'"
>
> "Everything. You do whatever you can, even though it doesn't seem to be working. You keep shoveling the shit against the tide and you hope the tide goes out before your strength fails. It feels meaningless. It feels overwhelming. It feels like you're a tiny, insignificant thing in a universe that barely notices you exist, because that's just accurate."
> — TFOB Ch 31

She then pulls him physically off the floor and puts him to work for half a shift on the children's school building:

> "Well, there's a beauty in truth. There's also a beauty in work. Come take half a shift building something concrete for the children. When you're done, you can go back to beating all the human impurities out of yourself so you can be the sword of almighty justice."
> — TFOB Ch 31

The scene is structured as a private therapy session but is also the book's explicit statement of its own genre. Uuya identifies that the curriculum she is being commissioned to build for the children is a **justice myth** (the Ke cycle); she herself lives by **wisdom myths**, which promise nothing and obligate everything. She is giving Dafyd a story to run on while retaining, for herself, the one that's actually true. The generosity of this is that she is letting him have the easier faith because she judges he needs it, while she carries the harder one.

### TFOB Ch 32 — The Decanting
At the ceremony for the first decanted lamb-sack infants — the culmination of the plan she originally walked out of a room to protest — she stands next to Dafyd and whispers in his ear:

> "I still think this is a horrifying perversion. If there is a Gallantist hell, we're all going to burn there."
>
> "I do. I know that."
>
> "You look glum for a man who won."
>
> "There's only one victory that matters to me. This isn't it."
>
> "All the throne and none of the crown for you. You ever regret getting the job?"
>
> "No. If it wasn't me someone else would have it, and they'd do it wrong. I'm where I need to be to do what I need to do. Self-pity is a waste of time."
> — TFOB Ch 32

She has never updated her ethical verdict on the lamb sacks. She has simply accepted that she is inside the atrocity now and that her job is to make its children into something that will survive it. The line "I do. I know that" from Dafyd is an ongoing ledger between them: she has not let him forget.

### TFOB Ch 35 — The Last Three
After the swarm (Jellit) dies in the attack on the Sovran and Rickar is already gone, Uuya is one of only three humans left alive who know the conspiracy exists — Dafyd, Jessyn, and herself. Dafyd has just learned from Jellit-the-swarm's final revelation that the Sovran is replaceable, that killing her will accomplish nothing. Uuya answers first:

> "She's right. We may be playing a very long game. It may be a game we leave to the great-grandchildren of our great-grandchildren."
>
> "I take some comfort in it. Consider that this isn't a problem we can fix. By ourselves, we don't have the power. It's never your responsibility to do something that can't be done. You do your part, and you help the next generation carry it a little farther, and then the one after that."
> — Uuya, TFOB Ch 35

This is where her arc lands. The folklorist who specialized in stories that outlive their tellers is now the conspirator who reframes a catastrophic strategic failure as the normal timeline of the work. She is, at the end of the book, the only character who has arrived at the time scale the Deep Lothark has been using for millennia.

## Relationships

### With Dafyd — Mentor disguised as employee
Their relationship is built on her recognizing his aunt Dorinda in him and choosing to treat him as Dorinda's heir. She is technically his subordinate in the moiety org chart ("minister of propaganda") but almost every scene has her correcting, teaching, reframing, or physically hauling him to his feet. She is the only person in the book who can tell him "That's inside voice shit" (Ch 31) and have him obey. She also keeps the moral ledger: every time the plan crosses a line she said shouldn't be crossed, she tells him she knows and doesn't forgive it, and then keeps working. This is not hypocrisy — it is the precise pattern her wisdom-myth worldview demands.

### With Dorinda (offstage, deceased) — The Absent Standard
Dorinda is the implicit third party in every Dafyd–Uuya scene. Uuya admired Dorinda's ruthlessness without being friends with her. She agrees to work with Dafyd only once she can see Dorinda in him, and the last thing she says in the Ch 9 recruitment is "Now I see Dory." Her acceptance of the plan is fundamentally an acceptance of Dorinda-style politics as the only politics now available.

### With Ver Cannedan — Her Instrument
She finds him a role (movement study of the Carryx) that both neutralizes his threat to Dafyd and extracts real strategic value from him. After Ver's rehabilitation she cheerfully calls the placement her own idea and takes credit for it. She treats him with the kind of briskly affectionate management a senior academic uses on a brilliant, difficult grad student.

### With Tonner Freis — Contempt Behind Politeness
She barely speaks to him. In their one direct exchange ("The burdens we bear") she is openly performing false sympathy. At his memorial she rewrites his story for him into what the moiety now needs to believe Tonner was, explicitly over-writing the real man because the real man is no longer useful. Her Ch 24 aside — "Look at your friend Tonner. He was a very clever man with a very narrow view of the universe, and now he was an open-hearted, kind, supportive teacher" — is both epitaph and manifesto for what her job is.

### With Jellit Kaul / the swarm — Suspicion
She notices by Ch 15 that something is wrong with Jellit and probes Dafyd directly about it. She is the first human besides Dafyd to register Jellit's fall from grace and flag it as politically meaningful. She never learns what the swarm actually is, but her instinct is sound throughout — Jellit is not Jellit, and Dafyd is lying about why. At the memorial for Tonner (Ch 24) she notes coldly: "Jellit Kaul didn't attend," and Dafyd can't hide his reaction.

### With Andermus — Counterpart, Not Ally
Uuya has Andermus's respect — Andermus is the one who browbeat her into the Ch 9 garden meeting — but Uuya's political analysis of her is cool: "That woman loves structure and law more than she loves people." They operate in parallel: Andermus runs coercion, Uuya runs narrative, and both report to Dafyd without liking each other.

### With Korham — Working Partnership
She is physically on site at the nursery/school construction alongside Bastien Korham's crew, steadying ladders while sweating. She approves of him — "charming and he makes things that people use" — and the one aesthetic object she owns (the Bellenn mural) came to her through the same barter economy Korham runs. Hers is the only interior we ever see built from that economy.

### With Jessyn and Campar — The Final Council
They are essentially strangers to her throughout the first nine-tenths of the book; Jessyn has been on World, Campar in deep space. When they return, Uuya is matter-of-fact about absorbing them into the conspiracy she has effectively been running ground-side. In the final meeting she is the one who speaks first after Jessyn proposes the long view, immediately upgrading Jessyn's "setback" into the full millennia-scale frame.

### With the moiety at large — Beloved
She tells Dafyd flat out "They love me" at Ch 24 and the narrative does not challenge her. This matters. She is the only senior member of the new governance Dafyd built who is popular, and she uses that popularity to make the rest of the plan go down: the memorial frames Dafyd and Andermus as necessary evils adjacent to Uuya's warmth. She has converted her public persona into structural cover for a conspiracy the public cannot know about.

### With Ekur-Tkalal — Studiously Invisible
She has no on-page interaction with the keeper-librarian. This is the point. The moiety's only surviving storyteller is the person the Carryx never have a reason to look at. The Ch 16 Ekur POV, which details every human in Ekur's personal sphere, notes Dafyd, Andermus, the Soft Lothark, Jellit's project — not Uuya. She is already operating below the bird's line of sight, exactly as her own Ke myth prescribes.

## Political and Philosophical Position

Uuya has a coherent theory of what she is doing that is internally consistent across her appearances. The elements:

1. **Stories control who the hero is, and heroes rotate.** (Ch 24) History is not fixed because memory is not fixed. The work of a storyteller is not to preserve the past accurately but to construct the past the future will need.

2. **Justice myths are what you commission; wisdom myths are what you live by.** (Ch 31) She is writing the Ke cycle as a justice myth for the children — "God will punish the wicked and reward the just, even if it takes a long time" — because justice myths are what motivate populations. She herself operates on wisdom myths, which promise nothing. She is aware of the gap and keeps it deliberate.

3. **Moral positions are maintained inside complicity.** (Ch 32) Her walkout in Ch 6 was ethically right and she has never retracted it. When the first lamb-sack child is decanted in Ch 32, she tells Dafyd to his face that she still thinks the project is "a horrifying perversion." She does this while actively working to make it succeed. The novel's ethics live in this refusal to collapse the contradiction — you can know you are doing wrong and still do it and still refuse to say it was right.

4. **Time scale is a political weapon.** (Ch 35) Playing the Carryx's timeline — generations — is the only coherent response to an empire the Deep Lothark has been undermining for millennia. Her formulation — "You do your part, and you help the next generation carry it a little farther" — is the end-state of her arc. She has scaled out until the horizon is wide enough for her work to fit.

5. **Physical labor is part of the cure for despair.** (Ch 31) She does not let Dafyd stay in his head. "There's a beauty in truth. There's also a beauty in work." She literally yanks him off the floor and into a shift at the school construction.

## Arc Summary

Refuser → recruit → architect → anchor.

- **Refuser** (Ch 6): She walks out of the lamb-sack meeting on ethical grounds. The book endorses her.
- **Recruit** (Ch 9): Dafyd reframes the project as embedded resistance through myth, naming her Gallantist models for her. She joins — not because she's reversed her ethical position but because she has identified him as the heir to a ruthlessness she recognizes.
- **Architect** (Ch 15, 16, 22–24, 31, 32): She produces the Ke cycle, writes Tonner's funeral, engineers Ver Cannedan's rehabilitation, briefs Dafyd on the affective politics of the moiety, and physically builds the school. She is simultaneously the conspiracy's writer, personnel officer, house therapist, and senior moral conscience.
- **Anchor** (Ch 35): When the conspiracy's strategic goal — killing the Sovran — collapses, she is the one who reframes failure as timeline. She becomes the closest thing the surviving conspirators have to a teacher.

She does not die in this book. The three-conspirator ending — Dafyd, Jessyn, Uuya, plus the spy — leaves her as the only one whose worldview is already adapted to what remains of the fight.

## Why She Matters to the Book

Uuya is the only character in *The Faith of Beasts* whose profession is narrative itself, and Corey uses her to make the book's meta-structure load-bearing. The six-part epigraph cycle — the Ke and Ash-Abbé myth — is attributed to her in every panel: "*From* Myths of Origin: Field Notes and Analysis*, Uuya Tomos, editor.*" This means the reader is reading, across the book's length, the very document she is in-universe constructing as the resistance curriculum for the captive-born children. The novel's myth is her working draft.

Three consequences flow from this:

1. **The reader is positioned as one of her students.** Every time a part opens with a Ke panel, the reader receives what the Carryx-born children will later receive: an ancient-sounding story whose coded content is patience, mimicry, and the slow tethering of the great bird by song. Corey has placed the reader inside Uuya's pedagogy.

2. **Her wisdom-myth/justice-myth distinction is the novel's own ars poetica.** TFOB itself is a wisdom myth for the reader (the Carryx may not fall in our lifetime, good people die unredeemed, the long game is the only game) framed around a justice myth for the children (Ke will tether the bird; Shavas Rey will wake). She has told us how to read the book she is inside.

3. **She is the book's argument that the human moiety will survive.** The science plots (Tonner's lab) produce corpses. The resistance plots (Jessyn's bombing run, the swarm's archive raid, the attack on the Sovran) produce corpses. Uuya's plot — a curriculum, a school, a memorial script, a myth — produces the only output that persists: the children, and the stories the children will be raised on. If there is a Book 3 in which humans begin to turn the tables, the infrastructure for it was built in her chapters.

One structural note: she is never a POV character. The reader never enters her head. Everything in this analysis comes from what she says and does on the page. This is consistent with what she says she does for a living — a folklorist works in the third person.
