# Jessyn's Sabotage Cylinder: Textual Evidence and Analysis

## Summary

In *The Faith of Beasts*, Jessyn Kaul is given a sabotage device by the deathless soldier Corvall during her field assignment on the planet Third Gardener calls "World." She smuggles it back to the Carryx exploration ships, opens it the morning the Carryx prep for combat with an incoming enemy ("rescue") force, and then experiences a violent but ambiguous sequence of events as the ships try to lift off. The text never definitively explains the device's mechanism, the *full* scope of its direct effects, or whether the sabotage technically "worked" in the sense Corvall intended. What the prose strongly implies — but never states outright — is that it crippled the Carryx ships' ability to make orbit and forced them to fight on the ground, with the apparent outcome that some of the ships survived (Jessyn's certainly did and eventually returned to the world-palace) and the rest is left for the reader to infer.

This file collects every relevant passage and analyzes what is and isn't explained on the page.

---

## 1. The Device's Origin and Physical Description

The device is handed to Jessyn at the wreck of the deathless landing platform after Corvall opens the three remaining drop-pods (one containing a dead comrade).

> "The four stood together like they were about to pray for the dead, but instead of a candle, Corvall held out a black ceramic cylinder the size of a fist. Its cap had indentations around the edge to make it easier to turn. When Corvall put it in Jessyn's hand, it was surprisingly heavy." (line 3365)

Key physical traits gathered across the text:
- **Material:** "black ceramic" (line 3365); later reduced to "tiny flakes of black rust" after activation (line 5471)
- **Shape/size:** cylinder "the size of a fist," with a turn-off cap that has finger indentations (line 3365)
- **Weight:** "surprisingly heavy" (line 3365); "heavy enough to pull at the shoulder of her tunic" (line 3387); "heavy in her pocket, but not unpleasant" (line 5399)
- **Temperature:** "still just a little bit warm, like an egg with something gestating inside it" while cached in the orchard (line 4123); later "the metal was warm. It seemed to weigh more than she remembered" (line 5383)
- **Casing decay:** the cylinder itself dissolves after use — first into "tiny flakes of black rust," then breaks down further until "all that was left was a persistent coolness and a smell like a stone quarry in winter" (line 5471)

## 2. What Garral Tells Jessyn It Is and How to Use It

Corvall's instructions are filtered through the chain of game-of-telephone translation Jessyn flags as suspect ("you know I have literally no faith that what you're about to tell me is accurate, right?" line 3371). Garral relays the operational summary:

> "If there were a better way, I'd be for it," Garral said. "For what it's worth, I don't think it's really all that complicated. Not your part, anyway. **This is the thing that will disable the ships and weapons. It's easy to use. You just open it. It's packed with a swarm of very, very small machines that are pre-programmed to do what needs to be done.** Corvall says that if you open the container as soon as the Carryx start gearing up for a fight, that'll be all you need to do." (line 3373)

Operational guidance:
- **Trigger condition:** open it "as soon as the Carryx start gearing up for a fight" (line 3373)
- **Range/coupling:** "It'll work better if you're close to the ships. Or inside one of them." (line 3377)
- **Concealment:** keep it hidden until use; if found, "the whole thing goes to hell" (line 3377)
- **Mechanism (as relayed):** "a swarm of very, very small machines that are pre-programmed" — i.e., nanotechnology of some kind. Jessyn later mentally calls the contents a "canister of spores that would disable the Carryx ships" (line 5393), using "spores" as a loose biological metaphor.

So the in-text label is "**a swarm of very small pre-programmed machines**." The narrative never gives a more technical explanation, never names the technology, and never has a Carryx, Sinen, or human scientist analyze it after the fact.

## 3. What Jessyn Sees When She Opens It

The activation scene is the most concrete physical description we get:

> "When she was alone in her room, she pulled the cylinder out from under her mattress. Thin lines of dried mucus marked where snails had traveled over it. She hefted it… She took the body of the thing in one hand, gripped the top with the other, and twisted. The cap came off easily." (line 5465)

> "**The dust that spilled out was a dull ashy gray, dense as sand but finer.** Jessyn let it pour through her fingers and onto the deck. For a moment, it seemed inert." (line 5467)

> "**The cold started at the vestigial webbing between her fingers, first a chill and then a numbing bite that spread.** She flapped her hand, brushing off the dust at the same time she thought *Well, that's pretty damned endothermic.* **The dust on the deck moved like invisible winds were stirring it and leaving a few pale needles of frost behind.**" (line 5469)

> "And then, between one breath and the next, it was all gone and the cylinder that had carried it decayed to tiny flakes of black rust on her bed. Then that broke down too, and **all that was left was a persistent coolness and a smell like a stone quarry in winter.**" (line 5471)

So the *immediately observed* effects of the device are:
1. Ashy gray dust pours out, behaving like sand.
2. The reaction is strongly **endothermic** — Jessyn's hand goes numb with cold; frost forms.
3. The dust appears to move on its own ("invisible winds stirring it") — consistent with self-propelled nanomachines.
4. Within a breath or two, the dust **disappears entirely** (consumed itself, dispersed, or moved away).
5. The cylinder casing also **self-destructs** into rust and then into nothing, leaving only a residual chill and a "stone quarry in winter" smell.
6. **No alarms. No visible chain reaction. No felt change to the ship.**

Jessyn explicitly lampshades the absence of any obvious result:

> "Jessyn sat, waiting. Now that it was done, she recognized that she'd strategized and worried over everything up to this moment, and then had no plan for what came after. **Some part of her had expected the sabotage to take place all at once, like a wave of darkness passing through the ships and disabling everything it touched. Or alarms to sound. Something. Instead, her cell slowly grew warmer, the voices from the common room changed without ever shifting their significance. The lights stayed on. If it had failed, it would have looked just the same.**" (line 5473)

She then explicitly invokes minima physics / superposition as a frame for her uncertainty (line 5475): she has no way to tell, from inside the ship, whether the device worked at all.

## 4. What Happens When the Carryx Try to Lift Off

The next scene is the only place the text shows possible *macro-scale* effects of the device. It is deliberately ambiguous — the Carryx ship-mind announcements and Jessyn's perceptions are the only data the reader gets. The Carryx half-mind announces departure (line 5481), and the ship begins to lift.

> "She also really hoped they wouldn't actually lift off. **If the ships managed to make it only halfway out of the atmosphere before the dust shut off the engines, she and all the others would be just as dead as if the enemy shot them.**" (line 5483)

The shaking grows worse, takes on "weird, uncomfortable harmonics like the metal was singing in a distant, inhuman choir" (line 5493). Then:

> "Something stuttered. Something snapped. For a fraction of a second, she was weightless, and the whole room rose up and struck her on the back. An alarm was blaring someplace, or maybe it was some alien screaming in panic of its own. The lights flickered, went off, flickered again." (line 5497)

> "**Relief rose up in her chest — the sabotage had worked, and she'd lived through the aftermath — but it didn't last.**" (line 5499)

> "All moieties not tasked to violence or repair are to remain braced and in position. Do not release from your areas of protection until given permission. All moieties with duties in protection and damage mitigation, prepare yourselves to serve. **Brace for enemy attack.**" (line 5501)

This is the closest the text comes to confirming the device functioned. Jessyn *interprets* the stutter, the snap, the moment of weightlessness, the alarm, and the lights flickering as the sabotage taking hold. The narration never explicitly attributes those events to the cylinder — the reader is meant to share Jessyn's inference.

After that announcement, Jessyn assumes she will die in the engagement. Then:

> "The lights of the ship went black, then came back on at a blinding intensity. The hum that had gone silent returned as an earsplitting shriek, and the ship lurched. Jessyn fell off the bed, splitting open both knees, and screamed. Something else happened, something violent enough to shake the room…" (line 5513)

> "The cell was quiet. The hum of the engines began, quieted, swelled." (lines 5517–5519)

> "All moieties, prepare to leave the local gravity well. Assume turbulence and lock down equipment. **The transition to asymmetric space will occur in seven cycles.** Plan your physical needs accordingly." (line 5521)

> "A few minutes later — a lifetime — the hand of gravity pressed her gently into her bed. The Carryx ship murmured as it rose. The planet Third Gardener had called World fell away invisibly behind her, and with it, everything that had happened there." (line 5523)

So **the ship Jessyn was on did, in fact, lift off and reach asymmetric space**. The engines came back online after the silence, and she ends up returned to the world-palace.

## 5. Aftermath: What Did the Device Actually Do?

This is the central unanswered question, and the text leaves it deliberately blurry.

**What we know happened:**
- There was a brief loss of engine function during liftoff (the "stutter," the "snap," the moment of weightlessness, the lights going dark).
- A combat exchange occurred while the ships were in or near the atmosphere ("Brace for enemy attack," line 5501).
- Jessyn's ship survived and made the jump to asymmetric space (lines 5521–5523).
- Garral managed to leave Jessyn a final coded note in her field notes during the crisis: "**one of the curious effects in botanic signaling — especially in strawberries — is the need for a plant to present sufficiently convincing evidence of countermeasures to an unwelcome guest. See also: authenticity and deception in interspecific predation markers, and forgive me for leaving without you.**" (line 5531) — implying he and the deathless escaped.

**What the text never confirms:**
- Whether the other four Carryx exploration ships were destroyed, disabled, escaped, or also lifted off.
- Whether the device's nanomachines actually shut down the engines, or whether the brief engine failure was caused by enemy fire.
- Whether the device was countered by the Carryx, dispersed too thinly to take effect on the larger ship systems, or only had a temporary local effect.
- Whether the Carryx ever traced the sabotage back to Jessyn (later text shows she was simply returned to the world-palace with the other survivors — see line 5630, "One hundred and sixteen were coming back, including Jessyn and Campar.")
- Whether the deathless rescue mission succeeded in evacuating Garral and the children.

## 6. Jessyn's Own Accounting of It Later

In Chapter 33 (line 5883), Jessyn summarizes her assignment to Dafyd:

> "**This deathless enemy that the Carryx are so wound up about? When I was on assignment, I came across a bunch of them. I sabotaged Carryx ships and assassinated a Sinen overseer to help them escape too.** It's a long story. I'll tell you the whole thing, but the important thing is I sent one of us off with them."

She uses the word "**sabotaged**" plainly and treats the operation as a success at the level of helping Garral escape. But notice she does *not* claim to have destroyed any ships — the framing is "to help them escape," consistent with the device's purpose being to *prevent the Carryx ships from killing the inbound deathless*, not to annihilate them.

This matches Garral's earlier statement of intent: the device is "**the thing that will disable the ships and weapons**" (line 3373) — disable, not destroy. A temporary disabling that grounds the Carryx for long enough for the deathless to retrieve their people would be consistent with the observed events: brief engine failure, a fight, eventual recovery and lift-off.

## 7. Interpretive Read

Putting all of this together, the most parsimonious reading the text supports — without ever making it explicit — is:

1. The cylinder contained a **swarm of self-propelled, endothermic nanomachines** (Garral's "very, very small machines" + Jessyn's observation of self-stirring dust + the heat sink behavior).
2. The machines **dispersed through the ship rapidly** (gone "between one breath and the next") and **self-consumed their casing**, leaving only a chill and a stone-quarry smell — i.e., they had a brief operational window.
3. Their effect was **temporary disablement of propulsion / weapons systems**, which is what Jessyn felt as the "stutter," "snap," weightlessness, and lights-out during attempted lift-off.
4. The disablement window was long enough to **let the deathless rescue ship intervene** but the Carryx ships either purged the swarm, restored function, or simply outlasted its operational period — Jessyn's ship recovers and makes orbit.
5. The author leaves the **strategic outcome** (how many ships survived, how many deathless were saved, what the Carryx conclude) deliberately offstage, mirroring Jessyn's own "I have no way to know from in here" experience.

## 8. Bottom Line

**The direct effects of the device are never fully explained on the page.** The most the text offers is:

- **Mechanism (claimed):** a swarm of pre-programmed nanomachines packed in a black ceramic cylinder.
- **Observable effects when opened:** ashy gray dust that self-disperses, sharply endothermic reaction (cold/frost), self-consumption of dust and casing within seconds, no alarms, no visible chain reaction.
- **Apparent macro effect:** a brief failure of the Carryx ship's engines and lights during attempted lift-off (interpreted by Jessyn as "the sabotage had worked"), followed by combat with an "enemy attack," followed by recovery of engine function and successful transition to asymmetric space.
- **Confirmed downstream outcome:** Garral and the deathless got away (he was able to leave Jessyn a goodbye note); Jessyn's ship survived and returned her to the world-palace.
- **Unconfirmed:** whether other Carryx ships were destroyed; whether the device's nanomachines or enemy fire caused the engine stutter; whether the sabotage was ever detected as such by the Carryx.

The novel uses Jessyn's confined, sensorily limited perspective to deny the reader (and Jessyn) any clean confirmation. The device is functionally a black box: she opens it, *something* happens, she lives.
