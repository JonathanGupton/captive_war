# Dafyd Alkhor — Relationship Arcs Across *The Faith of Beasts*

*The Captive's War, Book 2: The Faith of Beasts — James S.A. Corey*

---

## Starting Position

At the open of TFOB, Dafyd is the sole human authority figure in the Carryx world-palace and the most universally distrusted person in the human moiety. The events of *The Mercy of Gods* — turning in Ostencour's rebellion and getting *"Synnia and Dennia, Ostencour and Allstin"* killed, plus the woman he loved (Else) — are the precondition for every relationship in the second book. The narrator establishes the baseline plainly in Chapter 1:

> "Of the group Dafyd had known on Anjiin, the only ones around him now were Tonner, who hated him, and Jellit, who had gone from an almost-enemy to Dafyd's accomplice in exposing the human rebellion against the Carryx."
> — TFOB Ch 1

And from Uuya Tomos, three months in:

> "It's not paranoia when it's true. They hate you. Of course they do. They hate the Carryx, and you're the Carryx's whip hand. Every shitty thing they have to do, every policy they have to follow, it comes through you."
> — TFOB Ch 27

The book's arc is a sorting of who comes around, who walks away, and who hates him at a higher resolution by the end than they did at the beginning.

---

## Sorted by End-of-Book State

### Category 1 — Allied and Respecting (the new conspiracy)

These are the people who finish the book working *with* Dafyd, on his terms, with full knowledge of what he is.

#### Uuya Tomos

- **Start:** Conscripted into Dafyd's new governance team as a folklorist/social historian. Sits across from him at the first meeting with *"a frown that could have cut glass"* (Ch 5) and immediately needles him: *"Minister of propaganda?"*
- **Hinge:** Ch 17–20 — Dafyd recognizes that her propaganda work (myths of origin, schoolroom curriculum) is the only thing the children-being-decanted will inherit. He hands her the propaganda role explicitly. She accepts. By Ch 27 (Tonner's funeral) she's already writing his speeches: *"You want to pull attention off how Freis got killed, putting the focus back on everyone else who's died seems like a good strategy. And I only feel a little bit dirty helping you cover up whatever it is you're hiding."*
- **End:** One of the last three living conspirators (with Dafyd and Jessyn). Delivers the book's mature thesis on the conspiracy at the end: *"It may be a game we leave to the great-grandchildren of our great-grandchildren."* (Ch 36) She is the only person on-page who teases Dafyd casually, and the only one who can pinch him in public without consequence.

#### Bastien Korham

- **Start:** Conscripted into governance from the same first meeting. Was a manufacturing-cooperative leader on Anjiin (Ch 5). Treats Dafyd with brisk, unsentimental professionalism from the first handshake.
- **Hinge:** Builds the school, the gymnasium, the nursery. Delivers the human moiety's *physical* infrastructure for the next generation.
- **End:** Genuine collaborator. By Ch 30 he is putting a *"companionable hand"* on Dafyd's shoulder — *"in the same place that Ekur-Tkalal had"* — telling him *"Ferdan Bellenn is going to paint a mural across from the windows with great figures from human history. If I have my way, our children are going to grow up with our heritage."* Korham echoes Dafyd's plan back to him, executed. Uuya notes the moiety *"don't much like Korham either, but at least he's charming and he makes things that people use"* — but his working relationship with Dafyd is undamaged.

#### Llian Andermus

- **Start:** Same first meeting. Former director of operational security for Itzibahn Common. Gives a *"poker face"* through Dafyd's pitch (Ch 5).
- **Hinge:** Ch 24 — present when the Soft Lothark kill Tonner in the garden. She is the only human witness to the murder *and* to the Deep Lothark cover-up. Her body convulses in suppressed disgust; she swallows it; *"After, there was no sign she'd been disturbed at all by the things she'd just witnessed."* From that moment she is operationally locked in. Dafyd, walking away: *"Andermus saw the body but she's tough. I think I can count on her."*
- **End:** Runs internal security for the moiety, manages the assault-and-misconduct caseload, ramps up the kind of administrative state Dafyd needs in order to manage a population of three thousand. Uuya notes that the moiety *"hate Andermus too. That woman loves structure and law more than she loves people"* — but her loyalty to the conspiracy is solid.

#### Brun (early/mid arc)

- **Start:** Tonner's tall, thin replacement second-in-command. Greets Dafyd brightly with the static centrifuge demo (Ch 1). Initially happy to be working with Dafyd directly.
- **Hinge:** After Tonner's death, leads protein-translation work and manages decantation of the babies. Genuine professional partnership for most of the book.
- **End — fractures:** Brun organizes a labor stoppage demanding the removal of Carryx guards from the labs (Ch 30). Dafyd warns him repeatedly that the Carryx will not understand a labor demand and will only read it as an insubordination challenge. Brun pushes anyway. Dafyd has the Soft Lothark hold him down and **breaks his right arm** between elbow and a builder's brick (Ch 34) — the Carryx ritual de-escalation Ver Cannedan taught him. Brun's last line to Dafyd: *"You're a fucking monster. I helped you. I handed babies to you."* Brun is alive at end of book but the personal relationship is broken. The professional relationship he must maintain — there is no one else.

#### Ver Cannedan

- **Start:** Refused his lab assignment (Ch 7), called Dafyd in to dress him down personally, then **punched Dafyd in the face** when Dafyd asked him to do work he considered beneath him.
- **Hinge:** Dafyd, on Uuya's advice, doesn't punish him. Instead reassigns him to study Carryx body language and combat ritual — a job that uses his actual skills as a master choreographer. Ver thrives. By Ch 20 he is delivering Dafyd field-work-grade observations on Carryx submission rituals (the broken arm, the feeding-arm tuck) that Dafyd uses to *literally save his life* during the Tonner murder, and that Dafyd later uses to break Brun's arm in the same Carryx ritual register.
- **End:** Reliable contributor. *"He just needed a job that made him feel important again."* (Uuya, Ch 20) Ver doesn't appear in the final-act conspiracy meetings, but he's a working part of the moiety's intelligence apparatus.

#### Clae Audin (the swarm, second iteration)

- **Start:** A new researcher who arrives in Ch 33 — except she isn't. She is the swarm, having reabsorbed Jellit's body and refashioned it into a young woman with a fabricated Anjiin biography. She walks up to Dafyd in his garden mid-meeting with Jessyn and weeps with relief.
- **Hinge:** Dafyd accepts the new identity. Tells Jessyn: *"She's on our side. She knows about my… She knows about my plot for bloody and cosmic revenge. I trust her. You can too."* (Ch 33)
- **End:** Dafyd's principal archive researcher, the one who delivers the lichen theory of the deathless (Ch 36). They are professional partners. The romantic component the swarm-as-Else tried to revive is dead and buried; Clae does not push. They end the book holding a baby together while Dafyd works through the *"What if there were two?"* question that becomes the book's last line of conspiracy. Clae has earned a working trust Dafyd refuses to give the swarm-as-Else.

#### Jessyn Kaul

- **Start:** Sent off-world before the book begins. Carries a complicated mid-position on Dafyd: *"Dafyd was… He was just the guy who cleaned the glassware in my lab before he was in charge of everything"* (Ch 9, defending him to Garral). Defends the *"It was complicated"* version, not the *"he's a hero"* version.
- **Hinge:** Independently runs her own conspiracy on World — sabotages Carryx ships, assassinates a Sinen overseer, sends Garral off with the deathless. Returns to the world-palace harder and surer of herself than she left.
- **End:** Embraces Dafyd warmly on her return (Ch 33), drops the world-palace's biggest piece of intelligence on him in one sentence (*"The deathless enemy? It's us."*), takes a chair in the conspiracy's final meeting, and contributes the suggestion that becomes the second-half of the book's reframed strategy. She doesn't pretend Dafyd is uncomplicated. She just works with him.

### Category 2 — Returned-Friend, Loving but Not Aligned

#### Campar

- **Start:** Off-world. Loyal to Dafyd in a guarded way; defends *"Dafyd's plan"* to Rickar in Ch 18, only to be told flatly by Rickar that *"Alkhor doesn't have one."*
- **Hinge:** Survives the disabled enemy command ship at Ashtin-Kah, half-cooked by field weapons, returns to the world-palace as a damaged man with a cane. Debriefs warmly with Dafyd and Clae in the garden (Ch 35). Calls Dafyd *"Big guy"*. The friendship is intact.
- **End — divergence:** In the final conspiracy meeting (Ch 36) Campar proposes a *radical alternative*: stop fighting. *"Decide that dying as heroes is less appealing than living as corn and chickens."* Dafyd storms out — not in betrayal, but in exhausted disagreement. They are still friends. They simply now disagree about what the moiety is *for*. Campar's last on-page act is to call after Dafyd: *"Dafyd! Stop, you don't need to—"*

### Category 3 — Reconciled at the Last Moment, Then Dead

#### Tonner Freis

- **Start:** *"Tonner, who hated him."* (Ch 1.) Resents reporting to Alkhor, deliberately stalls on reports, withholds data to make Dafyd squirm, says *"I'm putting off real work so I can justify myself to the Carryx's favorite pet"* in front of Uuya minutes before his death.
- **Hinge — partial:** In their final conversation, Dafyd opens with *"Good to see you. I get a little tired from not having anyone from before left,"* and Tonner is genuinely moved — *"Tonner felt a stab of something like shame. He covered it up by being dismissive."* (Ch 24). They are about to have a real breakthrough conversation about the Soft Lothark's secret communication when the Soft Lothark hear Tonner explain it and beat him to death.
- **End:** Killed by Soft Lothark guards in Dafyd's presence (Ch 24). Dafyd organizes a public memorial and delivers a speech written by Uuya. The *"hated him"* of Ch 1 is never fully discharged on-page, but the last private exchange they had was warm. The widest reading: a man who hated Dafyd professionally but was about to find him bearable again, killed before reconciliation could complete.

### Category 4 — Stayed Hostile, Lost

#### Rickar Daumatin

- **Start:** Off-world. Carries a bone-deep grudge — Dafyd's exposure of the rebellion is what got Rickar's lover Dennia killed.
- **Hinge — there isn't one.** Rickar's most-quoted line in the book is to Campar in Ch 18: *"Dafyd Alkhor is their favorite pet. He doesn't have power. None of us do… You shouldn't tell him about Alkhor's plan. Alkhor doesn't have one."* He dies still believing this.
- **End:** Killed by the swarm's death-burst in the evacuation pod at Jurupe (Ch 29). Dafyd grieves him sincerely (*"Another one of my group died. Rickar Daumatin… and I don't know what to feel about it,"* Ch 30). The reconciliation they never had is one of the book's quiet losses; Dafyd never gets to tell Rickar he was wrong about there being no plan, and Rickar never gets to learn his own death was the plan's most important act.

#### Farad Morse and the visualization workgroup

- **Start:** Dafyd's exposure of the rebellion in Book 1 specifically killed **Llaren Morse** — Farad's nephew. Farad inherits Jellit's old workgroup mid-book.
- **Hinge:** None. He stands across from Dafyd *"with his arms crossed"* and the rest of his team *"arrayed like a gang getting ready for a brawl"* (Ch 25). When Dafyd tries to deflect his demands with a bureaucratic "Jellit's on a different project," Farad's response is *"If he won't be rejoining us, we can go to him. You just say where to find him."* — an explicit threat.
- **End:** Stays an enemy through book's end. Not on-page in the final-act conspiracy. The Morse line of the human moiety is hostile by a kind of family inheritance that Dafyd cannot fix.

#### The wider human moiety

- **Start:** Hostile. *"To everyone who hadn't known him before, Dafyd was the voice of the Carryx. The man to speak to when something was needed, and the conduit for demands from the empire. The high priest interceding between his people and their godlike masters."* (Ch 1)
- **End:** Still hostile. Uuya's *"It's not paranoia when it's true. They hate you. Of course they do."* (Ch 27) is the last word the book offers on the moiety's ambient feeling toward Dafyd, and the book never contradicts it. Korham gets the credit for the visible work; Andermus eats the resentment for security; Dafyd absorbs the diffuse hatred. He breaks Brun's arm in front of half the team. His Rak-hund-and-Soft-Lothark escort is read as a Carryx leash. By the close of Ch 34: *"as he glanced around the room, half a dozen people looked away, avoiding his gaze. Pretending that they hadn't been watching him."*

### Category 5 — Special Cases

#### Jellit Kaul / the swarm-as-Else / Clae

This is one entity in three forms; Dafyd's relationship with it splits in three.

- **Jellit (the original):** Dies before the book opens, mind absorbed by the swarm. Dafyd treats Jellit as still-alive throughout the early book — unaware that the swarm has consumed him. The personal relationship he believes he has with Jellit (warm, intimate, *"He's always been here"*) is in fact with the swarm. He grieves Jellit when he learns the truth.
- **Swarm-as-Else (Ch 26):** The swarm reshapes Jellit's body into Else's and tries to seduce Dafyd back into the relationship he had with Else in Book 1. Dafyd refuses, with the bookmark line of his arc: ***"The reason this is never going to happen again is that now I know you murdered my friends."*** This is the only on-page case where Dafyd actively *withdraws* a relationship rather than receiving distrust. He tells the swarm-as-Else to *"Find me when this is fixed,"* and means it as banishment.
- **Clae (Ch 33+):** The swarm rebuilds itself a new identity Dafyd can work with. Their relationship is then strictly professional, warm in a coworker register. They share secrets, run the lichen-theory archive work, end the book holding a baby — but Dafyd's personal trust is permanently capped. *"Complicated,"* he tells Jessyn, *"She's on our side, though."*

The arc is **trust → grief → rage → working alliance**. Dafyd never returns to intimacy with this entity. Clae is the second-best terms they can be on, and they stabilize there.

#### Ghati

Never directly meets Dafyd on-page in TFOB. Knows him only through Campar's accounts. Ends the book partnered with Vaudai on a six-thousand-year stellar-armature mission, having ended things with Campar. His view of Dafyd is implicit and inherited, not personal.

#### Ekur-Tkalal (the Carryx keeper-librarian)

Worth noting in any relationship inventory. Begins TFOB in the same dominance-by-default register as TMoG (chitin underfoot, fighting arms always near the floor). By Ch 30, after Dafyd has navigated the labor-dispute and report-format crises without triggering a moiety cull, Ekur-Tkalal *physically touches Dafyd's shoulder* with a feeding arm — a gesture *"Dafyd had never seen before"* — and says *"There is only one Sovran."* Dafyd registers it as *"the first time he could remember a Carryx doing that that hadn't resulted in the death of the human."* It is the closest thing in either book to a Carryx-side endorsement, and it functions as a parallel arc: while the human moiety hates Dafyd more, the Carryx keeper-librarian trusts him more. Both trends are operational by book's end.

## Patterns

### The hatred is *function*, not character

Uuya's *"It's not paranoia when it's true"* is the book's working theory: Dafyd is hated because he is the only conduit, and every Carryx-imposed cost arrives via him. His personal warmth is irrelevant to that load-bearing role. This is why his closest *new* allies (Korham, Andermus, Uuya, Ver) are all people who came in *with* an understanding of his structural position and judged him on his execution of it. The people who can't get past the Book-1 betrayal — Tonner, Rickar, Farad Morse, the broader moiety — judge him on identity, not function, and stay enemies.

### The pattern of conversion: *give them a job that uses them*

Every named character who comes around to Dafyd does so because Dafyd assigns them work that fits their skills. Uuya as propagandist-historian. Korham as builder. Andermus as security. Ver as movement-anthropologist. Brun as Tonner's replacement. Clae as archive researcher. The conversion mechanic is always the same: Dafyd hands them the role only they can do, and they discover they would rather be useful to him than aimless. Uuya names this explicitly after the Ver-Cannedan reassignment: *"He just needed a job that made him feel important again."*

### The pattern of failure: *the pre-existing grudge is unrecoverable*

Dafyd cannot reach anyone whose Book-1 wound is personal. Rickar (Dennia), Farad Morse (his nephew Llaren), the swarm-as-Else (the murders that built it). For these characters there is no professional reframe Dafyd can offer. The grudge is the relationship.

### Dafyd loses the people he was closest to and gains the people he's never been close to

By the close of TFOB, the people from the original Dyan workgroup are mostly gone:

- **Else, Synnia, Llaren Morse** — dead before TFOB.
- **Tonner** — dies hating him, mostly.
- **Rickar** — dies hating him.
- **Jellit** — dies absorbed by the swarm.
- **Campar** — survives, loves him, but breaks with him strategically in the final scene.
- **Jessyn** — survives, works with him, never reverts to the warmth of the original group.

In their place, Dafyd's working circle at the end of TFOB is:

- **Uuya Tomos** (didn't know him on Anjiin)
- **Bastien Korham** (didn't know him on Anjiin)
- **Llian Andermus** (didn't know him on Anjiin)
- **Clae** (a new person who is also the swarm)
- **Ver Cannedan** (knew him only as a child memory through his aunt)

The conspiracy that ends the book is being run by a cohort that did not exist in Book 1. The original moiety does not, as a group, forgive Dafyd; he replaces them.

## The Closing Image

The book's last private exchange between Dafyd and a human is with **Clae**, in the dimmed nursery, holding baby Thirty-Four together while Dafyd works out the *"What if there were two?"* idea that becomes the new hypothesis for breaking the Carryx (Ch 34). The image is precise: the original Dyan workgroup is gone, the moiety hates him, Tonner and Rickar are dead, Brun's arm is broken, the swarm-as-Else is banished — and Dafyd is rocking an infant with the swarm-as-Clae, planning the next phase of the conspiracy with the closest thing to a partner he has left.

The relationships he kept at the end are not the ones he started with. They are the ones he **assembled in spite of being hated**.
