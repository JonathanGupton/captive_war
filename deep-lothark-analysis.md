# The Soft Lothark and the Deep Lothark — Analysis

*The Captive's War, Book 2: The Faith of Beasts — James S.A. Corey*

---

## Summary

The Soft Lothark are a long-limbed, squat-bodied, greasy-pelted client species used by the Carryx as jailers and shock-guards — the empire's "agents of appropriate violence." Over the course of *The Faith of Beasts*, they are revealed to carry a hidden second self called the **Deep Lothark**: a species-level distributed consciousness that predates the Carryx conquest, survives on the surface of Soft Lothark bodies as plasmid-like information passed through nibbling and consumption of the dead, and has been planning the empire's destruction for **millennia**. Near the end of the book, the Deep Lothark delivers to Dafyd the single most strategically devastating piece of intelligence in the Captive's War so far: the Carryx Sovran is not unique. The empire runs on a **private creche** system that grows replacement Sovrans in blank eggs on *every* world-palace, and the original Carryx homeworld was already destroyed once — by an enemy called the **Epikainot** — without any perceptible effect on the empire's operation. Killing the Sovran is not a plan. The Carryx are built so that there is no head to cut off.

---

## I. The Soft Lothark — Surface Species

### Physical and behavioral description

From their first appearance the Soft Lothark are described consistently: long limbs, thick torsos, greasy pelts that smell like potting soil, wide thin mouths, flat faces. They use a half-mind translator at the neck, like the Carryx, to produce human-language speech.

Their observable behaviors:

- **Shock-troop violence.** They accompany Carryx librarians and kill on command. Jessyn remembers that she had "seen things like this one murder people and eat their own dead" (Ch 3).
- **Eat their own dead.** Established in MOG and reconfirmed through Jellit's reminder in Ch 11.
- **"Nibble each other hello."** Brun's early observation — Tonner at first takes this as vestigial grooming (Ch 11).
- **Explosive, poisonous death.** When one is killed, it "pulses. Like a balloon someone had blown a breath into. And then it burst. The pale, whitish pus spattered onto the Rak-hund, and it reared back screaming" (Ch 20). Their bodies are armed against posthumous dissection.
- **Self-described function.** When one of them formally delivers Dafyd's hundred-guard escort in Ch 7, it introduces the species: *"We are agents of appropriate violence. It is our role and place in society. The Rak-hund will keep you from unwelcome violence and kill anything of lesser status that you indicate."* (Ch 7)

Within the Carryx hierarchy Soft Lothark sit above rak-hund (real animal weapons) but well below Carryx librarians. They are trusted enough to guard prisoners, confer among themselves when the rak-hund cannot (MOG Ch 9), and escort individual humans; they are disposable enough to be killed without ceremony when they betray knowledge.

### Their strategic invisibility

The Soft Lothark's role in the Carryx system is that of *proxy violence*: they allow Carryx librarians to discharge enforcement duties without interacting directly with animals. This same structural position — always present, always overhearing, always in motion through the empire — is what makes them the ideal carriers of the Deep Lothark.

## II. Tonner's Discovery (Ch 11 → Ch 20)

### The touch and the swab (Ch 11)

Tonner notices an anomaly — Soft Lothark greeting each other by "nibbling" — and decides to collect a tissue sample. Pretending to test whether the species reciprocates human social touching, he lays a gloved hand on a Soft Lothark guard's arm. He is shoved off his feet. The half-mind at the creature's throat delivers a warning that is surprisingly elaborate — long vocalizations, short translation:

> *"Do not physically interact."*
>
> *"Humans often engage in social touching as a friendly gesture between peers."*
>
> The Soft Lothark made what seemed like entirely too long a response for the translated reply.
>
> *"Humans and Soft Lothark do not."*
> — TFOB Ch 11

Tonner's gloved fingers have picked up the creature's surface oils. He presses the glove to a sample plate and takes it back to the lab. Fur smells like potting soil.

### The plasmids — and Tonner's death (Ch 20)

In Ch 20, Tonner calls a meeting to present what he's found. His biological pitch:

> "So we've been talking about convergence, right? Organisms under the same environmental pressures stumbling into very similar solutions… You, me. The human moiety. But all the other moieties too. We're all under the same thumb… Maybe fuck up the competition the way the Night Drinkers tried. But when you're in prison, there's things that you want. Like a way to talk without the boss or the other moieties listening in. And there are only so many good strategies for that."
>
> "These look like plasmids," Dafyd said, holding out the pages. Plasmids were the little circles of DNA that bacteria swapped like trading cards. It was actually a pretty good metaphor.
>
> "These look like plasmids… But they're large-scale. Brun pointed out that our friends" — Tonner nodded toward the Soft Lothark guards — "nibble each other hello. He thought it was vestigial grooming, but Jellit talked about how they eat their dead too. Well, I started looking into it, and I found these. Tiny little information-dense nubblies that they pass back and forth like notes in study hall. Can't be overheard. Hard to intercept. Decoding the message requires being a Lothark. Our buddies here have a whole second channel of communication that —"
> — TFOB Ch 20

He never finishes the sentence. The Soft Lothark guards in the room kick him to death. The world makes "a thumping sound, deep and low and intimate" and Tonner is on his hands and knees, then gone. The Carryx-assigned rak-hund bodyguard intervenes against the Soft Lothark on Dafyd's behalf, and one of the Soft Lothark is killed; its body inflates and bursts, spattering poisonous pus.

The scene leaves Dafyd with three things simultaneously: (1) the surviving Soft Lothark waiting to see what he does, (2) Tonner's report still drafted on Dafyd's Carryx-language workspace, already reading **"Soft Lothark keep private reports sent as food,"** unsent, and (3) the instant awareness that both *sending* the report and *not sending* it are probably fatal.

### "We will understand each other better." (Ch 20)

Dafyd chooses the third option. He does not send the accusation. He instead fabricates for Andermus a cover story — *"The Rak-hund thought it was an attack, and it went for Tonner to protect me. The Soft Lothark tried to stop it. They tried to save Tonner. Do you understand?"* — and the half-mind at the Soft Lothark's throat delivers what is not a translation but an agreement:

> *"We will understand each other better."*
> — TFOB Ch 20

This is the Deep Lothark accepting Dafyd as a negotiating partner. The shared understanding is that Tonner was killed because he named the second channel aloud, that Dafyd now knows it exists, that Dafyd has declined to inform on it, and that a relationship has begun.

### First message: *Humans and the Deep Lothark can help each other.* (Ch 20)

Dafyd, a day or two later, composes a three-symbol message in the Carryx glyph system that floats in the air as a spatial diagram the half-minds cannot parse as sound: **Humans and Soft Lothark can help each other.** A guard comes in; five more wait outside. The guard enters alone, sits across from him, visibly distressed. It gnaws at its own hand for a full minute. Then it reaches up and rewrites Dafyd's symbols — changing the shade and shape of the glyph for *Lothark*:

> **Humans and the Deep Lothark can help each other.**
> — TFOB Ch 20

The distinction — between *Soft Lothark* (loyal to the Carryx) and *Deep Lothark* (their hidden counter-self) — is the reveal. From this point forward, the communication channel exists.

## III. What the Deep Lothark Is (Ch 23)

Dafyd's first extended conversation with the Deep Lothark happens over the course of a day, with multiple Soft Lothark coming in shifts and picking up the thread. The Deep Lothark's self-description is explicit:

> "Only a few moieties are older than the Lothark. When the Carryx found us, we were smaller and we lived on a single world. The High Lothark and its Stone-Mind guided and bound us. And then the Carryx came. They hadn't mastered the Void Dragons then. Or the Amanthea. They didn't have knowledge then to lock a sky in place. They overcame us with Dirhaj which became obsolete when the Bau-hund were domesticated. There are no Dirhaj now."
> — TFOB Ch 23

And then, to Dafyd's question *What is the Deep Lothark?*:

> "A way of keeping something for ourselves. It is memory that belongs to all of us, and because we wear it on our skins, they don't find it inside our bodies. The Soft Lothark are loyal to the Carryx. They are the trusted animals of violence in the endless war. They would gladly die for the Carryx. The Deep Lothark is loyal to the Lothark, and it remembers how the Stone-Mind was built and waits to remake it again someday.
>
> It has been a very long wait, but the Deep Lothark is all Lothark, so it can be patient when the individual cannot."
> — TFOB Ch 23

The architecture is clear enough:

- **The Lothark were an independent civilization**, older than most of the Carryx's client moieties, with their own governing structure: a **High Lothark** bound to a device or institution called the **Stone-Mind**.
- The Carryx conquered them with a now-obsolete weapon species called **Dirhaj**, superseded by later conquests.
- After the conquest the Lothark split into two phenotypes or two layers of the same phenotype:
  - **Soft Lothark** — fully loyal to the Carryx, would "gladly die" for them. Tonner's biology confirms this: they really do serve the empire. The loyalty is not feigned.
  - **Deep Lothark** — a species-wide distributed memory, encoded on the skin rather than in the germline or central nervous system, passed through the nibbling greeting and the consumption of corpses. It remembers the High Lothark and the Stone-Mind. It is patient because it is not any one individual — it is the species remembering itself. The Soft Lothark may not know it's there. ("He wasn't at all certain the Soft Lothark understood what the Deep Lothark was or what it was saying. To them, the conversations might be nothing more than flailing at the glowing shapes, unconscious of any reason for the actions they took. Some of them might not know how to read." — TFOB Ch 32.)

On the war and its own situation the Deep Lothark is also unsparingly clear:

> "There is no war with the Carryx. War is what they are. Enslavement is what they are. A state with no conflict is incomprehensible. Before the deathless enemy arrived, there was war. When the deathless die, there will be war. But the deathless never die. War will never end. We will be here in its mouth forever."
>
> "We are on every world that our bodies can be made to tolerate. Over half of the Carryx worlds and colonies, we aid in holding. But if they chose to kill us, we would die. The dream of insurrection is a dream. These things that own us are capable of such casual violence that on a word, they would end us all and forget us before the next day. The Deep Lothark guards against that word, that ending, and it waits."
> — TFOB Ch 23

The Deep Lothark is, by its own admission, **not itself a rebellion.** It is a preservation project. It survives by doing nothing that can be detected. It has no offensive capability. What it has is **time** — it has been in place on at least half the Carryx's conquered worlds for millennia, picking up every overheard report, every Soft Lothark's observations of Carryx weakness, and quietly accumulating understanding. It is waiting for an external lever it does not have.

## IV. The Sovran, Decoded (Ch 23)

When Dafyd asks *What do you know about the Sovran?* the Deep Lothark gives him his first working theory of Carryx political biology:

> *"I know this. There is only one Sovran."*
>
> "The will of the Sovran is what makes the Carryx a single thing, one being in a thousand billion bodies. It was the great leap that lifted them from the tide pools and salt marshes of their original world. We have studied for centuries to know this… Then you will know that the different selves vie for food, for territory. The Carryx were once like this. But then, back when their bodies were smaller, when they didn't yet dream of stars or worlds or the deep things that live in the void between them, a Sovran rose, brought all moieties into one, and the petty struggles of one against another became something else. The little wars between groups became the grand war of the Carryx expansion into the universe. They press out the bounds of their territory because that is what they are, what they have always been. What they find, they use or they destroy. Anything else is unthinkable for them. Not taboo, not known but forbidden. Unthinkable. It cannot be conceived. They are what they are. They do what they do. What is, is."
> — TFOB Ch 23

This is the theoretical foundation. The Sovran is the unifier — the political-biological fact that converts intra-species competition into interstellar expansion. **Take the Sovran away and, on this model, the Carryx revert to tide-pool behavior: they fight each other.** That is the hypothesis Dafyd and the conspiracy have been operating under ever since. Kill the Sovran, the species fragments, the empire collapses, and whatever remains of humanity (or the Deep Lothark) fills the gap.

The Ch 23 conversation closes with the Deep Lothark giving the conspiracy a testable strategic claim. The Ch 37 conversation will tell Dafyd that the claim is wrong.

## V. Operating Logistics (Ch 32)

Before the end-of-book reveal, Corey gives one more chapter of ambient texture: in Ch 32, Dafyd describes how he now works with the Deep Lothark on a regular basis.

> "For Dafyd, the strangest thing about conferring with the Deep Lothark was the variety of bodies that it arrived in. When he had first encountered the species, Soft Lothark had been indistinguishable from one another: long limbs, thick bodies, greasy pelts. He wasn't sure when he'd started being able to tell one individual from the others, but it had been right around when they'd been assigned as guards. Now, each new session was like meeting a stranger for the first time and picking up the thread of an old conversation."
> — TFOB Ch 32

The Deep Lothark is a *distributed* interlocutor. Dafyd composes a question; the current Soft Lothark considers it; it leaves; hours later a *different* Soft Lothark — sometimes several — arrives and edits his symbols. The continuity is in the species' shared skin-borne memory, not in the individual courier. An exchange from the same chapter:

> Dafyd changed the glowing Carryx shape-script to read something like **Can the Deep Lothark command Soft Lothark against the will of the Carryx?**
>
> The pale alien considered the question, gnawed for a moment on the end of a long finger, then shifted the symbols quickly, efficiently to a new set that took Dafyd almost a minute to decipher.
>
> **To do so would invite scrutiny, and so the Deep Lothark both can and cannot.**
> — TFOB Ch 32

This is the operational reality of their alliance. The Deep Lothark has enormous informational reach and essentially zero executive authority. It can know; it cannot yet act.

## VI. The End-of-Book Revelation (Ch 37)

The novel's final Deep Lothark conversation is the one that matters most. Dafyd, having just witnessed a Sovran die and be replaced (Ch 36) and having confirmed through Clae that the archive also records earlier such transitions, walks out into the garden and composes a question that is no longer abstract. The scene is careful about protocol — Dafyd himself has learned to draft in the glyph system without "impressing" the symbols into the Carryx archive, so the Q&A is private:

> The Soft Lothark that sat across from him was large for its species, with markings in the thin, scraggly pelt around its eyes that made it look sorrowful. As Dafyd composed his question, it looked around the garden like a bored child in church. Occasionally, it nibbled at the back of its hand or the front of its arm.
>
> When Dafyd sat back, it considered the glowing object, coughed wetly, then rose and walked away. A few minutes later, a different Soft Lothark ambled by and shifted two of the symbols before leaving. A few minutes after that, a third guard came to him and added a second run of virtual objects that commented on and expanded the first. The conversation went on like this for the better part of a day.
> — TFOB Ch 37

### Question 1 — *What happened to the Sovran when the first world died?*

> "You are asking about the Epikainot and the great loss. Yes, many Soft Lothark were there when it happened. The Epikainot were a powerful enemy. Clever and ruthless. The Carryx had found them only a few hundred years before, and their battles rivaled the conflict with the deathless, though they were not deathless themselves.
>
> The end came with no warning. Those who were within the world-palace did not escape it. The Epikainot had discovered an interaction between field effects, a manner in which the changes made by one innocuous beam would amplify and resonate with the effects of another, so that either one alone was beneath notice, together they would feed forward in a devastating torrent. In the course of an hour, the crust of that world dissolved. Towers and seas and the ancestral pools of the Carryx all vanished in fire and boiling stone. When the Epikainot were finally eradicated, the weapon was taken. The Carryx use a version of it now when they wish to unmake a world utterly. It is still very effective against opponents who do not expect it.
>
> The Sovran died as did her librarians. Some part of the archive was lost, though much of it remained on the other worlds. Only those messages that had not yet been sent outside the planet were lost utterly. The moieties that were exclusive to that world became extinct. There were other animals elsewhere. The Carryx are resilient and robust. They did not mourn their dead world. What is, is. Instead they turned their full attention to the Epikainot and ended that species and everything related to it."
> — TFOB Ch 37

### Question 2 — *How do the Carryx recover when a Sovran dies?*

> "The Sovran is born in the private creche, built and designed and selected there for perfection of function. There is always one at the ready should an accident befall the empire. When a promising new daughter is born, she is sent out to meet her mother, and the survivor of that meeting carries on. Age and time weaken all things, but not the Sovran. Before the entropy of life can weather her, she is dead.
>
> There are always blank eggs at the ready to prepare another if the need arises.
>
> There is a private creche on all the world-palaces. Hundreds, each in its order.
>
> When the Epikainot destroyed the first world and the voice of the Sovran went silent, the creche on this world opened, and the Sovran emerged. The librarians here became the librarians that had been. The empire shifted its attention a few degrees, and all carried on as it had.
>
> This is the resilience of the Carryx that no other has ever matched. Each can become the tool that is needed. None are valued because none are valuable.
>
> We have waited for millennia to see the empire fall, but it rises from every stumble. It wears down every enemy. Other beings feel loss or sorrow, other civilizations grow sick from war, but war is what the Carryx are. They will never mourn their dead, they rejoice that the weak give way to the strong. They will never grow tired of violence. They will never be other than what they are.
>
> For them, conflict ends when the last stars burn out and all life dies.
>
> Not before."
> — TFOB Ch 37

## VII. Analysis of What the Deep Lothark Actually Tells Dafyd

Unpacked, the Ch 37 answers contain seven separate strategic facts, each of which is load-bearing:

1. **The Sovran is not unique.** She is the current instance of a line-replaced office. There is always another one ready. The Ch 23 theory — that removing the Sovran dissolves the Carryx unity — survives only as a local effect; it does not propagate beyond the first death.

2. **Succession is by ritual intra-line combat.** "When a promising new daughter is born, she is sent out to meet her mother, and the survivor of that meeting carries on." This is the same mother-daughter dueling succession protocol the Surur-Tlassen POV chapters have shown us; it is confirmed here as routine, engineered, and continuous. The Carryx do not wait for a Sovran to die naturally — they kill her before she can weaken.

3. **Replacement is distributed across every world-palace.** "There is a private creche on all the world-palaces. Hundreds, each in its order." This is the decisive geometric fact. Even if Dafyd's conspiracy, working with the deathless enemy, could coordinate a simultaneous assassination in this one world-palace, there are hundreds of other worlds each holding at least one blank egg. The "decapitation" strategy requires *synchronized* strikes on hundreds of heavily fortified planetary capitals.

4. **The empire has already survived its own homeworld's destruction.** The **Epikainot** — an enemy the Carryx rate as rivaling the current deathless war — destroyed the original Carryx homeworld, killing the Sovran and her immediate librarians. The empire absorbed the loss. "The empire shifted its attention a few degrees, and all carried on as it had." There is a pre-existing historical experiment for what happens when you take out a Carryx capital, and the answer is *nothing durable*. The Epikainot were, for their trouble, exterminated down to the last adjacent species.

5. **The Carryx now have the Epikainot weapon.** The crust-dissolving resonant field that ended the first homeworld is now in Carryx hands. "The Carryx use a version of it now when they wish to unmake a world utterly. It is still very effective against opponents who do not expect it." This is the planet-killer the Carryx used against Rickar's or Campar's ship, and against worlds like the one Jessyn surveys in Chapter 10. It is why the Carryx scorched Jessyn's planet to prevent the enemy from evacuating.

6. **The fungibility is total.** "Each can become the tool that is needed. None are valued because none are valuable." This is the Carryx civic philosophy reduced to one sentence. The individual body — including the body of the Sovran — is an instrument. Killing any one of them removes nothing.

7. **The Deep Lothark's own time horizon.** "We have waited for millennia to see the empire fall." This is the Deep Lothark telling Dafyd, politely, that the conspiracy's assumed timeline is wrong. The species that has had the most time and the deepest surveillance access of any conquered civilization has already concluded that it cannot bring the Carryx down alone — and it has waited thousands of years for someone else to show up.

Together these facts eliminate the conspiracy's core premise. Dafyd and the swarm spent the middle of the book assuming that if they could kill the Sovran — or help the deathless enemy do so — the war ends. Ch 37 closes that path. The Sovran is a replaceable part.

## VIII. What This Information Does to Dafyd's Plan

Dafyd gathers the surviving conspirators (Jessyn, Campar, Uuya Tomos) and briefs them on what he has learned — Deep Lothark existence, the dead homeworld, the creche system:

> "They gathered in Campar's quarters. Dafyd, Jessyn, and Uuya Tomos. The only conspirators besides the spy who were still alive. … He told them what he'd planned, and he told them what he'd learned. What he suspected. He told them about the Deep Lothark and the dead homeworld. He didn't tell them about the spy or Jellit or Else."
> — TFOB Ch 37

Jessyn's first instinct is to scale up: find every world with a creche and hit them all at once.

> "If there were a way to locate all the planets that had these private creches, and get that information to Garral? Is there a version of your plan that could still work? I mean, I see that it would have been simpler if we could just do the one thing here, but taking out the whole Sovran class at once… I mean, it seems like that might actually work."
> — TFOB Ch 37

Campar immediately refutes it on operational grounds — coordinated strikes against hundreds of fortified Carryx throne-worlds would require a deathless fleet vastly more capable than the one whose battles he and Rickar witnessed.

Uuya Tomos then reframes the whole horizon, naming what the Deep Lothark already is:

> "She's right. We may be playing a very long game. It may be a game we leave to the great-grandchildren of our great-grandchildren."
>
> "The Deep Lothark has been playing that game for millennia," Dafyd agreed. "Maybe thousands of generations."
> — TFOB Ch 37

This is what the Deep Lothark's intel has actually given Dafyd: not a path to victory but a corrected sense of the problem. The conspiracy is now explicitly a **multigenerational** project. Uuya's wisdom-myth theology becomes the operating doctrine: *"It's never your responsibility to do something that can't be done. You do your part, and you help the next generation carry it a little farther, and then the one after that."* The Deep Lothark's millennia are the template.

### The closing insight — *"What if there were two?"*

The book does not leave Dafyd entirely stuck. Its final line of strategic thinking, in the very last pages of Ch 37, seizes on one implication of the creche system the Deep Lothark has laid out:

> "Well," Dafyd said. "What if there were two?"
> — TFOB Ch 37 (closing beat)

If the Carryx unity is produced by one Sovran, and every world-palace is preloaded with a ready replacement in a blank egg, then the intervention that might actually break the empire is not to *kill* a Sovran but to cause **two simultaneous Sovrans** — to light off multiple creches at once so that the mother-daughter succession ritual happens in parallel on different worlds. Two Sovrans cannot share the empire. The reflexes the Deep Lothark described — "the different selves vie for food, for territory" — would reassert themselves. The Carryx's own fungibility protocol, turned against itself, becomes the civil war that seven thousand years of Epikainot-survived continuity could not produce.

The book ends with this idea intact, not executed. It is the seed from which TFOB's sequel is presumably grown.

## IX. What the Deep Lothark Wants

Corey is careful to keep the Deep Lothark's ask implicit. It never makes demands of Dafyd. It does not ask for resources. It does not attempt to recruit humans into an armed rising. What it offers, and accepts, is an information exchange: Dafyd's questions for its observations. Its operational posture — *"The dream of insurrection is a dream"* — is unchanged from Ch 23 to Ch 37.

But what it *is* is a species that has spent millennia watching for the single external catalyst the Lothark cannot be, because it is too compromised, too present, too biologically fused to the empire's nervous system. Reading the novel's arc, the clearest read of the Deep Lothark's interest in Dafyd is this: **humans are the first animal-caste the Carryx have promoted to cross-world deployment since the Lothark themselves were broken.** Humans are about to be seeded on a thousand worlds. If a counter-memory can be encoded in a species before it is scattered — a curriculum, a myth, a conspiracy — the Deep Lothark may finally have, on a hundred thousand worlds, the ally it has never had on any.

This is the strategic frame that makes Uuya Tomos's quiet work on the *Myths of Origin* Ke cycle, Dafyd's lamb-sack breeding of a new human generation, the field-agent roster Ekur has just commissioned, and the Deep Lothark's millennia of patience all converge into a single project at the end of Book 2. The Deep Lothark has not given Dafyd a plan. It has given him the correct time scale, the confirmed impossibility of the quick plan he had, and the germ of a second one — and it has shown him, by its own continued existence, that a conquered species can survive inside the empire's own nervous system for three thousand years without being detected, if it is willing to be patient and to never be seen to act.

---

## X. Bottom Line

The Deep Lothark is the book's single most important plot revelation outside of Jessyn's contact with the deathless enemy, and the two discoveries interlock. Together they tell Dafyd: the Carryx are structurally unkillable by any single strike, the deathless are not going to win quickly, the only resistance movement older than humanity's captivity has concluded that it can only preserve, not liberate, and the whole fight is going to be measured in generations.

The Deep Lothark's Ch 37 briefing is **the book's closing strategic fact** — it reframes every earlier conspiracy thread (the archive raid, the Sovran assassination plan, the field-agent seeding, Uuya's curriculum) as preparation for a multigenerational campaign that Dafyd will not live to finish. Every later installment of *The Captive's War* has to begin inside the frame the Deep Lothark drew on that Ch 37 afternoon in a Carryx garden, with a sorrowful-looking guard gnawing on its own arm.
