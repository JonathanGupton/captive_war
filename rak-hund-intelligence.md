# Rak-hund Intelligence & Communication — Findings

Consolidated evidence from *The Captives War 1: The Mercy of Gods* (MOG) and *The Captives War 2: The Faith of Beasts* (TFOB) by James S.A. Corey, bearing on whether the rak-hund are sentient, whether they can communicate, and whether they can be communicated with.

---

## Summary Verdict

MOG portrays the rak-hund as **engineered biological weapons**, not sentient peers. They possess enough cognition for coordinated combat and instant obedience to Carryx signals, but no passage in MOG shows them exercising independent judgment, producing language, or responding to any species other than the Carryx. The Carryx themselves state plainly that rak-hund compliance is the result of selectively killing every variant that deviated from obedience.

TFOB does not overturn that picture but **softens and complicates it**. When one rak-hund is reassigned as personal bodyguard to Dafyd for an extended span of chapters, the narrative begins to describe it in dog-like emotional terms — apparent preference, apparent attachment, unease in the presence of threats, reluctance to lose sight of its charge. The picture shifts from "pure weapon" toward "engineered working-animal with canine-grade inner life." Still no language, still no negotiation, still no independent agenda of its own.

- **Sentient?** No evidence of sapience in either book — no self-awareness, individuality, abstract thought, or culture. MOG depicts them as weapons; TFOB adds signs of emotion and attachment consistent with a dog, not a person.
- **Can they communicate?** With the Carryx/Soft Lothark, yes — one direction humans can perceive (command → compliance), and possibly a return channel outside human sensory range. Among themselves, clicking footfalls are noted in TFOB as "too low for human ears, but not for it," hinting at an intra-species signaling channel we don't get to read. No rak-hund-to-rak-hund dialogue is shown on the page.
- **Can they be communicated with?** By Carryx/Soft Lothark, yes — instantly and unconditionally. By humans, no. No human in either book successfully addresses a rak-hund or receives a response, though TFOB shows a rak-hund responding to a human's *presence* and *emotional state* in nonverbal ways.

## Primary Evidence: The Carryx Epigraph

> "The Rak-hund execute our will. If there is any because in that, it is because we killed or sterilized all aspects of the Rak-hund that didn't, and we kept the remnant that did."
> — MOG Part Two epigraph (preceding MOG Chapter 7)

This is the single most decisive passage in the book. The Carryx describe rak-hund obedience as a *bred trait*, produced by eliminating every lineage that showed resistance or independence. Whatever the rak-hund once were, the species that exists in the narrative has been filtered to a behavioral remnant. This frames every later observation: rak-hund behavior is the *designed output* of Carryx husbandry, not the natural cognition of a wild species.

## Evidence That Rak-hund Are NOT Sentient

### Described in purely animal terms
- "The Rak-hund were woken to eat and shit and beg for approval as was their nature." (MOG Chapter 7)
- "Long servants of the Carryx, and loyal as a dog." (MOG Chapter 24)
- Grouped with "animals of violence" and other Carryx tool-species throughout.

### No individuation
- Never named. Always referred to collectively ("the Rak-hund," "a Rak-hund," "a dozen Rak-hund").
- No rak-hund is shown displaying preferences, memory across scenes, or a distinct personality.
- No rak-hund is ever shown hesitating, dissenting, deliberating, or negotiating.

### No participation in planning
- In MOG Chapter 9, when a group of Carryx subordinates must decide how to handle a prisoner, the **Soft Lothark confer among themselves**; the rak-hund simply wait for the order and then execute (literally — one "stomped him to death with its knife legs"). Rak-hund are present as instruments, not participants.

### Deployed as equipment
- "Ranks of Rak-hund and Soft Lothark prepared to fall through the high air of the world, bringing the word of the Carryx…" (MOG Chapter 7) — they are part of a deployment, referenced in the same grammatical category as weapon platforms.

## Evidence of Cognition (but not sentience)

These passages show the rak-hund are *more than mindless*, which is consistent with trained working-animal intelligence:

- **Recognize and obey Carryx vocalizations directly**, without translator boxes. In MOG Chapter 9, the Carryx librarian produces its meat-slap fluting sounds and "four Rak-hund turned and walked to it."
- **Impulse control / timed engagement.** "The pale thousand-legged beasts shuddered and surged, but they didn't move forward. Not yet." (MOG Chapter 9) — they hold until released.
- **Tactical formation.** "A dozen Rak-hund boiled forward, making a barrier between the transports and the huge green-gold carapaced one — the Carryx — with their bodies." (MOG Chapters 9 / 23)
- **Modulated violence.** In the Dennia killing (MOG Chapters 29 / 33), a rak-hund first *pushes* a human "like a bully on a playground, fighting over a toy," then, once escalated, executes a coordinated multi-leg stabbing strike. It can calibrate force.
- **Damage response and disengagement.** After being shot in an eye-like organ, the same rak-hund rears back, continues attacking, then breaks off and withdraws into the watching crowd — suggesting either pain response plus unseen Carryx recall, or conditioned mission-abort behavior.
- **Intimidation display.** "The bent-knife legs rippled without moving them, like a man flexing his hands before a fight." (MOG Chapter 33) — pre-combat posturing, which requires some model of threat display.
- **Avoidance in uncommanded encounters.** "A Rak-hund undulated into their path and then turned away." (MOG Chapters 16, 20) — they route around humans when no attack is commanded.
- **Controlled handling of captives.** In MOG Chapter 34 they gently deposit prisoners ("Another dropped Tonner beside them before returning to their place among the guard"), demonstrating that the same knife-legged anatomy used for killing can execute careful placement on command.

All of this is consistent with a highly trained, domesticated predator — dog-like, as the text itself says — not with a sapient being.

## Communication

### Carryx → Rak-hund (clear, one-way, effective)
- Rak-hund act on Carryx vocal commands with no observed delay or error.
- They understand at least: "hold," "attack," "form barrier," "escort," "release the prisoner," "place this body here."
- The Carryx can apparently **stop an attack mid-strike** — the Dennia-killer disengages and departs while the crowd watches, implying an off-page signal.

### Rak-hund → Carryx (possible, but outside human perception)
- The one passage suggesting a reply channel notes that "the Rak-hund's reply, if there was one, wasn't in a register human senses could detect."
- No definitive confirmation that rak-hund actually *send* information back; the text leaves this ambiguous.

### Rak-hund → Rak-hund (essentially unattested)
- They coordinate in groups (barriers, surges, flanking) but no dialogue, no ritualized signals, and no named intra-pack communication behavior is ever shown.
- One wounded rak-hund "chittered to itself" (MOG Chapter 29) — explicitly to itself, not to another rak-hund. Read as a distress vocalization, not speech.

### Humans → Rak-hund (fails completely)
- No human ever successfully addresses a rak-hund. Pleas, shouts, and warnings during attacks are ignored.
- Humans use translator boxes to speak with Carryx and with Sinen (Night Drinkers), but never attempt — or succeed — at dialogue with a rak-hund.
- The only human-rak-hund "exchange" on the page is Dennia pressing a gun to a rak-hund's sensory organ and firing; the rak-hund's response is to keep attacking despite the wound.

## Physical & Behavioral Traits Relevant to the Question

- **Form:** pale, centipede-like, many-legged; legs are knife / bone-blade shaped and function as weapons.
- **Scale:** sized as large soldiers; in one description, creatures of roughly horse size move among them in the cathedral.
- **Locomotion:** undulating, silent when they choose ("bone-knife feet hushing against the floor," MOG Chapters 27 / 32), noisy when they don't ("footsteps that clicked like hail falling on slate tiles," MOG Chapter 9).
- **Sensory organs:** at least one eye-like structure (destroyed by Dennia's gunshot).
- **Vocalization:** chittering noted once, in a wounded individual, to itself.
- **Role:** Carryx shock troops and prison guards. Paired operationally with Soft Lothark, who handle the *decisions*, while rak-hund handle the *violence*.

## Evidence from *The Faith of Beasts* (Book 2)

TFOB substantially expands the rak-hund portrait. A single individual — assigned as Dafyd's personal bodyguard after the Carryx identify him as politically significant — becomes a recurring, almost-named presence across many chapters. It is seen lying around, reacting to threats, following Dafyd, and eventually dying in a separate incident involving another rak-hund guarding a Sinen. The new material pushes in one direction: toward dog-like inner life, not toward language or sapience.

### The bodyguard framing (TFOB Chapter 7)

When the Soft Lothark assign the rak-hund to Dafyd, they describe its function in tool-like terms that reaffirm MOG:

> "We are agents of appropriate violence. It is our role and place in society. The Rak-hund will keep you from unwelcome violence and kill anything of lesser status that you indicate."
> — TFOB Chapter 7

Two things to note. First, the rak-hund is described as a subordinate implement *of* the Soft Lothark's "moiety of appropriate violence," not as a peer. Second, Dafyd is told it will **kill on his gesture** — confirming that rak-hund can take targeting input from a designated human, but only when that human has been ritually authorized by the Carryx hierarchy. This is the first time in either book a human is given operational command over a rak-hund, and even here it's a narrow kill-what-I-point-at channel, not dialogue.

### Sensory range wider than humans'

> "A noise, a scent, a presentiment of something dangerous. It turns toward the archway, the gentle clicking footfalls too low for human ears, but not for it."
> — TFOB Chapter 7

Two relevant facts: (1) rak-hund hear infrasonic or sub-audible clicks that humans cannot; (2) their own footfalls emit those clicks. This is the strongest textual hint so far that rak-hund have an intra-species acoustic channel. The text does not confirm it is *language*, but it is at least a signaling medium invisible to humans.

### Apparent preference and attachment (TFOB Chapter 9)

The pivotal passage — and the one most at odds with MOG's "tool" framing:

> "The Rak-hund bodyguard lay under the archway back into the building, its pale legs arrayed around it like a spiral of swords. It seemed to enjoy the cool tile floor against its belly, a behavior Dafyd had seen in dogs back on Anjiin. And like dogs, it didn't seem to like leaving Dafyd out of its sight for too long."
> — TFOB Chapter 9

In MOG, rak-hund are never described as enjoying, preferring, or missing anything. Here, Dafyd — a careful observer — explicitly reads two dog-like states in the creature: a sensory preference (the cool floor) and a proximity bond (dislike of separation). The "seemed to" hedging is worth noting: this is Dafyd's inference, not the narrator's guarantee. But the hedging is standard for animal cognition and does not undercut the new picture.

### Threat response in a non-combat context (TFOB Chapter 9)

> "Far behind her, the Rak-hund shifted uneasily like it was smelling a threat."
> — TFOB Chapter 9

Again a hedged observation ("like it was"), but the behavior described — olfactory threat assessment and a visible unease response without any Carryx command in the scene — is consistent with an animal that tracks its handler's safety autonomously, not one that waits inert for orders.

### Humans still don't know what rak-hund understand (TFOB Chapter 15)

> "He glanced at the Rak-hund, but it was too far to hear them. And hopefully not sophisticated enough to understand them if it did."
> — TFOB Chapter 15

Dafyd, the character with by far the most exposure to a rak-hund at close range, still *doesn't know* whether it understands human speech. He hopes not. This is notable: if the rak-hund were demonstrably non-linguistic, Dafyd wouldn't need to hope. The book leaves the comprehension question deliberately ambiguous.

### Continued dog-language framing (TFOB Chapter 20)

> "The Rak-hund, waiting outside the door for him like a loyal dog, followed after him. The clicking of its knife-feet against the floor still made Dafyd's flesh crawl."
> — TFOB Chapter 20

The "loyal dog" simile is now the default register for how the narrative describes Dafyd's bodyguard. Dafyd's own reaction — flesh crawling despite the loyalty — captures the moral awkwardness TFOB introduces: a weapon that is also, maybe, a companion.

### Independent combat initiative (TFOB Chapter 31)

When Jessyn needs to kill a Sinen ("Third Gardener") in an orchard, the rak-hund guarding it reacts on its own:

> "She shot the Rak-hund first, walking forward as she did. The first shot went wide, but then it charged her, and as the distance between them grew smaller, her aim improved. The second shot shattered one of its leading legs, and the third, fourth, and fifth pulped its head."
> — TFOB Chapter 31

No Carryx is present. No command is given. The rak-hund assesses the threat to its assigned Sinen and charges on its own initiative — and continues attacking after the first bullet misses. This is the clearest in-book evidence that rak-hund act autonomously in defense of their charge when no handler is in earshot. It's also the clearest confirmation that they can be killed with conventional human small arms, though at cost.

### What TFOB does NOT show

Despite all of the above, TFOB still shows:

- **No rak-hund speech**, in any language, to any species, ever.
- **No named individual rak-hund** — Dafyd's bodyguard is on-page for dozens of chapters and is never given a name, a nickname, or a moment of individual identity.
- **No rak-hund POV**, even glancingly.
- **No rak-hund resistance to orders.** The Jessyn scene is defense of a Sinen charge, not independent goal-pursuit.
- **No successful human-rak-hund communication.** Dafyd never speaks to his bodyguard, and no human in the book tries.
- **No rak-hund-to-rak-hund dialogue on-page**, though the sub-audible click channel is now confirmed to exist.

---

## Bottom Line for Future Reference

Across both books, the rak-hund remain **engineered working animals** rather than sapient persons. MOG depicts them as pure Carryx weapons; TFOB refines that to something more like trained war dogs — capable of preference, attachment, olfactory threat assessment, and autonomous defensive action, but still non-linguistic, still unnamed, still without any on-page interior life beyond what Dafyd infers by analogy to dogs.

If a later book asks whether characters could reason with, recruit, or negotiate with a rak-hund: **nothing in MOG or TFOB supports that possibility.** What TFOB *does* open up is the possibility of **bonding** with one — a handler relationship, loyalty conditioned on proximity and assignment, communication through gesture and command. That is a real shift from MOG's "carpet of knives" framing, and worth tracking as the series continues. A rak-hund POV, a rak-hund refusing an order, or a rak-hund communicating in a human-intelligible way would all still be **genuine revelations** beyond anything the text has so far committed to.
