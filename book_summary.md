# The Mercy of Gods -- Complete Book Summary

**By James S.A. Corey (Daniel Abraham & Ty Franck)**
*The Captive's War, Book 1 (2024)*

---

## PART ONE: BEFORE

### Part One Epigraph

> "You ask how many ages had the Carryx been fighting the long war? That is a meaningless question. The Carryx ruled the stars for epochs. We conquered the Ejia and Kurkst and outdreamt the Eyeless Ones. We burned the Logothetes until their worlds were windswept glass... We brought fire, death, and chains to Anjiin. We took from it what we deemed useful to us and culled those who resisted. And in that is our regret. If we had left it alone, nothing that came after would have been as it was. If we had burned it to ash and moved on as we had done to so many other worlds, I would not now be telling you the chronicle of our failure. We did not see the adversary for what he was, and we brought him into our home."
> -- From the final statement of Ekur-Tkalal, keeper-librarian of the human moiety of the Carryx

### Part One Summary

Part One establishes a world on the brink of annihilation that does not know it is on the brink. The human population of Anjiin -- itself an alien species on a planet they colonized thousands of years ago under forgotten circumstances -- is consumed by the petty politics of academic status competition. Tonner Freis's research team has achieved a genuine scientific breakthrough in bridging the planet's two incompatible biologies, but their triumph immediately makes them targets for institutional power grabs. The characters are drawn with sharp specificity: Dafyd the charming manipulator who hides behind listening; Tonner the brilliant egotist whose identity is fused to his work; Else the rationalist who sees further than anyone; Jessyn who fights her own mind as fiercely as any external threat; and the older Nol and Synnia, who just want to tend their garden.

Running beneath the human drama is the thread of the swarm -- an alien intelligence that has already infiltrated Anjiin, inhabiting human hosts, studying the population with inhuman precision, and operating under urgent time pressure. The swarm's interlude sections create dramatic irony: the humans are fretting about who will control a research lab while something vastly more consequential is already among them. Meanwhile, Jellit's astronomical data reveals seventeen city-sized objects approaching the planet -- the arrival that the epigraph's Carryx narrator describes as bringing "fire, death, and chains."

The part's arc moves from celebration to crisis to cataclysm, compressing from the slow tempo of academic intrigue into the sudden violence of first contact. The final image -- a kiss between two people interrupted by the sound of a world ending -- crystallizes the part's central irony: everything the characters fought to protect was already irrelevant.

> "Small moments, unnoticed at the time, change the fate of empires."

> "A month is all the lifetime many of the people on this world have left."

---

### Chapter 1

The novel opens with a proleptic reflection from Dafyd Alkhor, the protagonist, who will later look back and marvel at how many critical choices in his life seemed small at the time. At the end-of-year celebration at the Scholar's Common in the city of Irvian on the planet Anjiin, Dafyd finds himself cornered in conversation with Llaren Morse, a near-field astronomer from Dyan Academy who is studying an anomalous zone at the edge of the heliosphere. Dafyd deploys his signature social skill -- reflective listening -- drawing Morse out while revealing nothing about himself.

The conversation takes a darker turn when Tonner Freis, the celebrated lead researcher of Dafyd's workgroup, arrives in the courtyard below to applause. Morse mutters "Enjoy it now" with barely concealed contempt, a small remark that triggers Dafyd's suspicion that something political is afoot. Dafyd navigates the elaborate social hierarchies of the celebration, ascending through levels of increasingly powerful attendees. The Common itself is described as a structure grown from forest coral, a living building that embodies the medrey system -- Anjiin's network of research institutions.

Dafyd finds his aunt, Dorinda Alkhor, a powerful figure in the funding colloquy. Their exchange reveals a complex relationship: she is his guardian (his mother is dead), his political patron, and his sharpest critic. She sees through his self-effacing charm and warns him it's a vice. When Dafyd probes her about a potential threat to Tonner's workgroup, her reaction -- a mirthless poker-player's smile -- confirms his fears.

The chapter ends with Dafyd approaching Else Yannin, the woman he is infatuated with, who is also Tonner Freis's acknowledged lover and the group's second-in-command. He tells her with certainty that someone is making a play to take over the research. As she places her hand on his arm, the narrator observes that this small moment would later be connected to a much larger catastrophe.

> "Later, at the end of things, Dafyd would be amazed at how many of the critical choices in his life seemed small at the time. How many overwhelming problems had, with the distance of time, proved trivial."

> "He listened because he was good at listening. He had a lot of practice. It kept the spotlight off him, people broadly seemed more hungry to be heard than they knew, and usually by the end of it, they found themselves liking him."

> "Later, when he stood in the eye of a storm that burned a thousand worlds, he'd remember how it all started with Else Yannin's hand on his arm and his need to give her a reason to keep it there."

---

### Chapter 2

The chapter opens with a brief history of humanity's presence on Anjiin. Humans appeared in the fossil record approximately 3,500 years ago, arriving by unknown means along with an entire Earth-origin biome -- dogs, cows, lettuce, bees, mushrooms, snails -- all based on DNA, which is fundamentally incompatible with Anjiin's native life built on aperiodic quasicrystals of silicon, carbon, and iodine. Whatever records the original settlers brought were destroyed when something turned most of their island of origin into glass and black rock. Over millennia, the two trees of life spread across the planet but could never truly nourish each other at a biochemical level -- until Tonner Freis's team found a way to translate between them, achieving the first successful proteome reconciliation.

The perspective shifts to Jessyn, one of the team's junior researchers, who is at the plaza outside the Scholar's Common with teammates Irinna, Nol, and Synnia. Through Jessyn's eyes, we see the team's close-knit family dynamic. She treasures the memory of the moment when the first uptake results came through -- the hinge on which the world swung. Jessyn and Irinna dance in the plaza. There is warmth and gratitude between them. Jessyn walks home through the night feeling, for once, satisfied and free from the "dark things in the back of her mind" -- a first hint at her struggles with mental illness. She lives with her brother Jellit, who works in astronomical imaging.

Just as she's savoring the rare feeling of contentment, a message arrives from Tonner Freis, his face showing unmistakable rage, summoning her to an emergency meeting and demanding secrecy.

> "Their strange, awkward, haphazard little group had translated one tree of life to the other. Two utterly incompatible methods of heritability had been coaxed into sitting beside one another, working together. That little blade of grass had been the product of a biochemical marriage thousands of years in the making."

> "I am part of the most successful research team in the world. I am not too anxious to dance in public. My brain isn't betraying me today. Today is good."

---

### Chapter 3

Tonner calls a secret meeting of select team members -- Dafyd, Campar, Nol, Else, and Jessyn -- at the nearly deserted laboratories during the term break. Jessyn arrives with her brother Jellit, who mentions in passing that he's been studying a strange anomalous extrasolar phenomenon, one dataset from which appeared superluminal. He departs when he realizes it's a private team meeting, and this casual detail about the anomaly will prove far more consequential than anyone realizes.

Tonner reveals the crisis: someone has asked the funding colloquy to reassign their work. The plan would scatter the three primary scholars to new labs at other institutions. The group debates who could be behind it. Suspicion falls on Rickar Daumatin, whose father is a landgraf near Dyan Academy. Jessyn raises an uncomfortable question: what if splitting into four coordinating labs would actually be good for the project, even if bad for them individually? Tonner shuts her down. He assigns roles for a quiet political counteroffensive.

The chapter contains a critical interlude narrated from the perspective of "the swarm" -- an alien intelligence inhabiting the body of a woman named Ameer Kindred, who is "both dead and not dead." The swarm has been observing the team's meeting from nearby, disguised as a woman eating a sandwich. It extends millions of tiny needle-like antennae through its host's skin to sense pheromones, heartbeats, and social dynamics. The swarm knows its time is running short and that it must move to a new host.

> "The swarm is not capable of feeling fear, but it does now feel a heightened sense of pressure. It knows there is very little time left."

> "Ameer Kindred senses the swarm's intention. She knows her time as the host is coming to an end. And though she has screamed her hatred of the swarm's invasion of her body and thoughts every second of every day since it took over, Ameer also understands that the swarm will not leave her alive when it moves on. The thought of her own impending death is like an ocean of sorrow. This sadness is not currently useful to the swarm, so it files it away and then ignores it."

---

### Chapter 4

The full team confronts Rickar Daumatin, who admits his complicity with disarming casualness: "It's true." He claims the plan was initiated by Samar Austad, Dyan Academy's chief administrator, and that he simply went along with it as the pragmatic choice. Tonner is barely containing his rage. With Rickar expelled from the lab, the group strategizes. Dafyd counsels a subtle approach -- mapping Austad's allies and enemies to build a counterproposal. Else backs Dafyd over Tonner, a shift in power dynamics that doesn't go unnoticed.

The chapter's second half shifts to Tonner's private life. He and Else sit on the balcony of the apartment he chose specifically to please her. Their conversation reveals the depth of Tonner's identity crisis. He confesses his distrust of Dafyd -- "He's a schemer. Everything he does is for effect" -- while Else defends the young man. The chapter ends with Tonner falling asleep and dreaming of paving stones falling away beneath him into a vast pit.

> "This work is what I am. If I don't have it, I'm not sure what's left of me. I know that all the literature talks about reinvention and remaking your career every ten years, but I don't know if I have it in me to start over."

> "I think he's charming. And schemer or not, I've never seen him be cruel."

> "In his dream, he was trying to get across the square to his labs, but the paving stones were falling away into a vast pit. Every step was dangerous, and the ground collapsed behind him or where he'd been about to go as the world ate itself under him. Later, it would seem like a premonition."

---

### Chapter 5

The chapter opens with another swarm interlude. In its new host (distinct from Ameer Kindred, who is now gone), the swarm stands in the night sensing cosmic rays passing through the planet. It is learning to experience consciousness -- noticing artifacts of Ameer's personality persisting within it (a fondness for sweet pickles, a memory of a boy named Elial). The swarm reflects that "it has already traded purity for experience, and there is no path back." It knows time is short: "A month is all the lifetime many of the people on this world have left." The swarm approaches Samar Austad.

The narrative shifts to Jessyn and Irinna. Jessyn privately believes Tonner cannot save the workgroup. Their conversation reveals Jessyn's dependence on her brother Jellit, who serves as her caretaker during episodes of mental illness. Back at home, Jellit is buzzing with excitement about his astronomical data. The lensing effect that Llaren Morse had been studying now shows ordered, nonexotic matter -- probably titanium and carbon -- with structure that suggests design rather than natural formation. As Jessyn processes the enormity of this -- possible contact with an intelligence beyond their world -- the chapter pauses for a beautiful digression about her childhood priest who taught her that the universe's vastness could make personal suffering feel lighter.

The chapter ends abruptly: Campar calls Jessyn to warn her that Samar Austad has been killed.

> "The swarm is young in the universe. Consciousness is a mystery it is only beginning to experience."

> "It has already traded purity for experience, and there is no path back. Nor should there be."

> "Her life was a mess. Her career was a mess. Normal people would succeed where she couldn't because she'd been born a little broken. But there was also a world outside her head that was filled with mystery and exploration and unexpected things to discover. Looked at in a wide enough frame, maybe her problems weren't so large. They just seemed that way when she held them up against her eyes where they'd block out all the light."

---

### Chapter 6

Dafyd is being interrogated by security forces about the murder of Samar Austad. His inquiries about Austad's proposal to restructure the workgroup have made him a person of interest. The interrogation ends when his aunt, Dorinda Alkhor, collects him. She is distracted by urgent messages and cryptically warns him: "Things are happening. Some news is about to break." She drops him off on the street. The narrator states flatly: "He never saw her again."

The perspective shifts to Rickar Daumatin, released from his own questioning. When he re-enables his communication system, he is flooded with alerts about seventeen city-sized objects approaching Anjiin. He watches Llaren Morse describe the objects on a news clip. His career crisis evaporates into something between religious awe and terror.

Nol and Synnia watch events unfold from their home. The objects begin releasing smaller oblong spheroids heading toward Anjiin. Then the live feed begins looping, suggesting censorship.

The final section brings the threads together at the lab. Else notices the feed is being censored and suggests they go to her basement rooms. There, Dafyd and Else have their most intimate conversation. He explains the political dynamics; she asks why he's in research when he has the wrong kind of intelligence for it. He admits he's living his father's dream. As alien ships descend toward their planet, Dafyd begins an awkward attempt to confess his feelings. She kisses him. Something rumbles -- deep as thunder or an earthquake -- and alarms begin to shriek. Part One ends.

> "He never saw her again."

> "Maybe this was what religious awe felt like. Maybe it was only fear. Either way, he was done drinking for the day."

> "She pressed her mouth to his and it tasted like salt and smoke. Something rumbled, deep as thunder or an earthquake. The lights flickered. In the distance, alarms began to shriek."

## PART TWO: CATASTROPHE

### Part Two Epigraph

> "The word you use is *evil*. We have heard it many times. It and *good* are sloppy shorthand that lesser beings use when they are too small, too dim, or too frightened to look the universe in the face and accept the implacable truth that it tells every being from the greatest to the least... What you did with a tree branch, we did with you and countless others before you. *Why me?* is not something the universe ever answers."
> -- From the final statement of Ekur-Tkalal, keeper-librarian of the human moiety of the Carryx

### Part Two Summary

Part Two chronicles the Carryx invasion and conquest of Anjiin, the traumatic abduction of the planet's intellectual elite, and their weeks-long transport to an alien world. The narrative arc moves from the cold, clinical efficiency of the invasion (seen through the Carryx's own perspective in Chapter 7) through escalating human horror and degradation, to the first tentative steps toward understanding what the captors want.

A central theme is the danger of anthropomorphism -- of projecting human motivations onto fundamentally alien minds. Dafyd's realization, through the parable of Little Dot the water beetle, that the Carryx simply do not think in human categories of punishment, mercy, or anger becomes the intellectual foundation for everything that follows. The part also introduces a hidden third element: the "swarm," a parasitic spy intelligence disguised among the captives, which has its own agenda connected to an unnamed enemy the Carryx are fighting. The human characters are caught between forces they do not yet comprehend.

Throughout, the novel balances grand-scale spectacle with intimate human detail: Synnia's grief, Jessyn's secret medication, Campar's defensive humor, the shifting dynamics between Else, Dafyd, and Tonner. The message of the part is encapsulated in its closing declaration from Tkson: survival depends on usefulness. The humans must now figure out what their captors want from them -- or die.

> "I don't understand how they think, and that's terrifying."

> "For the moieties, usefulness is survival. I am here to help you succeed and survive."

---

### Chapter 7

Chapter 7 shifts perspective entirely away from human characters to depict the Carryx invasion of Anjiin from the alien point of view. Seventeen Carryx colony ships emerge from "asymmetric space" at the edge of the heliosphere. Their "half-minds" -- semi-sentient biological computing systems -- confirm coordinates and identify the target world. The ships are accompanied by various servant species: the Rak-hund, the Sinen, and the Soft Lothark.

As they approach, they detect Llaren Morse's signal from the planet's surface. This alarms them briefly, as it partially resembles enemy targeting systems, but they determine it to be local, parallel technological development. They drop their gravity bubble early to see if the planet has a "protector" -- some connection to the unnamed great enemy. Nothing arrives.

The half-mind analyzes Anjiin's inhabitants, identifying two species: humans who communicate with sound and light, and a second, slower species integrated with the soil. As military forces across Anjiin scramble to respond, the Carryx observe the chaos with clinical interest, allowing it to continue "longer than was strictly required so that they could see a little more of the world in its natural state before they changed it." Missiles are launched and disabled effortlessly. Launch sites are destroyed. Then the half-mind executes "breaking the limb": mapping every human on the planet (3,670,862,533), dividing them into eight groups, and selecting one group for extermination.

> "The Carryx allowed it to go on longer than was strictly required so that they could see a little more of the world in its natural state before they changed it."

> "A handful of cities and military bases dissolved in fire. It was no more an attack than a singer clearing her throat was a song."

> "The future fell invisibly toward the inhabitants of Anjiin at the speed of sound."

---

### Chapter 8

The chapter returns to the human perspective. Nol and Synnia choose to garden rather than flee to Else's shelter. In a moment of quiet intimacy, Nol asks why they never had children, and reflects that he's grateful not to have that additional terror. As they weed, Synnia tells him with deliberate calm, "it has been a pleasure being with you." Then the alien voice speaks from the sky. When it declares it will kill one-eighth of the population, something invisible strikes Nol -- he feels what he thinks is a hornet sting on his clavicle, falls face-first into the garden soil, and dies without fully understanding what has happened.

Campar has volunteered to retrieve Nol and Synnia. He drives through a landscape of chaos -- military aircraft, fleeing civilians, smoke and fire. When he arrives, he finds Nol dead in the garden and Synnia in shock. He bundles her into his transport and drives back through the invasion itself. Alien transports rain down. He sees the first Rak-hund -- knife-legged centipede creatures -- and the Soft Lothark. In the chaos, Campar and Synnia become separated. Campar is confronted by Soft Lothark who demand submission. He refuses, raises his fists, and is struck unconscious.

> "I'm just wondering how much more terrifying this would be if we had kids."

> "No. You go fuck yourself, monster."

---

### Chapter 9

The war for Anjiin lasted five days. Dafyd lies face-down on the paving stones of a plaza among hundreds of other captives, noting details with his characteristic analytical mind: the alien fencing material is sized precisely for human bodies, meaning the Carryx knew what they would be containing before they arrived. He witnesses a man stomped to death by a Rak-hund for resisting. A rescue attempt by Irvian Security Services is annihilated with devastating force.

Afterward, a Sinen identifies Dafyd by name and assignment. He is led to an alien transport ship, where he finds Campar, wounded but alive. When Dafyd asks "What's going on?", Campar replies with characteristic dark humor.

> "Understanding was both his only entertainment and the only power he had now. Having any power at all was the comforting self-delusion he clung to."

> "I think some important scientific questions have finally been answered. Alien life exists, and they are assholes."

---

### Chapter 10

This long chapter follows Jessyn and the workgroup through weeks of imprisonment in the hold of an alien ship. Jessyn and Else are in a crowded, low-ceilinged chamber lit in dull orange monochrome. Urrys Ostencour, a security forces officer, is already organizing -- cataloging people's skills, assessing the situation. He notes that the Carryx took "the top" from every field. Synnia arrives, confirming Nol's death in the one-eighth culling.

The chapter contains a crucial interlude from the perspective of the swarm -- the alien intelligence inhabiting one of the captives. It is a spy, deliberately placed to be captured by the Carryx. It catalogs everything it has learned and considers this intelligence invaluable to unnamed "generals and analysts." This is the first hint of a third faction in the conflict.

The ship transits through "asymmetric space," causing a disturbing inversion of time where memory and experience briefly run backward. Life in the cell degrades into misery. Jessyn secretly manages a dwindling supply of psychiatric medication. People begin to break down. Synnia, desperate and broken by grief, tells Jessyn that Ostencour is planning an escape attempt.

> "Of course I am. It's how I keep from screaming all day. What do you do?"

> "I've been trying not to imagine the seven people Nol died for. I'm angry at people I don't even know for being alive when he's not."

---

### Chapter 11

The workgroup debates the wisdom of Ostencour's planned escape. Dafyd argues it will get them all killed. Else goes to talk to Ostencour, attempting to dissuade him.

The escape attempt happens anyway. The Soft Lothark guard is dragged down by Ostencour and six others, including Synnia. But the alien's body has a devastating defense mechanism: it swells and explodes, spraying caustic biological material over the attackers. Two prisoners die. The Soft Lothark's corpse is consumed by two others of its species who enter the cell -- they eat their dead, like a biological cleanup protocol.

Dafyd eventually recovers from the poisoning and shares a pivotal insight. He tells Else about a childhood tutorial where a teacher named a water beetle "Little Dot," let the students bond with it, then cut it open. The beetle ate its own spilled organs -- not out of fear, but because its reflexes identified a high-energy food source. Dafyd realizes he has been making the same error with the Carryx -- projecting human motivations onto beings whose psychology is fundamentally alien. No punishment came for the escape attempt, not because the Carryx were merciful, but because punishment may not be a concept that applies.

> "I put myself in the other person's place. It's what I do... I thought there would be punishment because I thought they were like us. But there wasn't any, and I don't know why."

> "Because they aren't like us. They aren't like beetles either. But they aren't like us."

---

### Chapter 12

The ship arrives at the Carryx homeworld. The prisoners emerge into staggering alien architecture: vast ebony ziggurats the size of mountains, silver arches rising past the sky, a "world-city" of incomprehensible scale. A woman who shouts in defiance at the white Carryx is instantly obliterated. Dafyd and Else prostrate themselves.

Jessyn takes stock of the quarters the Carryx have prepared -- a crude approximation of human living spaces, all slightly wrong. She showers for the first time in weeks. Irinna and Tonner arrive, completing the workgroup (minus Nol). Tonner and Else have a tense private conversation; she firmly declines to share his room.

The chapter's final scene introduces Tkson of the cohort Malkal, a Carryx designated as "keeper-librarian" of their moiety. It arrives with Rickar Daumatin, the last of the workgroup. Tkson offers awkward condolences for Nol's death and states the foundational principle of their captivity.

> "We never had a chance. Look at all this. We never stood a chance against this."

> "We'd have fought anyway. It's what we do... We don't stop just because there's no hope."

> "For the moieties, usefulness is survival. I am here to help you succeed and survive."

> "There's always hope for something. Just not always... not always what we want."

## PART THREE: PUZZLES

### Part Three Epigraph

> "... the delusion that peace is possible and desirable... What can be subjugated, must be... the betrayer desired peace and destroyed countless worlds to take it, holding both dreams of perfection and mass destruction in his mind simultaneously... You would be wise to kill him too."
> -- From the final statement of Ekur-Tkalal, keeper-librarian of the human moiety of the Carryx

### Part Three Summary

Part Three traces the human workgroup's transition from passive captives to active participants in a system they are only beginning to understand. Given a seemingly straightforward biochemical task -- make alien berries nourishing for an alien creature -- they initially fall into familiar patterns: Tonner leads, the scientists research, everyone tries to pretend normalcy is possible. But the Carryx world-city is not a university lab. The "feather monkeys" (Night Drinkers) are rival test subjects competing for the same niche, and they escalate from nuisance to lethal threat, bombing the lab and killing Irinna.

The emotional and psychological toll is the part's constant undercurrent. Jessyn is suicidal, Campar has panic attacks, Rickar is isolated, Synnia has shut down, and Tonner's authoritarian control is a thin shell over terror. The swarm sections reveal that "Else" is actually an alien infiltrator recording everything, shaped by the dead women it has absorbed. Jessyn's pill becomes a symbol of desperate resourcefulness, and her savage defense of Irinna reveals a capacity for violence no one expected.

The part culminates in Dafyd's intellectual breakthrough: the biochemical task may not be the test at all. The Carryx may be evaluating the humans' ability to self-organize, adapt, defend themselves, and navigate a hostile environment. This reframing shifts power from Tonner's rigid authority to Dafyd's flexible thinking.

> "The only test is whether a subject species is useful. Usefulness is survival."

> "We're all broken. We're trying to find something we can control, because we can't control anything."

> "Just because it's what we're supposed to do, that doesn't mean it's the test."

---

### Chapter 13

Rickar trails behind the others as the librarian leads the human group through the Carryx world-city for the first time. He observes the group dynamics -- Campar checking on everyone, Synnia's absence unquestioned. The corridors are vast and aggressively manufactured, nothing like the organic coral buildings of Anjiin. They pass many alien species: Rak-hund, Soft Lothark, floating globes, crow-ape hybrids, and the Carryx themselves.

Rickar flashes back to the invasion -- a woman he knew dying in front of her ten-year-old son. These memories haunt him. The librarian leads them to an alcove marked with a red band. Inside is a laboratory reconstructed from salvaged Anjiin equipment. In cages at the back are the specimens: red orbs (berries) clinging to a stick from one alien world, and a flat, iridescent blue creature resembling a three-legged turtle from another. The task: "You will make these first organisms nourishing for the second."

When Dafyd asks what happens if they fail, the librarian's answer is chilling: usefulness is survival. Back at quarters, Tonner takes command. But when Rickar volunteers to help, Tonner rejects him bitterly: "I couldn't trust you when the stakes were low. Why the fuck would I trust you now?"

> "The only test is whether a subject species is useful. Usefulness is survival."

> "The others grabbed onto Tonner's certainty like it was an umbrella and they were caught in a hailstorm."

---

### Chapter 14

This chapter shifts entirely to the Carryx perspective, following Ekur-Tkalal, subjugator-librarian of the sixteenth dactyl. It reveals the vast hierarchical structure of the Carryx empire. The chapter opens on a conquest of a world called Ayayeh, home to hexapod creatures called the Eelie. But the conquest goes badly wrong -- the Eelie are "manufactured life-forms intended to trick us into believing they are the indigenous species." The entire world is a trap set by the Carryx's unnamed enemy.

Carryx forces suffer massive casualties as enemy ships pour through rifts in space. The battle becomes a grinding stalemate. The enemy is described as "virtually deathless" -- their soldiers can be riddled with injuries and keep flowing forward. Eventually, orders arrive: the dactyl is to be sacrificed. Each remaining ship is to provoke different enemy responses, record the results, and die. The information about how the enemy fights is more valuable than the ships themselves. Ekur-Tkalal escapes with captured enemy soldiers -- thin-bodied animals with fivefold symmetry.

> "It would have been unthinkable, except that it was happening."

> "Out of more than a thousand species, this enemy was the most recalcitrant, but the patterns in its behavior and design would bring it to heel in time."

> "If any of the Carryx ships survived, they would rejoin the librarian later. If not, then it was better for the species that they had died. What is, is. It can be nothing else."

---

### Chapter 15

Told from Jessyn's perspective, this chapter reveals the depth of her psychological suffering. She falls asleep each night by imagining her own death. Work is her only lifeline. She and Irinna run cellular motility tests, checking whether not-turtle tissue migrates toward simple sugars. Irinna devises a clever workaround for the lack of writing materials: coding lab notes as fake peptide sequences and using the proteomic dictionary's error log to store them.

Campar, who has seemed the most together of all of them, suffers a complete panic attack while washing dishes. Jessyn recognizes it immediately and guides him through breathing exercises. In the aftermath, she names what everyone has been avoiding: they are all broken. Tonner is tripling down on research, Synnia has shut down, Rickar fixates on their anger at him, Else is creating sexual drama, Dafyd is "intellectualizing himself into a small steel cube," and Jessyn herself is "pretty sure I'm suicidal."

The chapter ends with a passage from the swarm's perspective. The alien intelligence is a spy, recording everything. It can influence others through pheromones. Its mission: find a way to transmit the data it has gathered.

> "We're all broken. We're trying to find something we can control, because we can't control anything."

> "It's like we've been given the most important test of our lives, but all the questions are in secret code."

> "The swarm was designed as a tool of war. It was built to slip behind the defenses of the Carryx and expose the great enemy's weaknesses. It still is that. It is also becoming something stranger."

---

### Chapter 16

From Dafyd's perspective, the chapter opens with a scientific breakthrough. Tonner has discovered that the sandy silicate center of the berries is a "farm" -- a microbiotic environment where hundreds of tiny organisms process sugars. This opens a potential shortcut: rather than reengineering berry metabolism, they might introduce a new organism into the existing farm, like a "cuckoo."

Dafyd's defining moment comes when he decides to explore the world-city alone. He sits on an outcropping, forcing himself to look at the alien environment until it becomes less terrifying -- a lifelong pattern of confronting fear through deliberate exposure. He observes the alien ecosystem and has an insight: the species he knows by name are the ones with translator squares; the ones without translators are silent and unknowable.

Combining observations, Dafyd navigates through the maze of Carryx corridors to find the librarian's den. He returns eight hours later, exhausted and elated, carrying a metal crate. Inside: paper-like pages, pots of ink, and metal styluses. "I got pens," he says.

> "It was strange the way that life kept going. They had lost everything -- homes, lives, their place in the vastness of the universe -- and Campar still made coffee every morning."

> "The horror behind it was real. There wasn't a moment that Dafyd couldn't feel the fear and tension in his body if he just turned his mind to it. But there were some moments when there was also awe."

---

### Chapter 17

Jessyn is preparing berry samples for amino acid toxicity testing. The feather monkeys (Night Drinkers) bring a small gray box into the lab. Meanwhile, Jessyn is sinking deeper into depression. She has one antidepressant pill left, which she knows is pharmacologically useless but clings to as a talisman.

Then comes a breakthrough realization: the berries are biochemical factories. Her medication is just a molecule. If the berries can be engineered to produce specific biochemicals for the not-turtle, they could also produce her medication. Hope surges through her "like a breath of air when she'd already resigned herself to drowning."

She rushes back for the pill, but when she returns to the lab, disaster has struck. The gray box was a bomb. The Night Drinkers are in full attack -- smashing samples, shredding notes, and biting Irinna. Jessyn fights with terrifying ferocity. She clubs one monkey to death, breaks another's neck with her bare hands. She drives them off, but Irinna is badly burned and bleeding. She hoists Irinna in a fireman's carry and walks the entire distance back. Irinna dies.

> "The pain was permission."

### Chapter 18

Tonner, enraged by Irinna's death, confronts the librarian. He demands safety, insisting "We can't work if we're not safe." Dafyd intervenes with diplomatic questioning and extracts crucial information: the Night Drinkers are working on the same biochemical project, and the test is competitive. The Carryx are not protecting anyone; the subject species must protect themselves.

Back at the quarters, Tonner announces they are relocating the lab for security. Then he visits Jessyn's bedside, where she confesses about her hidden medication and unauthorized experiment. Later, alone with Else, Tonner breaks down: "How do I keep them safe?" His entire plan -- be obedient, stay focused, do the work -- has failed. Else tells him gently that maybe it isn't his job. He weeps.

### Chapter 19

The chapter opens with an informal wake for Irinna. They share small memories. The Sinen arrive to take the body. Tonner says only "Work tomorrow." The narrative voice reflects: "Life went on. That was the terrible thing."

Dafyd then delivers a paradigm-shifting argument. He suggests the biochemical task may not be the real test at all -- it may just be busywork while the Carryx evaluate something else entirely. Can the humans self-organize? Protect themselves? Produce their own medicine and food? Build networks? The fact that bombs exist in a supposedly scientific competition proves the Carryx care about more than lab results. Jessyn's unauthorized medicine experiment, rather than being a betrayal, may have been exactly the kind of initiative the Carryx are looking for.

Campar backs him up, Else signals assent, and even Tonner grudgingly agrees. In the chapter's final line, Campar observes the power shift: "the workgroup had belonged to Tonner Freis, but at least for the moment, it was now Alkhor's."

> "Life went on. That was the terrible thing. They were ripped out of their world, their lives, their sense of who and what they were. Their history. They were killed, or made to watch the people they loved die. And then, at some point, they were hungry. Thirsty. They had to piss."

## PART FOUR: TURNABOUT

### Part Four Epigraph

> "The moieties have always been a project of exploration and learning. Not of preservation... That which we encountered, we studied, often to destruction. That which we touched, we changed... That which had utility was incorporated, that which had none was culled. Any being who has chosen to pluck weeds out of a garden has done the same. You condemn us even as you follow our example, and with your foot on my throat, I applaud you. Which of us, then, has greater integrity?"
> -- From the final statement of Ekur-Tkalal, keeper-librarian of the human moiety of the Carryx

### Part Four Summary

Part Four chronicles a decisive shift in the humans' circumstances. On the surface, the human research team transforms from vulnerable captives into effective combatants. They reverse-engineer the Night Drinkers' bioweapon, launch a devastating retaliatory strike, and force their rivals into unconditional surrender. Jessyn channels depression and self-hatred into violent agency. Tonner synthesizes her medication from alien organisms. The team's scientific instincts prove to be their greatest survival asset -- even mid-battle, their first impulse is to learn from the enemy.

But the deeper turnabout operates at the level of awareness and connection. Dafyd's attempts to understand the Carryx reveal disturbing patterns: the hallway crows have vanished entirely (presumably culled), and the librarian reacts with fury to the idea of humans learning about the Carryx. Meanwhile, the swarm makes contact with captured "fivefold" pilots -- manufactured beings fighting the Carryx in a larger war. The Night Drinkers' surrender provides Dafyd with a translator that enables communication with other species, leading to the reunion of Jessyn and her brother Jellit. The humans are building networks beyond their small group.

The epigraph -- framed as a "final statement" -- foreshadows an ultimate reckoning. The humans are learning to play the Carryx's game, but Dafyd's unease about "agreeing to play things the way the Carryx want us to" suggests the real victory will require something beyond the rules the Carryx have set.

> "The enemy had come, bent on murder. They'd broken into their home, assaulted them, and the first impulse of the team was to see what they could learn from it."

> "Jessyn knew the medicine was starting to work when her fantasies changed from suicide to murder."

> "You condemn us even as you follow our example, and with your foot on my throat, I applaud you. Which of us, then, has greater integrity?"

### Chapter 20

Jessyn, Campar, and Rickar return to the bombed-out lab to retrieve the proteomic dictionary. Jessyn reflects on her lifelong struggle with depression -- her first crisis during adolescence, her brother Jellit's role as caretaker. Now without medication, she relies on behavioral interventions: sleeping, showering, eating, exercising.

When they encounter five Night Drinkers watching them, something shifts inside Jessyn. Her self-directed hatred transforms into outward aggression. She strides out to confront the Night Drinkers, armed with a crowbar. They flee. She pursues them relentlessly, discovering their nest -- a wall of soft material stippled with burrow holes. She bangs her crowbar on the ground and shouts that she knows where they live. Campar calls her their "war leader."

### Chapter 21

Dafyd visits the Carryx librarian with a proposal: he wants to learn about the Carryx so the humans can better serve them. The librarian reacts with near-violence, declaring "An animal does not choose its essential nature and place in society." Dafyd retreats, shaken but analytically replaying the conversation.

Walking back, Dafyd notices the hallway crows have completely vanished -- a chilling implication that the species has been culled. Back at quarters, the group is in higher spirits. Jessyn's medication can be synthesized from the berries. The group dynamic is shifting.

Else comes to Dafyd's room at night. She tells him they don't have much left and asks to stay. The chapter shifts to the swarm's perspective -- observing the human experience of sex and intimacy with fascination while also detecting environmental signals indicating the Carryx are preparing for another wave of attacks.

### Chapter 22

Tonner tries not to notice Else has gone to Dafyd's room. He throws himself into work, staying up through the night running centrifuge assays to produce Jessyn's medication. Campar gently confronts him: "You're pouting, but I think I would too, in your position."

Just as Tonner begins articulating a philosophical insight about the Carryx and emotional control, the Night Drinkers attack in force with gel sacs made from modified berries that act as chemical weapons. A chaotic battle erupts. The group fights them off. In the aftermath, the team turns scientific immediately. Jessyn begins dissecting the dead Night Drinker. Tonner gives Jessyn her synthesized medication. Campar makes the crucial observation that the gel sacs are made from the same berries -- the Night Drinkers made a bioweapon from the same material the humans used for pharmaceuticals. Tonner reorganizes priorities to include weapons testing.

### Chapter 23

Dafyd is preoccupied with trying to understand the Carryx -- why they gave humans windows, why the librarian reacted violently to his offer to learn, why the Carryx reject the tools of successful empire. He catalogs the contradictions: "They won't learn from us. They won't teach us."

Jessyn's medication begins working. In a stark passage, she describes how she knew: her fantasies shifted from suicide to murder. She talks with Synnia, performing normalcy while internally acknowledging she's "just feeling a different kind of bad."

The group launches a retaliatory raid on the Night Drinkers' nest, hurling bioweapon bulbs into their burrow holes. The weapon devastates them. Jessyn experiences the violence with intoxicating joy, recognizing that all her suffering has been "reforged into something sharp." The raid is a decisive victory. Dafyd alone sounds a note of caution: "there's something about it that feels like we're agreeing to play things the way the Carryx want us to."

> "Jessyn knew the medicine was starting to work when her fantasies changed from suicide to murder."

### Chapter 24

This chapter introduces Ekur-Tkalal, a subjugator-librarian being reassigned to interrogator-librarian for captured "fivefold" enemies. The swarm slips away from the group, having detected a distress call on frequencies only it can perceive. ## PART FIVE: FISSURE

### Part Five Epigraph

> "He wasn't unique, or not more than any other individual being would be. He had no insights that changed the way I saw the order of things, he didn't have any particular philosophy that opened the doors of reality. He was one among many, with variations as any of them had. The only thing that I think made him stand out at all was the depth of hatred that the others came to have for him."
> -- From the final statement of Ekur-Tkalal, keeper-librarian of the human moiety of the Carryx

### Part Five Summary

Part Five traces the splintering of the human captives along ideological and moral lines. The reunion with Jellit and other human groups, initially a source of joy, quickly becomes the catalyst for crisis. Urrys Ostencour, the security officer who tried to revolt during transit, has spent his captivity building a clandestine network and manufacturing crude weapons. He enlists the biochemistry team to develop biological weapons against the Carryx. The workgroup divides: Synnia and Jellit are committed to the revolt; Campar opposes it as futile suicide; Tonner is intellectually seduced by the research challenge; Jessyn understands both positions with the clarity of someone who has lived alongside death; Dafyd is caught in the middle.

The fissure deepens catastrophically when Else reveals the novel's central secret: she carries an alien spy -- a swarm of billions of tiny machines implanted by the Carryx's enemies in a vast interstellar war. The spy needs the humans to survive so it can eventually transmit its intelligence. Else asks Dafyd to betray the rebels to the librarian to save the larger cause. Dafyd is trapped between betraying people he loves and serving a purpose he cannot verify, asked to do so by a being that admits it would lie to him if necessary. Meanwhile, Jellit and Merrol are considering whether Dafyd needs to be eliminated as a security risk.

Running through all of this is the question the Carryx are testing: can humanity be domesticated? The alien testimonies in Chapter 27 show the full spectrum of answers other species have given. The humans' answer is still unfolding, but Part Five makes clear that the question itself is tearing them apart.

> "The first thing our captors took from us was all of our choices."

> "He wasn't unique... The only thing that I think made him stand out at all was the depth of hatred that the others came to have for him."

### Chapter 26

Jessyn is euphoric at being reunited with her brother Jellit and meeting new humans: Allstin, a charismatic performer, and Llaren Morse, the astronomer who first detected the Carryx objects. The evening becomes a gathering as the groups exchange stories. The conversation turns somber when Llaren notes that everyone speaks of home in the past tense.

Unable to sleep, Jessyn's mind churns through memories of her old life on Anjiin. Anger wells up. She reflects on a therapist's saying: "Anger is pain in a party mask." Rising from bed, she catches Llaren Morse speaking conspiratorially to Synnia in the hallway. Synnia's eyes are bright with "open hunger," and Jessyn hears her say "Yes, tell him I'll help."

Dafyd and Else take the translation device into the cathedral to interview alien species. Then Rak-hund soldiers begin tearing apart the Night Drinkers' home. The Night Drinkers are systematically slaughtered over more than an hour, even those trying to flee carrying eggs. Dafyd, horrified, realizes the humans' "peace" with the Night Drinkers may have sealed their fate. He vomits.

Dafyd sits in the cathedral, weighed down by despair. He sees the Carryx world-prison as analogous to the berries: a boundary organism that farms and manages other life. He is haunted by knowledge that a conspiracy is growing.

## PART SIX: SMALL BATTLES IN THE GREAT WAR

### Part Six Epigraph

> "They are a social species. A hive species, but without the clarity that a biological hive brings. They are structureless, living always in conflict with themselves and each other... Their great genius is rationalization: lying into mirrors until they bully and seduce themselves into things they would never otherwise do. That is their way, and for a time we harnessed it as we did everything. For a time."
> -- From the final statement of Ekur-Tkalal, keeper-librarian of the human moiety of the Carryx

### Part Six Summary

Part Six brings the novel's major threads to their devastating climax. Dafyd's betrayal of the human resistance -- motivated by Else and the alien spy's argument that it is the only way to save the species -- triggers a violent purge. The Carryx systematically hunt and kill the conspirators. Synnia, Dennia, Allstin, Merrol, Llaren Morse, and many others die. Else Yannin is found dead without a wound -- her body abandoned by the swarm, which has jumped to Jellit. The old librarian is executed not as punishment but as an "honor" -- because it was saved by an animal, and no Carryx can hold status after that. A new, more formidable librarian, Ekur-Tkalal, takes command.

The part is structured around a series of terrible transactions. Lives are traded for survival; collaboration is exchanged for the preservation of Anjiin; scientific success is rewarded with deeper enslavement. Dafyd's critical intellectual breakthrough -- understanding that Carryx bodies literally transform to match their social status, that their determinism is biological rather than philosophical -- gives him the key insight he needs. He emerges from the wreckage not as a collaborator but as a patient revolutionary, vowing to learn the Carryx from the inside and destroy them.

The final chapter reveals the swarm has taken Jellit's body, complicating Dafyd's war with an espionage mission he cannot see and a love built on deception. The epigraph's ominous "For a time" frames everything: the Carryx believe they have domesticated humanity, but the seeds of their downfall are already planted.

> "You are too small to see the pathos, and I am too impure to escape it. What is, is."

> "For a moment, Dafyd Alkhor saw the universe the way a Carryx would, and it was beautifully simple and utterly horrific."

Dafyd stands before the Carryx librarian, sleepless and terrified, preparing to betray Ostencour's resistance conspiracy. He exposes the conspiracy carefully and completely, naming leaders, weapons caches, and strike teams.

You are too small to see the pathos, and I am too impure to escape it." Left alone together, Dafyd and Jellit share raw emotional connection. Jellit tells Dafyd he was brave and that it was the right thing. He reveals Else "gave me a deeper understanding of the situation" -- implying the spy revealed itself to him. ### Chapter 33

Jessyn and Rickar prepare the final test of Tonner's breakthrough. The not-turtle creature races toward the prepared food and devours it -- a moment of scientific triumph. But celebration is immediately shattered when a Rak-hund appears. Dennia pulls a hidden gun and shoots the Rak-hund, shouting for Synnia to run. The Rak-hund kills Dennia, but deliberately spares Jessyn and Rickar -- they had not joined the conspiracy.

Jessyn and Rickar trace Synnia's path and discover the aftermath: Allstin dead, Synnia face-down, Merrol and Llaren Morse dead. In Jellit's bedroom, they find Else Yannin's body -- no wounds, no blood. Jellit is nowhere to be found.

### Chapter 34

Ekur-Tkalal, an interrogator-librarian, is summoned before the Sovran herself -- the supreme ruler of the Carryx. The Sovran is described as vast, her abdomen pulsing with light, her hundred eyes shifting independently. Ekur-Tkalal's body involuntarily metamorphoses in response to the pheromones. It is being reassigned to manage the human moiety.

Dafyd learns that Else and Synnia are dead. The surviving workgroup is transported to an enormous arena where thousands of human captives are assembled before the Sovran. During the ceremony, Dafyd has a critical breakthrough: he recognizes that a former librarian's body has transformed into a green soldier's. The Carryx do not merely hold social positions; their bodies physically transform. "Essential nature and place in society" is literal biological reality.

Tonner stands up during the ceremony and demands a translator. Dafyd tackles him, breaking his arm to demonstrate submission. The old librarian is ritually executed by the Sovran -- because "there is no place in the moieties for a Carryx that was saved by an animal." The new librarian, Ekur-Tkalal, takes command and returns Jellit to Jessyn.

### Chapter 35

The new librarian, Ekur-Tkalal, addresses the humans. Their duties will be reassigned. Greater usefulness brings greater resources; less usefulness brings diminishment. Some will be sent to other worlds. As consolation, the librarian informs them that because of their success, Anjiin has been preserved rather than destroyed: "In this, you are your people's saviors."
