# The Faith of Beasts — Mythology Compendium

*The Captive's War, Book 2 — James S.A. Corey*

---

## Scope and Structure

*The Faith of Beasts* contains exactly one myth cycle, serialized across the novel's six parts: **the story of Ke and Ash-Abbé, the bird that ate the sky.** Each of the six parts opens with one installment, attributed in every case to the same in-universe source:

> *—From* Myths of Origin: Field Notes and Analysis*, Uuya Tomos, editor.*

Uuya Tomos is not just the nominal compiler of these epigraphs — she is a character in the novel. A folklorist, social historian, and author of the "Wolf of the World" mythic novels and "The People of the Panopt," she is drafted in Chapter 9 by Dafyd Alkhor into a secret project: to build stories and songs that will teach the human children born under Carryx captivity to "seem like one thing and be something else." The myth cycle that runs as the book's spine is thus simultaneously ancient Anjiin folklore AND the resistance material Uuya is curating — a story about using song to master an empire, told as the weapon that will be used to master an empire.

No other embedded myths, fables, songs, or folktales appear as discrete told-stories in the book. Single passing references to **Shavas Rey, the sleeping god** of Gallantist tradition (who will "wake at the end of time to remake the world") and to **the Great Heron that split the sky** are named but not retold.

The six installments of the Ke cycle follow below in book order, verbatim. Each is prefaced with its part title, the chapter it precedes, and its line range in the source text.

## I. Part One: STRUCTURE — precedes Chapter 1 (lines 210–218)

> The world grew sick.
>
> The people saw it in the rotting fields of newly sown crops, they smelled it on the fish they pulled into their canoes dying and covered in sores, and they heard it in the hollow words of their elders and ancestors which had ceased to carry meaning or solace. In every mind and every heart, a single truth appeared as if each heart was the first to feel it and each mind the first to put it in words: Their home was dying.
>
> Grief came and it smothered the fields. Rage came and it drove away the fish. Doubt came and it silenced the voices that offered wisdom. All across the nations a great hopelessness grew, for no one in the lands saw a future beyond the sickness and rot.
>
> Except in one village at the foot of a great mountain, there was a boy whose name was Ke. And Ke looked up at the night, and the brightest light was not a star, but the twin world that was made when the Great Heron split the sky. The light had been known by another name, but Ke saw it and he gave it a new name: Anjiin.
>
> *—From* Myths of Origin: Field Notes and Analysis*, Uuya Tomos, editor.*

## II. Part Two: ANTITHESIS — precedes Chapter 9 (lines 1372–1384)

> Ke looked up at the world he had named, and he went to his father. *Father, I have named a new world for us so that we may leave the sickness of our past behind us and live purified.*
>
> But Ke's father told him: *We cannot reach this new world because it is too far from us, and our boats never learned to sail across the starry night. Only the Gods know this magic.*
>
> So Ke went to the lord of all that grows and asked him to grow a stalk of bamboo so long that it could reach the new world, but the lord of all that grows had become weak, and could not grow bamboo long enough to climb to the new world, and so they could not climb to Anjiin.
>
> And Ke went to the lady of the oceans and asked her to spin a waterspout powerful enough that they could sail to the new world, but the lady of the oceans had grown sick, and could not raise a waterspout powerful enough to climb to the new world, so they could not sail to Anjiin.
>
> And Ke went to the spirits of the deep fires and asked them to open a volcano so wide that its heat would float them to the new world, but the spirits of the deep fires did not answer him because they had already turned away from the concerns of living things, and so they could not float to Anjiin.
>
> And Ke went to the holy mountain of Buruq where Ash-Abbé, the bird that ate the sky, made its nest. There he could not ask for anything for fear that the great beast would eat him. And so there he settled in to make his plan.
>
> *—From* Myths of Origin: Field Notes and Analysis*, Uuya Tomos, editor.*

## III. Part Three: PRESSURE DRIVES CONVERGENCE — precedes Chapter 16 (lines 2367–2375)

> Ash-Abbé, the bird that ate the sky, had its nest at the top of the mountain, high above the plains. Its wings were as wide as the world and its beak as sharp as obsidian and its claws were black ice that could strip the flesh from anything they touched. Ash-Abbé the Terrible was its name, and any who drew the attention of the bird that ate the sky would surely die.
>
> But Ke was clever. For many days, he sat at the mountain's base as still as a stone and watched to see when the great bird woke and when it slept, when it flew and when it returned from flying. And so Ke grew wiser in the great bird's ways.
>
> When he knew what times the bird's attention was elsewhere, he began to climb up the side of the mountain, and as he did he found the scat and the leavings of the great bird, the bones and feathers of those things that died to feed Ash-Abbé, and so Ke grew wiser in the great bird's hunger.
>
> After long seasons slowly climbing to Ash-Abbé's nest, Ke became as familiar to the mountain as the stones and trees, his body as wild as the bears and elk and foxes. And because he had been clever enough to become a part of the mountain, the great bird did not see him. And so he sat, silent and still, and he listened to the great bird as it sang. And so Ke became wise in the great bird's song.
>
> *—From* Myths of Origin: Field Notes and Analysis*, Uuya Tomos, editor.*

## IV. Part Four: DOGS AND WOLVES — precedes Chapter 22 (lines 3491–3499)

> And so the night came when Ke had learned the ways and hungers and songs of the bird that ate the sky. Knowing all these things, Ke sang new songs in the cool night of Buruq so that the great bird could hear them.
>
> He sang first of the ways that the bird already knew: of the rise and fall of the sun, of the turning of the seasons, of the terror in the raising up of children and the beauty of the fading of the very old. He sang of winter and night giving way to daylight and spring. He sang of the breath of the world. And Ash-Abbé listened.
>
> He sang second of the hungers that the bird already suffered: of the salt of blood that flowed from fallen prey, of the richness of fresh water gulped from the stream, of the longing that all animals feel in their season for the comfort and bodies of others of their kind and the fires that in their slaking bring new life to the worlds. And Ash-Abbé listened.
>
> He sang at last of the new place which he had named: Anjiin that waited across the void. And this song he did not end as the others had, for as he sang it, Ke mounted the great bird's wide back. When the great bird rose, Ke shaped his song to guide it, and the bird that ate the sky was tethered all unknowing by the power of the boy's song and it did as it was bid.
>
> *—From* Myths of Origin: Field Notes and Analysis*, Uuya Tomos, editor.*

## V. Part Five: KINDS OF LIFE — precedes Chapter 28 (lines 4537–4551)

> Ke sang Ash-Abbé to the rainforest where the elder trees sent their children with all the seeds and birds and animals of that land onto the great bird's back.
>
> And Ke sang Ash-Abbé to the desert where the villagers took the old knowledge of hidden water and the wisdom that the wind had taught them and the animals and insects of that land onto the great bird's back.
>
> And Ke sang Ash-Abbé to the coast of the greatest ocean where the fishers lifted their boats and their nets and carried the fullness of their catch onto the great bird's back.
>
> To every corner of the sickened world, Ash-Abbé flew and gathered from those places the people and animals and plants and fungi until all that portion which was still good had gathered together on its back.
>
> And Ke sang Ash-Abbé to his father's home, but his father would not come out, for he had seen a vision of all that was to come and was sick from grief. Though they called for him three times, he would not come out. And so the father of Ke remained behind and did not step foot in the new lands.
>
> When all who would come had come, Ke sang Ash-Abbé into the sky and through the sky and beyond it.
>
> And this is how humanity came to Anjiin.
>
> *—From* Myths of Origin: Field Notes and Analysis*, Uuya Tomos, editor.*

## VI. Part Six: DUCKS AND RABBITS — precedes Chapter 33 (lines 5718–5730)

> But when the last of the animals and plants and people of the world left the broad back of Ash-Abbé, the bird that ate the sky, Ke did not follow them but instead remained where he was and sang the songs to the great bird as he had done before.
>
> The elders of the people called out to him, inviting him down to the world he had given them with promises of comfort and wine and feasting. Ke heard them, but he did not leave his place.
>
> The young of the people called out to him, challenging him to come down to the world he had given them with offers to join their games and lead their hunts. Ke heard them, but he did not leave his place.
>
> The infants, the first to be born on the new world, cried out to him, wailing without words to the world-father who had brought them to this new home but then abandoned them there. Ke heard, and though he ached to comfort them, he did not leave his place.
>
> For thirty days and thirty nights, they called to him, and for thirty days and thirty nights, he refused. And on the thirty-first day, a weaver girl tied the new grasses of Anjiin and the old grasses that they had carried from the world and crept up to Ke thinking to seduce him back down with her.
>
> When he saw her, Ke cried out in pain and longing, for he wished nothing more than to be part of the new world he had brought them to. But the great bird Ash-Abbé had been tricked by his song, and if he ceased to sing it, the bird would grow angry and punish all those who had gained from its humiliation. And so Ke could not be as his people were, and flew Ash-Abbé to the space between worlds. And if he has not died, he sings there still to keep his people safe.
>
> *—From* Myths of Origin: Field Notes and Analysis*, Uuya Tomos, editor.*

## Other Mythic References (Named, Not Retold)

Three additional mythic threads are named in passing but not narrated:

- **The Great Heron that split the sky** (Part One epigraph) — the cosmogonic act that created the twin world later renamed Anjiin. No further retelling appears.
- **Shavas Rey, the sleeping god** of the Gallantist tradition — referenced by Uuya Tomos in TFOB Chapter 9 as a model of embedded resistance mythology: "They built a whole body of folktales around Shavas Rey, the sleeping god, and how she would wake at the end of time to remake the world." Dafyd immediately identifies this as the template he wants for the children's curriculum.
- **The Wolf of the World novels** and **The People of the Panopt** short stories — Uuya Tomos's own prior published works, named by Dafyd in TFOB Chapter 9 ("I didn't understand *Wolf of the World*, but you did a set of short stories. *The People of the Panopt*. I don't know how many times I read those"). Contents unspecified.

## Analysis: How the Ke Cycle Maps Onto the Novel

The Ke cycle is not decorative. Read against the plot, it is a near-exact allegorical skeleton for *The Faith of Beasts*, and its in-universe editor is the character the book's resistance arc turns on.

### The sickened world is Anjiin, and Anjiin is both

Part One's opening — "The world grew sick" — reads at first as prehistory: the ancestral home humanity fled before arriving on Anjiin. But the book's situation collapses the timeframe. Anjiin itself has now fallen. The "sickened world" of the myth is simultaneously the ancient ancestral home AND the Anjiin the Carryx have just destroyed. Humanity is again looking at a brightest light that is not a star (the Carryx world-palace, the sites of human deployment across "thousands of worlds") and again giving it a new name. The myth frames the novel as a **second migration**: not this time by flight, but by the slow biological and cultural re-rooting of captive humans into Carryx space, including the "lamb sack" infants who will know no home but the empire.

### Ash-Abbé is the Carryx

Ash-Abbé, "the bird that ate the sky," with obsidian beak and black-ice claws that "strip the flesh from anything they touched," whose attention is itself lethal — this is a near-transparent figure for the Carryx. The myth insists on two things about the great bird: it is terrible beyond negotiation, AND it can be mastered, but only by someone who has become invisible to it, learned its ways, learned its hungers, learned its song, and then sings a song it does not recognize as a leash. Every element of that method is a policy Dafyd enacts on the page:

- **Invisibility and mimicry**: Dafyd drafts reports in proper Carryx archival grammar, inhabits the role of "factor" that Ekur-Tkalal half-consciously assigns him, and accepts the humiliation of being called the Carryx's "lapdog" by Uuya because it keeps the great bird from seeing him.
- **Learning the ways**: The moiety is explicitly redirected into cross-tree nutritional translation, gravimetric lensing, and archive work. The Ke who "grew wiser in the great bird's ways" by reading its scat is the Dafyd who reads Carryx report syntax and Jellit-the-swarm who, by the end of Part Three, "can now see everything" inside the archive.
- **Mastery through song that the subject does not recognize**: Part Four is the cycle's explicit thesis — Ke wins by singing first what the bird already knows, then what the bird already hungers for, and only then the new destination. Dafyd's pitch to Uuya in Chapter 9 reproduces this sequence exactly: *embed the resistance in stories that sound like the old stories, so that "even if they listen in, they'll hear things that maybe they also heard when they were spying on us before the invasion. Then it just looks like continuity."* The disguise of familiar song is the mechanism of capture.

### The cycle is itself the curriculum

The frame attribution ("*Myths of Origin: Field Notes and Analysis*, Uuya Tomos, editor") is the trick the whole book is playing. The reader has been reading — across the full length of the novel, at the head of every part — **the stories Uuya is editing for the children.** The myth we read in Part One as context for Anjiin's history is the same myth that, in-story, Uuya is preparing as the primer for the first Carryx-born human cohort. The reader and the lamb-sack generation are being instructed by the same text. What the book teaches its captive children, it teaches its reader: that a conquering empire can be tethered by the quiet patience of those who look like they have been tamed.

### The part titles rhyme with the myth's movements

- **Structure** (Part One) — the world's form and the naming of what comes next. Ke names Anjiin; Dafyd assembles the new governance structure for the moiety.
- **Antithesis** (Part Two) — the failure of the old gods. The established powers of Ke's world cannot help; the established human leadership (Tonner, Jellit-as-Jellit) is broken; Ke must turn to a terrible alternative. Dafyd must turn to the swarm, to Andermus's security policing, to lamb sacks, and to Uuya's secret curriculum.
- **Pressure Drives Convergence** (Part Three) — Ke patiently merges into the mountain until the bird cannot see him. Dafyd and the swarm merge into the Carryx archive; Jessyn and Garral converge with the surviving "deathless" humans in the cave.
- **Dogs and Wolves** (Part Four) — Ke songs the bird into saddle. The part in which the rak-hund is, repeatedly, described as a loyal dog; in which Jellit-the-swarm is both captive and weapon; in which the distinction between domesticated instrument and wild adversary begins to blur on every front. The title asks the novel's central category question and the myth answers it: **the wolf can be ridden if you know its song.**
- **Kinds of Life** (Part Five) — the gathering of every kind of life onto Ash-Abbé's back, with the poignant note that Ke's own father refuses to come. The part in which Tonner dies (the father-figure of the original Anjiin scientific project, who cannot make the transition), and in which the disparate human remnants (at the palace, on World, in deep space) are gathered toward convergence.
- **Ducks and Rabbits** (Part Six) — ambiguity of perception (the gestalt-switch image), and Ke's terrible choice to remain in the "space between worlds" rather than enjoy the home he built. The title signals the book's refusal to resolve its ambiguities (is the swarm Else or is it her killer; is the rak-hund a loyal dog or a weapon; are humans the Carryx's prize or their prey; is Dafyd a savior or a collaborator), and the myth gives the final answer: the one who delivers his people cannot come down to live among them. He stays on the bird's back, singing.

### Ke's final refusal is Dafyd's doom

The Part Six panel is the most load-bearing. Ke cannot stop singing, cannot descend into the life he has made possible, because the moment he stops, Ash-Abbé will turn on those who rode it. This is the novel's bleakest self-diagnosis of Dafyd's position. He is the one who has embedded himself deepest in Carryx machinery — the factor, the lapdog, the keeper of the secret conspiracy, the one Uuya and everyone else is allowed to hate. The myth says flatly that he cannot come down. Even if the Carryx fall, even if the children one day lift the mask, the singer on the back of the great bird does not get to join the feast. Uuya, in Chapter 9, sees him for what he is and calls him by his aunt's name: Dory. By Part Six the reader is being told, in the myth's own voice, what becoming Dory will cost him.

### The missing pieces cue future books

Two named mythic seeds in the book are *not* filled in: **the Great Heron that split the sky** (a prior cosmogony underlying the Ke story — possibly the even deeper origin of whatever Anjiin's ancestors ran from, which Jessyn's Chapter 10 discovery hints may be literal galactic history) and **Shavas Rey the sleeping god who wakes to remake the world** (a Gallantist eschatology Dafyd wants the Uuya curriculum to fold in). Both are placed on the shelf with obvious intent. In a book where every myth panel is a key to a lock, the unopened myths are the ones the series will open later.

---

## Bottom Line

The Ke cycle is the book's thesis in mythic form. Humans are the passengers on a terrible bird whose song they have learned to mimic. Their survival depends on a singer who cannot dismount. The singer is Dafyd, the song is the curriculum Uuya is now editing, the great bird is the Carryx, and the new Anjiin on the other side of the void is whatever the lamb-sack children and their grandchildren make when the song finally stops. The fact that the myth is credited to Uuya in every epigraph tells the reader, if they're paying attention, that the resistance has already begun — on the page they are holding.
