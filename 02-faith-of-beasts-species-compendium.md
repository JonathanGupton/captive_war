### 2. Soft Lothark / Deep Lothark

The major Book 2 revelation: the Soft Lothark carry a second, hidden consciousness on their skin oils — the **Deep Lothark** — a Carryx-invisible collective mind that remembers what the Stone-Mind was and waits to remake it.

#### Physical Reminder

> "One of the Soft Lothark turned to look at her. The attention of the squat-bodied, long-limbed creature made Jessyn flinch away, her heart racing a little. She hadn't done anything to bring their wrath down on her, but the Lothark scared her anyway. She'd seen things like this one murder people and eat their own dead."
> -- Chapter 3

#### The Hidden Plasmid Channel (discovered by Tonner)

> Tonner covertly swabs a Soft Lothark guard, then observes: the Soft Lothark exchange large, information-dense plasmid-like packets by nibbling each other and consuming their dead — a second private communications channel invisible to the Carryx.
> -- Chapter 11 / Chapter 20 (this discovery is what gets Tonner killed)

#### First Contact with the Deep Lothark

> "Soft Lothark keep private reports sent as food." (Dafyd's draft threat on the Carryx symbol-board) — to which a Soft Lothark responds by altering the phrase to: "Humans and the Deep Lothark can help each other."
> -- Chapter 20 (the moment Dafyd realizes another captive species is also hiding a conspiracy)

#### What the Deep Lothark Is

> "A way of keeping something for ourselves. It is memory that belongs to all of us, and because we wear it on our skins, they don't find it inside our bodies. The Soft Lothark are loyal to the Carryx. They are the trusted animals of violence in the endless war. They would gladly die for the Carryx. The Deep Lothark is loyal to the Lothark, and it remembers how the Stone-Mind was built and waits to remake it again someday."
> -- Chapter 23

> "It has been a very long wait, but the Deep Lothark is all Lothark, so it can be patient when the individual cannot."
> -- Chapter 23

#### Relationship Between the Two Selves

> "Maybe it was. The exact dynamic between the murderous enforcers of the Carryx and the other self they traded back and forth on their lips and skin followed a logic that Dafyd hadn't seen before. He wasn't at all certain the Soft Lothark understood what the Deep Lothark was or what it was saying. To them, the conversations might be nothing more than flailing at the glowing shapes, unconscious of any reason for the actions they took. Some of them might not know how to read."
> -- Chapter 32

> "To do so would invite scrutiny, and so the Deep Lothark both can and cannot."
> -- Chapter 32 (answering whether the Deep Lothark can command Soft Lothark actions against Carryx will)

#### The Lothark Origin Myth

> "Only a few moieties are older than the Lothark. When the Carryx found us, we were smaller and we lived on a single world. The High Lothark and its Stone-Mind guided and bound us. And then the Carryx came. They hadn't mastered the Void Dragons then. Or the Amanthea. They didn't have knowledge then to lock a sky in place. They overcame us with Dirhaj which became obsolete when the Bau-hund were domesticated. There are no Dirhaj now."
> -- Chapter 23

#### Diaspora

> "We are on every world that our bodies can be made to tolerate. Over half of the Carryx worlds and colonies, we aid in holding. But if they chose to kill us, we would die. The dream of insurrection is a dream. These things that own us are capable of such casual violence that on a word, they would end us all and forget us before the next day. The Deep Lothark guards against that word, that ending, and it waits."
> -- Chapter 23

#### Historical Witness

> "You are asking about the Epikainot and the great loss. Yes, many Soft Lothark were there when it happened. The Epikainot were a powerful enemy. Clever and ruthless. The Carryx had found them only a few hundred years before, and their battles rivaled the conflict with the deathless, though they were not deathless themselves."
> -- Chapter 37

#### The War-Is-Forever Doctrine

> "There is no war with the Carryx. War is what they are. Enslavement is what they are. A state with no conflict is incomprehensible. Before the deathless enemy arrived, there was war. When the deathless die, there will be war. But the deathless never die. War will never end. We will be here in its mouth forever."
> -- Chapter 23

### 5. The Swarm → "Clae Audin"

The alien spy-weapon evolves dramatically in Book 2. It moves from a second host (Else Yannin, now dead) into **Jellit Kaul**, whom it consumes over the course of the novel, before reshaping the body into an entirely new woman: **Clae Audin**.

#### Composite Consciousness

> "In the dark prison of the swarm's consciousness, the dead man fights. Jellit is the only male it has taken, the only one who had already dedicated himself to violence, and the only one who carried the weight of that trauma on his soul."
> -- Chapter 2

> "Oh for fuck's sake, the dead man sneers in the chaos of the swarm's mind. Again? You've got more days-I'm-going-to-tell-him than Else had last cigarettes."
> -- Chapter 2 (Jellit's voice inside the swarm)

#### First High-Testosterone Host

> "For the first time, it feels the cognitive deformation of a high-testosterone environment."
> (Carried over from Book 1; in Book 2 becomes a persistent theme of Jellit's rage)

#### Archive Infiltration

In Chapter 16, Jellit/swarm interfaces with a remote Carryx archive node, filaments sprouting from his skin:

> "I can see everything."
> -- Chapter 16

#### The Great Realization (Chapter 27)

After Dafyd rejects the swarm in Else's remade body, the swarm introspects Else's memories from the inside and realizes she — and Jellit, and every human — were already a chorus of competing voices, not unified selves.

> "He was right. They're gone. I wasn't them. I killed them."
> -- Chapter 27

This inaugurates an autonomous self — neither Else, Jellit, nor Ameer.

#### The Birth of Clae Audin

#### Pheromone-Memory Manipulation

While the room filled with the scent of her manipulations, she grabbed onto a memory... Clae Audin's roots traveled back into time and memory. She became someone who had always been on the periphery, but always present."

#### Physical Manifestation

#### Remnant Fragments

### 7. The Deathless Enemy — The Great Revelation

Book 2 delivers the series' central revelation: the Carryx's great enemy includes **humans**, or beings extremely close to them. They appear to field two distinct forms:

**Form A** — Humanoid soldiers clad in black, regrowing living armor (the "Corvall" form).
**Form B** — Five-limbed silver "pentapus" creatures, seemingly sessile mechanisms that animate only when the ship powers up.

Clae's late-novel hypothesis: the deathless are a lichen-like symbiosis — a viral "template" that colonizes a substrate species (e.g., humans) and turns them into unkillable soldiers, with no independent consciousness of their own.

#### Jessyn's Discovery on "World"

> "This planet had been inhabited by the great and deathless enemy of the Carryx. The opposite side of a war that stretched out past the edge of history. Humans were the enemy. She was the enemy. And somehow—how was it possible?—the Carryx didn't know."
> -- Chapter 10

> "If the deathless enemy of the Carryx were humans, if the army standing against the empire were people like her, then maybe there was someplace to escape to."
> -- Chapter 10

#### The Secret Carried Back to the Moiety

> "Never tell Dafyd Alkhor the secret she'd discovered: The deathless enemy were just humans in weird black regrowing armor."
> -- Chapter 25 (Jessyn's interior — she must survive to tell Dafyd, so she must *not* get caught)

#### The Pentapus (Five-Tentacled Silver Form)

> "In among the corpses of men and women, alien flesh lay dead as well. Thin silver limbs that glistened in the light like salmon skin and met in a small central node. Something like octopuses, but with only five tentacles."
> -- Chapter 28

> "The five-armed alien nearest her was next. It didn't seem at first to be wearing clothing, but there were complicated rings near the base of each tentacle that might have been keratin shell that had grown there or artifacts put in place."
> -- Chapter 28

#### The Reanimating Dust-Black Form

> "At the far end of the corridor, the thing swam toward him. Its tentacles flowed with an eerie grace, twirling as it moved through the space. Only the two missing limbs gave it a sense of wrongness and unbalance."
> -- Chapter 28

> "Campar pulled himself to the black, misshapen thing and prodded it. Numbed as he was by the vacuum sheath, he couldn't be sure, but he thought the texture of the flesh was different as well. Harder, and somehow more mechanical."
> -- Chapter 28 (on the surviving creature — dust from a scraped sample reanimated inside its jar and nearly kills the team)

> "The deathless enemy is on the ship with us. I am not certain how it was damaged, but as it was sessile until the ship regained power, I believe it was very badly compromised."
> -- Chapter 28 (Vaudai)

#### Campar's Debrief

Campar confirms to Dafyd and Clae that the corpses inside the bridge were unmistakably human; the surviving pentapus acted more like a mechanism than an animal.
-- Chapter 35

#### Clae's Lichen Hypothesis

> "A lichen has an algae-like function that can pull energy from light and a mineral decomposer like a fungus that can mine nutrients out of the environment. Together, they're what makes soil out of stone... The algae and the fungal decomposer? Not genetically related. The same algae on a different substrate organism is a different lichen. You can mix and match. The deathless may be the same."
> -- Chapter 35

> "That humans are on the other side... That's not the same as being the great enemy. Not any more than I think these pentapods are the great enemy."
> -- Chapter 35

### 8. Corvall (Living Black Armor)

An individual whose species remains ambiguous. Corvall is a soldier of the anti-Carryx alliance encased in living, self-repairing black armor that is failing as it tries to regrow his missing limbs. Garral Pär identifies him as human-under-the-armor; Jessyn describes the armor itself as part of him.

#### Physical Description

> "Something had lumbered out... a black, asymmetric soldier-figure (one half a stick-thin wireframe) unfolded from the wall..."
> -- Chapter 12

> "Corvall held up his good hand, signaling them to stop. For a long moment, the black helmet scanned back and forth across the landscape."
> -- Chapter 21

> "She had the impression that the soldier and his living armor could have stayed there motionless as a stone for years conserving the last dregs of whatever fuel the suit and its occupant ran on."
> -- Chapter 17

#### Armament

> "Corvall drew his gun, aimed at a clump of bushes farther down the trail, and fired twice. Something hidden in the brush yelped and fled."
> -- Chapter 21

Demonstrates a small handheld weapon by blasting a hole through the cave wall. -- Chapter 12

#### Situation

> "Corvall was part of the army that fought the Carryx forces. They lost. He and a group of other soldiers were trying to escape. I don't know how many, but their ship was forced down, and he's the only one that's left."
> -- Chapter 14

> "They'll come hunting for Corvall, and they'll find him. He's not going to live through this. He knows that."
> -- Chapter 17

#### Equipment

Gifts Jessyn a black ceramic sabotage cylinder and a pistol:
> "The four stood together like they were about to pray for the dead, but instead of a candle, Corvall held out a black ceramic cylinder the size of a fist. Its cap had indentations around the edge to make it easier to turn. When Corvall put it in Jessyn's hand, it was surprisingly heavy."

### Key Revelations Carried by Book 2

1. **The Carryx Sovran is a replaceable hive-queen**, with hundreds of private creches across the empire's world-palaces. Decapitation does not work.
2. **The Soft Lothark carry a hidden second consciousness**, the Deep Lothark, on their skin oils — a millennia-old resistance unknown to the Carryx.
3. **The deathless enemy includes humans**, or beings that wear humans like a lichen wears its substrate — and the Carryx have never recognized the Anjiin moiety as the same species.
4. **The swarm has become an autonomous being** named Clae Audin, no longer merely a composite of dead hosts.
5. **The Carryx already lost once** — to the Epikainot, who destroyed the first homeworld and the first Sovran. The empire didn't break. It won't break to a second such strike, either.
