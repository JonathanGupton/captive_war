# Who Is Ekur-Tkalal Speaking To?

*Evaluating the hypothesis that the "final statement" interlocutor is a deathless interrogator*

---

## The hypothesis

The TMoG part epigraphs are all attributed to *"the final statement of Ekur-Tkalal, keeper-librarian of the human moiety of the Carryx."* The form is one-sided — only Ekur's words appear — but Ekur is plainly responding to questions and conditions imposed by an unseen interlocutor. The "deathless interrogator" hypothesis (suggested by the structural mirror to Carlon's interview of Garral in TFOB's final scene) reads the unseen interlocutor as a representative of the deathless enemy after the Carryx empire's fall.

This document evaluates that hypothesis against the actual text of all six "final statement" epigraphs in *The Mercy of Gods*.

---

## The full corpus — every epigraph in the "final statement" voice

### Part One (BEFORE)

> "You ask how many ages had the Carryx been fighting the long war? That is a meaningless question. The Carryx ruled the stars for epochs. We conquered the Ejia and Kurkst and outdreamt the Eyeless Ones. We burned the Logothetes until their worlds were windswept glass. **You wish to know of our first encounter with the enemy**, but it seems more likely to me that there were many first encounters spread across the face of distance and time in ways that simultaneity cannot map. The ending, though. I saw the beginning of that catastrophe. It was the abasement of an insignificant world that called itself Anjiin."

### Part Two (CATASTROPHE)

> "The truth is this: That which is, is. The Rak-hund execute our will. If there is any *because* in that, it is because we killed or sterilized all aspects of the Rak-hund that didn't, and we kept the remnant that did. The Gar of Estian, Kirikishun, Ouck, the seven Lek-Variable, the Whirl-Ghost. The same is true of all of them. If there is a moral question in that, it is the same moral question a species asks when its star novas and melts its world to glass.
>
> **When a primitive of your own kind cut a branch from a tree and carved the wood into a tool — an axe handle, a tentpole, whatever your will designed — you placed no moral judgment on the act, nor should you have. To do so would have been perverse. The tree had no power to stop you, and so it became a tool in your hand.**
>
> **What you did with a tree branch, we did with you and countless others before you.** *Why me?* is not something the universe ever answers."

### Part Three

> "**Your species is not the first we have encountered with the delusion that peace is a desirable state.** The Eklil of Hannabor spent half of their history composing scent-symphonies in praise of a state free from tension and pain that none of them ever experienced. The Mitria Salo worshipped the Point of Equanimity in defiance of all evidence that such a thing was impossible. **Your own people were in a constant generational argument over which group or subgroup, gender or culture or religion, suffered most at the hands of the others, as if the concept of justice were not narrative and abstract.**
>
> Over and over again, we found it in our subject species, this faith that a state of peace was not only possible, but desirable. That if only someone were clever enough or wise enough, a way could be found for everyone to be in comfort and satiety.
>
> This delusion has never been the error of the Carryx... 
>
> **The betrayer, though.** That one managed both. He could dream of perfection without being fettered by it. He desired peace and destroyed countless worlds to take it. He held both these ideas in his mind at the same time, and instead of this dissonance ripping him apart, it made him powerful. **If we had known that before it was too late we would have killed him. You should take note of that. You would be wise to kill him too.**"

### Part Four

> "The moieties have always been a project of exploration and learning. Not of preservation... Some species accepted the yoke, and some defied it. That which had utility was incorporated, that which had none was culled. Any being who has chosen to pluck weeds out of a garden has done the same. **You condemn us even as you follow our example, and with your foot on my throat, I applaud you. Which of us, then, has greater integrity?**"

### Part Five

> "He wasn't unique, or not more than any other individual being would be. He had no insights that changed the way I saw the order of things, he didn't have any particular philosophy that opened the doors of reality. He was one among many, with variations as any of them had. **The only thing that I think made him stand out at all was the depth of hatred that the others came to have for him.**"

### Part Six

> "The genius of the Carryx is that we brought the peculiar and often idiosyncratic brilliance of a thousand different species into a central system of control... 
>
> **Humans are structureless. They live in conflict with themselves and each other. Their great genius is rationalization: lying into mirrors until they bully and seduce themselves into things they would never otherwise do.** They are creatures of self-delusion, regret, and desire. That is their way, and for a time we harnessed it as we did everything.
>
> For a time."

## What the text tells us about the interlocutor

### Confirmed properties

These read off the surface text of the epigraphs:

1. **The interlocutor asks questions and Ekur answers.** *"You ask how many ages…"* / *"You wish to know of our first encounter with the enemy."* This is an interrogation, not a soliloquy.

2. **The interlocutor is the victor.** *"You condemn us even as you follow our example, and with your foot on my throat, I applaud you."* (Part Four.) The Carryx empire has fallen by the time Ekur is speaking. He is in custody.

3. **The interlocutor is a tool-using species with a recognizable evolutionary "primitive" past involving carved wood.** *"When a primitive of your own kind cut a branch from a tree and carved the wood into a tool — an axe handle, a tentpole, whatever your will designed…"* (Part Two.) Axe handles and tentpoles are very specific tool examples — physical-bipedal, planet-surface, vegetable-resource technology. Not octopoid, not energy-creature, not asymmetric-spacetime entities. Tool-using sapients with hands and a forest.

4. **The interlocutor's species behaves in recognizably human ways at the cultural level.** *"Your own people were in a constant generational argument over which group or subgroup, gender or culture or religion, suffered most at the hands of the others, as if the concept of justice were not narrative and abstract."* (Part Three.) This sentence describes identity politics. It is so culturally specific to the human reader that it functions as a tell. Whatever the interlocutor is, their society sorts itself into groups by culture, gender, and religion and argues about which groups suffered most.

5. **The Carryx have personally subjugated the interlocutor's people.** *"What you did with a tree branch, we did with you and countless others before you."* (Part Two.) "We did with you" — second-person plural. The interlocutor's people were a Carryx tool. Humans were a Carryx moiety.

6. **The interlocutor knows about, and is in a position to act on, "the betrayer."** *"You would be wise to kill him too."* (Part Three.) Ekur is recommending that the interlocutor kill someone — almost certainly Dafyd Alkhor, given the description ("desired peace and destroyed countless worlds to take it"). The interlocutor is therefore in a position where they could plausibly reach Dafyd — i.e., post-war, the betrayer is alive and still inside their reach.

7. **The interlocutor has an interest in the deepest history of the Carryx-vs-deathless war, and in what happened at Anjiin specifically.** *"You wish to know of our first encounter with the enemy"* (Part One). *"It was the abasement of an insignificant world that called itself Anjiin"* (also Part One). This is an interlocutor probing Carryx history with Anjiin as a focal point.

8. **Ekur describes humans in the third person, anthropologically, while addressing the interlocutor.** *"Humans are structureless. They live in conflict with themselves and each other."* (Part Six.) The grammar separates "humans" from "you" — at least in this passage, Ekur is talking *about* humans *to* someone who is asking about humans.

### What's notable by its absence

- The text never names the interlocutor.
- The text never uses the word "deathless" in any of the interlocutor's implied questions or in Ekur's responses about them. Ekur uses *"the enemy"* and *"that catastrophe."*
- Ekur never grants the interlocutor the dignity of a species name. Compare: he names *the Ejia, Kurkst, Eyeless Ones, Logothetes, Eklil of Hannabor, Mitria Salo, Gar of Estian, Kirikishun, Ouck, Lek-Variable, Whirl-Ghost, Temperantiae of Au, Janantie, Phylarchs of Astrdeim, Void Dragons, Sinyas and Vau* as he goes — but not the people sitting across from him.

## Match to the Carlon/Garral framing in TFOB's epilogue

TFOB closes with Garral being interviewed by **Carlon**, an old man in a clean facility somewhere in the deathless civilization. The framing is identical to Ekur's interlocutor:

- A captive who has been brought from the war's aftermath.
- An interrogator who asks calm, leading questions.
- The questions converge on **what happened at Anjiin** as the central historical concern.

Carlon's last on-page line:

> "Why don't you and I start at the beginning. Tell me about this planet you say you came from. You call it Anjiin?"

Ekur's first on-page line in the "final statement":

> "You ask how many ages had the Carryx been fighting the long war?"

Both are responses to an interrogator's *"start at the beginning"* prompt. Both pivot to Anjiin as the central event. The structural rhyme is hard to read as accidental.

Carlon is human-presenting (clinical Earth-style sofas, "Medical / Cafeteria / Administration" signage, an Anjiin-cognate language family), and his civilization is the deathless civilization Garral has just been delivered to. The Carlon scene is the only on-page model of "deathless interrogation" the books provide.

## Evidence that supports the deathless-interrogator hypothesis

### Structural

- **The Carlon/Garral framing is the only matching template** the books offer for Ekur's situation. There is no other "after the war, an interrogator asks a captive about Anjiin" scene in either book. If the form is supposed to evoke a specific kind of conversation, it evokes Carlon's.
- **"You wish to know of our first encounter with the enemy"** identifies the interlocutor *with* the enemy. Ekur isn't being asked about an abstract historical curiosity; he's being asked, by the deathless, about the deathless.
- **"with your foot on my throat"** matches the only on-page victor of the Carryx-vs-deathless war whose victory is forecast in the books — Vaudai's prognosis is that the Carryx win, but Dafyd's working plan is that the deathless invade and *"do to these bastards what they did to Anjiin"*. The "final statement" comes from a future where Dafyd's plan succeeded.

### Linguistic

- **"What you did with a tree branch, we did with you"** is consistent with a deathless human-substrate interlocutor. The deathless wear human bodies (Corvall, Carlon). The "tree branch / axe handle / tentpole" is exactly the toolkit a human civilization would have in its evolutionary memory.
- **The cultural snapshot in Part Three** — "your own people were in a constant generational argument over which group or subgroup, gender or culture or religion, suffered most" — is functionally a description of humans. The deathless current-substrate is human.

### Strategic

- **Ekur tells the interlocutor to kill Dafyd.** The deathless are the only entity in the books with an organized motive to receive that recommendation. The Carryx are gone; Anjiin's surviving humans don't have organized military force; only the deathless have the capacity AND the motive (Dafyd is the one human captive of the Carryx who was, briefly, in a position to advance their cause as a fifth column — and to outlive his usefulness).
- **The interest in Anjiin** is consistent with the deathless's belated discovery — first via Garral, then via the corridor-image broadcast from Rickar's body — that Anjiin existed and had been subjugated.

## Evidence that complicates or weakens the hypothesis

### The "describing humans" problem

Part Six describes humans in the third person — *"Humans are structureless. They live in conflict with themselves and each other."* If the interlocutor is human, Ekur could plausibly use "you" instead. He doesn't.

Two readings:

1. **Compatible with the hypothesis.** Ekur is describing the *substrate* (humans-as-evolved-species) to a deathless interlocutor whose human substrate is real but who is not, in their own self-conception, "human." The deathless are millions of years old and have worn other substrates. A deathless interrogator might naturally distinguish between *the species we currently inhabit* and *us*.

2. **Argues against the hypothesis.** A simpler reading is that the interlocutor is a non-human victor learning about humans from Ekur. (But then who? No non-human civilization in either book has the capability to overthrow the Carryx.)

The most parsimonious reading: **the deathless interlocutor distinguishes themselves from their substrate the way Clae distinguishes herself from Jellit's body.** Clae says outright in TFOB Ch 36 that she is *"not"* human in the species sense — she is an instruction-set running on a human chassis. A deathless interrogator, especially an old and self-aware one like Carlon, would use the same grammar. *"Humans are structureless"* would then read as Ekur's needled jab — *here is what your wetware actually is, when you stop dressing it up.*

### "You condemn us even as you follow our example"

This is the one line that does NOT fit a Carryx-style or deathless-style victor cleanly. The deathless have been a millions-of-years war machine; the books characterize them as cold, sessile-when-unpowered, behaviorally indistinguishable from a Carryx mirror image. Why would they *condemn* the Carryx? "What is, is" applies to them too.

A possible reading: **the deathless interrogator is human-influenced enough — by inhabiting human substrates over an indefinite span — to have absorbed human moral grammar.** Carlon is described as warm, courteous, smiling, with "laugh lines [that] reached from his eyes to his temples." That is not a cold-pragmatist interrogator. The deathless instruction-set may be unchanged, but its current human substrate provides moral coloration.

### "Final statement"

The attribution always says *"final statement,"* not *"interview"* or *"interrogation."* This frames Ekur as someone delivering a last testament — possibly under duress, possibly knowing he is about to be killed, possibly transcribed posthumously. "Final statement" is consistent with interrogation (a captive's last words) but is also consistent with a Carryx-style ritual final declaration before voluntary death (compare: the way the Sovran's old generation is killed by her daughter in TFOB Ch 36 as a routine, expected final ceremony).

### The "abasement of Anjiin" framing

Ekur calls Anjiin *"the beginning of that catastrophe."* If the interlocutor is the deathless, Ekur is essentially saying *"you won because of Anjiin."* That is consistent with the lichen theory: the Carryx took Anjiin thinking it was wild substrate; in fact (per Clae) humans were a deathless-aligned substrate the deathless themselves had lost track of; bringing the Anjiin moiety into the world-palace introduced the swarm and, eventually, Rickar's death-burst. The Carryx imported the catastrophe by mistake. The interlocutor has won because the Carryx made one categorical error about one substrate species, and that error compounded.

This frame *only makes sense* if the interlocutor knows the deathless story. A non-deathless interlocutor would not understand why Anjiin specifically was the catastrophe. The phrasing therefore actively supports the hypothesis.

---

## Verdict

**The hypothesis is well-supported by the text.** The single most parsimonious reading of all six "final statement" epigraphs is:

> Ekur-Tkalal, in custody after the fall of the Carryx empire, is being interviewed by a deathless interrogator — almost certainly the same Carlon-style figure who interviews Garral in TFOB's epilogue, or a successor of his. The interrogator wears a human substrate, asks calm leading questions, and is centrally interested in Anjiin and "the betrayer" (Dafyd Alkhor) because the deathless inherited their victory from events the Carryx triggered there.

The strongest individual evidence is, in order:

1. **"With your foot on my throat"** — Ekur is captive of the war's victor. The deathless are the only forecast victor.
2. **"You wish to know of our first encounter with the enemy"** — the interlocutor's interest is in the Carryx-vs-deathless war, identifying them with the enemy.
3. **The Carlon/Garral structural rhyme** — only matching template in either book.
4. **"You would be wise to kill him too" (about Dafyd)** — the interlocutor is in a position to act on the recommendation, which only the deathless plausibly are.
5. **"What you did with a tree branch, we did with you"** — the interlocutor is a tool-using planet-surface biped whose evolutionary toolkit matches humans.
6. **"You condemn us even as you follow our example"** — the interlocutor has moral grammar, consistent with Carlon's warm-old-man register and with a long-time human substrate.

The strongest counter-pressure is Part Six's third-person-about-humans grammar, but Clae's lichen theory provides a clean reconciliation: the deathless distinguish themselves from their substrate the way Clae distinguishes herself from Jellit's body.

### Alternative readings considered

- **Non-deathless human victor.** Ruled weak. The 4,000-captive Anjiin moiety has no military capability to overthrow the Carryx without the deathless behind them.
- **A Carryx successor.** The Carryx have a private creche and can replace a Sovran (TFOB Ch 36); but they would not put Ekur "with your foot on my throat" — and they would not need to ask about their own first encounter with the deathless.
- **A third party never named.** Possible but unparsimonious; the books offer no evidence for any other galactic-scale victor.

### What the verdict implies for Book 3

If Ekur's interlocutor is a deathless interrogator, the TMoG epigraphs are the book's *epilogue projected forward as foreshadowing*. Each of them is a sentence Ekur will eventually say, after the Carryx have fallen, in conversation with the people the human moiety helped bring to victory. Several specific predictions follow:

- Dafyd Alkhor survives the war and is then in deathless custody, where his execution is being deliberated. Ekur is recommending it.
- The deathless win because of compounding consequences from Anjiin specifically — Rickar's broadcast, Garral's information, possibly the Anjiin-substrate's value as previously-lost deathless wetware.
- Ekur survives long enough to be interrogated. He gives one or more "final statements" — meaning the Carryx allow him to speak before whatever ending he comes to.
- The deathless interrogator is Carlon-shaped: warm, patient, human-substrate, interested in the deepest history of the war.

Whether Carlon himself is the interlocutor or a successor is unrecoverable from the present text.
