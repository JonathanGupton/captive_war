# Void Dragons — Analysis with Textual Evidence

A close reading of every Void Dragon reference in *The Faith of Beasts*, with the one anchoring reference from *The Mercy of Gods* that supplies the working definition. Six in-text mentions in TFOB (three direct, three of the linked "void tendrils"), plus the single TMOG epigraph reference that names what Void Dragons are. Together they form the most coherent picture the series has so far given of one of the Carryx empire's foundational technologies.

---

## 1. The Anchoring Definition (TMOG, Part Six Epigraph)

The only place the series explicitly tells us what a Void Dragon **is** appears in Book 1, in the Part Six epigraph attributed to *"the final statement of Ekur-Tkalal, keeper-librarian of the human moiety of the Carryx"* — the same Ekur-Tkalal who narrates the Eelie/Ayayeh debacle and who later interrogates the captive humans after the Anjiin conquest.

> "The genius of the Carryx is that we brought the peculiar and often idiosyncratic brilliance of a thousand different species into a central system of control. We conquered asymmetric space by harnessing the birth shrieks of the Temperantiae of Au. We built machines of loyalty by harvesting the poem-patterns of Janantie moss gardens. We built world-palaces designed by the Phylarchs of Astrdeim, **communication networks woven from the bodies of the Void Dragons that eat the foam at the edge of black holes**, battleships strengthened by the living shells that choked the oceans of Sinyas and Vau."
> — *The Mercy of Gods*, Part Six epigraph

Three load-bearing facts come out of this single sentence:

1. **Habitat.** Void Dragons "eat the foam at the edge of black holes." This is precise enough to be evocative: spacetime foam (the quantum-scale froth of virtual particles and metric fluctuations near an event horizon) is their food source. They live where almost nothing else can.
2. **Function.** Their *bodies* — not their songs, not their shrieks, not their patterns — are woven into Carryx **communication networks**. They are infrastructure. The Carryx empire's nervous system runs through them.
3. **Mode of acquisition.** They are listed alongside the Temperantiae of Au, the Janantie, the Phylarchs of Astrdeim, and the shells of Sinyas and Vau — species the Carryx *harvested or harnessed*, almost always after destroying or reducing them. This is the Carryx's signature pattern: take a species' one peculiar genius and convert it into a permanent imperial organ.

Everything in *The Faith of Beasts* extends this picture. Nothing contradicts it.

## 2. The TFOB References (Six, in Narrative Order)

### Reference A — Chapter 18 (Part Three): "void tendril probability arcs"

**Setting.** Rickar is on a Carryx warship in transit, the Budon of Luus dying around him in the common room. He has discovered that Vaudai (a giant slug-thing whose species' strategy is *make a mistake and watch your species burn*) has access to the Carryx tactical display and will share it freely. The display is the Carryx's holographic schematic of the local solar system.

> "The display was the same volumetric spread of light that the Carryx used for their version of a written language, only instead of abstract shapes, Vaudai had a schematic version of the solar system they were in from the local star in the center to the skin of the heliosphere. At scale, it would have been an emptiness punctuated by dots of light too small to see, but with the overlays that Vaudai conjured up, Rickar could track the twelve major planets, the clouds of asteroids in two distinct belts, and—more importantly to him—the positions of the Carryx ships and the enemy fleet. Thin cones defined something Vaudai called exclusion-and-control fields, wider ones fundamental decoherence zones, and a few **hair-thin curving lines void tendril probability arcs**."
> — TFOB Ch 18

**Significance.** Void tendrils show up in the Carryx tactical display *as probability arcs* — not deterministic vectors. Whatever they are, the Carryx themselves do not always know exactly where their tendrils are. Combined with later passages, this hair-thin probabilistic geometry is consistent with something that exists in (or peers through) a non-Euclidean substrate — likely asymmetric space.

This is also the chapter that establishes the visual/operational vocabulary the rest of the book uses: "exclusion-and-control fields," "fundamental decoherence zones," and "void tendril probability arcs" become Vaudai's standing legend for the war.

### Reference B — Chapter 19 (Part Three): "A tender of the Void Dragons in Caarlast"

**Setting.** Surur-Tlassen, the regulator-librarian — "the highest of the librarians in the empire" — making his daily report to the Sovran in her bath. The chapter is a careful, reverent portrait of the Carryx empire's administrative top of mind. Surur passes upward only what he judges worth her notice. Among his "few instances where some smaller-grained incident seemed of interest":

> "**A tender of the Void Dragons in Caarlast had been inhabited by larvae but not died, and now claimed to have insight into a new technique to deploy void tendrils.** An enemy ship in Ashtin-Kah system had fought against the third dactyl of the seventh limb of the three hundred and fifty-second exploratory body and failed to destroy itself when the enemy fled, and the subjugator-librarian of the dactyl was preparing animals of service to examine it."
> — TFOB Ch 19

**Significance.** This single sentence is the densest single piece of Void Dragon worldbuilding in the duology. Several distinct facts:

- **A specialized caste exists: "tenders" of the Void Dragons.** Confirmed in the Jellit passage (Ref C) as "a whole division of Carryx that just tend to them." This is a permanent imperial occupation, not an ad hoc assignment.
- **Caarlast is one site where Void Dragons (or their tenders) are kept.** It joins the imperial geography as a Dragon-tending world. (See `02-faith-of-beasts-locations-compendium.md` §"Caarlast.")
- **The tender survived a process that is normally fatal.** "Inhabited by larvae but not died" implies that being inhabited is ordinarily lethal — the survival is the noteworthy fact that earns the line a place in Surur's report to the Sovran. The text does not specify whose larvae (Carryx? Void Dragon? other?), what species the tender is (Carryx, or one of the empire's animal moieties), or whether the inhabitation is a deliberate process or an incident. The only stated downstream effect is that the survivor "claimed to have insight into a new technique to deploy void tendrils." Whether the larval inhabitation produced the insight, or merely co-occurred with it, the text leaves open. (The word "larvae" appears only here in either book, so there is no other passage to triangulate against.)
- **Critical link: the same tender claims insight into deploying *void tendrils*.** This is the textual hinge that ties the Void Dragons to the void tendrils. They are not the same entity, but they are close enough in the imperial taxonomy that a Dragon-tender's expertise translates directly into tendril-deployment technique. The most parsimonious reading: **void tendrils issue from, are extruded by, or are filaments of the Void Dragons themselves** — the way a deep-sea polyp extends fishing tendrils into the dark. Dragons remain anchored at black-hole edges; tendrils reach outward through asymmetric space to scout target systems.
- **The fact reaches the Sovran herself.** Voluntary reporting upward indicates Dragon-tending is sufficiently high-priority that even idiosyncratic technique innovations percolate to the empire's central organ.

The Sovran does not respond to this item in her returned directives — her attention is on the captured enemy ship. The Caarlast tender's innovation is left unjudged. But the very fact that Surur passed it up tells us how the Carryx weight the technology.

### Reference C — Chapter 20 (Part Three): Jellit on the Tendrils as Living Probes

**Setting.** Jellit is no longer Jellit. His body has been inhabited by a Carryx archive-spy ("the swarm"), a network the conspirators have inserted into the Carryx information system. Dafyd is meeting with the swarm to extract intelligence. The swarm is exhausted, sliding in and out of focus as it processes the empire's archive. As Dafyd is leaving, the spy volunteers an aside:

> "'Yes,' he said, but with a vagueness that meant he was only half aware. '**They use something called void tendrils to spy on planets they haven't been to yet. There's a whole division of Carryx that just tend to them. I think they're almost alive. The void tendrils, I mean. They talk about them like they're adaptable in a way that…**' Jellit's attention drifted back away."
> — TFOB Ch 20

**Significance.** This is the cleanest English-language summary of the void tendril role anywhere in the duology, and it confirms the pattern from Reference B:

- **Function: pre-arrival scouting.** Tendrils spy on planets the Carryx have not yet visited. This matches the TMOG Chapter 7 arrival at Anjiin, where "early void tendrils had scouted and laid down" the beacons that the colony ships followed in.
- **A tending caste.** "A whole division of Carryx that just tend to them." Confirms Surur's report (Ref B) and tells us this is a non-trivial fraction of the Carryx workforce.
- **"I think they're almost alive."** The swarm — itself a manufactured Carryx archive-spy — judges them living. They are "adaptable in a way that…" The sentence fragments before the comparison can be made. The implication: tendrils adapt in ways that surprise even the Carryx archive's own self-description.
- **Critical inference.** If the tendrils are extensions of the Dragons (Ref B's Caarlast-tender link), then "almost alive" and "adaptable" describe the Dragons' reach as much as their tendrils. The swarm is reporting an emergent intelligence in the Carryx's communication-and-scouting substrate.

### Reference D — Chapter 23 (Part Four): The Lothark Chronology

**Setting.** Dafyd's first long conversation with the Deep Lothark, the secret hive-mind that hides inside the Soft Lothark's body chemistry and has waited millennia for an opportunity against the Carryx. The Deep Lothark is establishing its own history.

> "Only a few moieties are older than the Lothark. When the Carryx found us, we were smaller and we lived on a single world. The High Lothark and its Stone-Mind guided and bound us. And then the Carryx came. **They hadn't mastered the Void Dragons then. Or the Amanthea. They didn't have knowledge then to lock a sky in place. They overcame us with Dirhaj which became obsolete when the Bau-hund were domesticated. There are no Dirhaj now.**"
> — TFOB Ch 23

**Significance.** The single most important historical statement about the Void Dragons in the duology. From the Deep Lothark's frame:

- **The Lothark are among the oldest moieties in the empire.** "Only a few moieties are older."
- **Void Dragons are a *late* Carryx acquisition.** They did not yet possess them when they conquered the Lothark. The Carryx instead used "Dirhaj" — now-extinct — supplemented later by domesticated Bau-hund.
- **Dragon mastery is grouped with three other late capabilities:** the Amanthea (unknown elsewhere) and "the knowledge to lock a sky in place." The "lock a sky" phrase has direct lexical support in TMOG, where the swarm's interior narration (Ch 21) names the geostationary grid covering the Carryx homeworld as **"the veil of drones that locks the planet's sky."** The same kind of grid — "stippled with dots… frame locked with the surface" (Nöl/Synnia, Ch 8); "the glowing, regular grid that still surrounded the planet like the bars of a jail cell" (Dafyd on Anjiin, Ch 9); "A grid of tiny black dots like the ones that had come to humble Anjiin" (Dafyd on the world-palace, Ch 12); "the grid that surrounded the world prison like a net holding a marble" (Dafyd, Ch 31) — is the standard Carryx installation on every world we see them control. The Ayayeh subjugation (Ekur-Tkalal, Ch 15) shows the same technology in active deployment: "the drone grid identified and destroyed the remaining Eelie," and afterward "the drones withered and fell into the atmosphere of Ayayeh, burning one by one." Reading the Deep Lothark's "lock a sky in place" as the drone-veil capability is therefore the most economical inference: same verb, same Carryx context, attested as standing infrastructure on every Carryx-held world from Anjiin to the homeworld. (The text leaves residual ambiguity — even the swarm once calls the orbital layer "ships or drones or organisms" — but no other TMOG technology fits the "lock a sky" phrase nearly as well.) All three escalations — Dragons, Amanthea, sky-lock — are presented as roughly contemporaneous.
- **The acquisition pattern is generational.** Dirhaj → Bau-hund → Void Dragons + Amanthea + sky-locking. Each stage obsoletes the prior one. The Dragons sit at or near the top of the empire's escalation ladder.

This is also the only TFOB reference that uses the capitalization "Void Dragons" outside a battle context — the Deep Lothark speaks of them as a category of *thing the Carryx own*, comparable to the Amanthea, and worth naming because their mastery marked a turning point in imperial capability.

### Reference E — Chapter 26 (Part Four): "The void tendrils had only revealed [Jurupe] recently"

**Setting.** Subjugator-librarian POV, omniscient interior. The third dactyl of the seventh limb of the 352nd exploratory body has just split off from its damaged enemy command-ship target in Ashtin-Kah and is moving into Jurupe system to attack a previously-undiscovered enemy world.

> "That the Carryx were aware of Jurupe would be a surprise to the deathless enemy. **The void tendrils had only revealed it recently.** The security the local enemy had thought to keep by staying silent and small had failed already. The ecology of the world teemed with life, including hundreds of cities and townships."
> — TFOB Ch 26

**Significance.** A direct, on-page demonstration of the tendrils' strategic function: revealing systems the enemy thought were hidden. Jurupe is a populated world the deathless had successfully kept off the Carryx star-charts until void tendrils discovered it. This is the same scouting function described in TMOG Ch 7 (Anjiin) and Ch 14 (Ayayeh), and the same one Jellit's swarm describes in Reference C.

Two implications worth pulling out:
1. **The tendrils are slow.** Surveillance "had only revealed it recently" — Jurupe was a deliberately quiet, low-signal target, and the tendrils still found it. Whether "recently" is hours, years, or centuries on the Carryx timescale is unclear, but the tendrils' eventual penetration of even a deliberately stealthy enemy holding seems inevitable, given enough time.
2. **The deathless enemy doesn't fully know where the tendrils have looked.** The Carryx's information advantage in this engagement comes from the tendrils having seen Jurupe before the enemy realized they had been seen.

### Reference F — Chapter 29 (Part Five): The Battle of Jurupe — Void Dragons in Combat

**Setting.** The climax of the Jurupe campaign. Rickar has just been loaded into a fleshy "evacuation pod" in the silver body of a Carryx ship; the captive animals are being shipped out as the engagement begins. The narrative cuts to the omniscient battle view:

> "Across the full volume of the system, the battle began. Thousands of hardened missiles flew from the enemy ships, dancing and darting in attempts to defy the Carryx trackers. Beams of coherent light moved through the dark, seeking and burning. Fields that mimicked the primordial energy of the birth of the universe sought to swamp the enemy's dampening counter fields and annihilate them utterly. **In the heart of the Carryx ship, Void Dragons woke and remade the nature of space, narrowing the degrees of freedom with which the enemy could travel. A few enemy ships set up fields to counter them that caused the Void Dragons to screech their distress like claws across the subjugator-librarian's mind.**"
> — TFOB Ch 29

**Significance.** The single most important *operational* description of Void Dragons in the duology. Several mechanics fall out of this passage:

- **Location: the heart of the Carryx ship.** Void Dragons are carried inside warships as core systems — confirmation that the empire's "communication networks woven from the bodies of the Void Dragons" (TMOG epigraph) have been miniaturized or distributed enough that individual battle-cruisers carry their own. Whether each ship has a single Dragon, a partial Dragon, a budded Dragon-tendril, or a network node is undetermined.
- **They sleep.** "Void Dragons woke." This is consistent with a living organism, and consistent with Reference D's framing of them as *creatures* rather than fields or constructs. The verb "woke" is the same the text uses for the Carryx's intelligent sub-systems (nodes, half-minds), not for inert weapons.
- **Combat function: spatial geometry warfare.** They "remade the nature of space, narrowing the degrees of freedom with which the enemy could travel." They do not destroy enemy ships directly. They constrain the enemy's possible-movement manifold, removing maneuver options. This is the combat application of an entity that natively eats spacetime foam — an organism whose normal feeding is the manipulation of the local metric.
- **They can be countered.** "A few enemy ships set up fields to counter them" — the deathless enemy has a known counter-protocol. The Carryx's reality-warping is not absolute.
- **They feel pain, and the pain is telepathically broadcast.** "Caused the Void Dragons to screech their distress like claws across the subjugator-librarian's mind." This is the most striking single clause. It tells us:
  - The Dragons are conscious enough to suffer.
  - Their suffering propagates into the librarian's mind directly — i.e., the Dragon-librarian connection is not merely instrumental, it is neural / mental in some way.
  - The connection is bidirectional. The Carryx librarian who controls the ship feels the Dragon's distress as if it were happening to them.
  - The simile "like claws" carries both the pain's intensity and a hint that the librarian experiences it as an attack rather than a signal — the Dragon doesn't choose to scream; the field-counter wrenches the scream out of it.

This passage is also the only place in either book where a Void Dragon is *characterized* — given an interior life with which the reader is asked to sympathize. Two sentences earlier the prose has been cataloging missiles and beams. Then the Dragons wake, do something nearly metaphysical, and are tortured. The juxtaposition is deliberate.

## 3. Synthesis

### What a Void Dragon is

Drawing the six TFOB references and the TMOG epigraph together:

- **A living organism.** Not a machine, not a field, not a construct. They wake, they suffer, they "screech distress," they have to be *tended*.
- **Native habitat: the edge of black holes.** Their food is the spacetime foam at the event horizon — quantum-scale fluctuations of the metric. The Carryx have somehow extracted them from this habitat (or built channels for them to operate without it).
- **Native ability: manipulation of spatial geometry.** What they do for food (consume foam) is what they do in war (remake the nature of space, narrowing degrees of freedom). The combat capability is the feeding behavior weaponized.
- **Acquired late by the Carryx.** Post-Lothark conquest. Mastery of the Dragons is grouped with mastery of the Amanthea and the "knowledge to lock a sky in place" — late-stage capabilities that constitute a generational escalation from the now-obsolete Dirhaj and the merely-domesticated Bau-hund.

### The Dragon / Tendril relationship

The textual evidence supports — though does not strictly prove — that **void tendrils are extensions of the Void Dragons**, not a separate species or technology:

- A tender of the Void Dragons gains "insight into a new technique to deploy void tendrils" (Ref B). A Dragon-tender's hands-on expertise transfers directly to tendril-deployment.
- Both are tended by "a whole division of Carryx" (Ref C).
- Both are described as "almost alive" (Ref C tendrils, Ref F Dragons explicitly).
- Tendrils manifest as "hair-thin curving lines" with "probability arcs" on tactical displays (Ref A) — consistent with non-localized appendages of an entity rooted in non-Euclidean space.
- Tendrils' core function (scouting through space the Carryx have not visited) is what the TMOG epigraph attributes to Dragon "communication networks": the Dragons' bodies woven across systems.

The simplest model: a Void Dragon sits anchored at a black-hole edge (or in a deep Carryx structure that simulates one); its tendrils are the long, fine extensions of its body, threaded through asymmetric space, used both for empire-wide communication and for forward scouting. *Tendrils do the looking; Dragons in ship-cores do the spatial warping.* Both functions follow from one organism's native relationship with the metric of spacetime.

### Functional roles in the empire

| Role | Source | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| FTL communication substrate | TMOG epigraph | Bodies "woven into communication networks" |
| Pre-arrival scouting (via tendrils) | TMOG Ch 7, TFOB Ch 20, Ch 26 | Reveals enemy worlds the Carryx haven't visited |
| Combat reality-warping | TFOB Ch 29 | Carried in ship-cores; constrains enemy maneuver |
| Tactical-display infrastructure | TFOB Ch 18 | "Probability arcs" visible to Vaudai's display |

### The Carryx's escalation ladder (per the Deep Lothark)

```
  Dirhaj  →  Bau-hund domesticated  →  Void Dragons mastered
                                       Amanthea mastered
                                       Knowledge to lock a sky in place
```

The Lothark's own subjugation predates the Dragons. By the time the Carryx took Anjiin (TMOG / TFOB era), the Dragons had been imperial infrastructure long enough that void tendrils had already laid down beacons in the Anjiin system years before the colony ships arrived. The Carryx empire described to the human captives is the *post-Dragon* empire — an empire whose nervous system, scouting reach, and combat reality-shaping all run through the same harvested species.

### Sentience, suffering, and the Carryx's pattern

The Jurupe-battle passage (Ref F) carries unusual moral weight. The Dragons "screech their distress" — they feel and broadcast pain. The Carryx have built their core military and communication infrastructure out of an organism that suffers when used against a sufficiently sophisticated enemy.

This is consistent with the imperial pattern Ekur-Tkalal proudly catalogs in the TMOG epigraph: the Temperantiae's "birth shrieks" were *harnessed* (the screams powering FTL), the Janantie's "poem-patterns" were *harvested*, the shells of Sinyas and Vau were *repurposed* from animals that drowned in their own oceans. The Void Dragons' role is the same. The empire is built from the captive utility of conscious beings.

Whether the Dragons are bred, captured, traded with, or merely *contained* at black-hole edges is not in the text. We know only that they exist, that they live, that they hurt, and that the Carryx have made themselves their masters.

### Open questions

- **Are the Dragons a single species or a category?** "Void Dragons" is plural and capitalized, like "Carryx" or "Phylarchs." Internal naming custom suggests a species-level term.
- **Do they have a homeworld?** "Eat the foam at the edge of black holes" suggests they may have evolved at horizons rather than on a planet. If so, their conquest by the Carryx would involve crossing into one of the most physically extreme environments in the universe — perhaps the same kind of operation as "lock[ing] a sky in place."
- **Are tendrils discrete organisms or limbs?** The most economical reading is: limbs of a Dragon, with each Dragon's tendrils stretching across many systems via asymmetric space. But the text never confirms this.
- **What is the Carryx's leverage over them?** Other harvested species in Ekur-Tkalal's catalog were destroyed or reduced before being used. The Dragons' ongoing tending — a permanent caste — implies a living, ongoing relationship rather than a one-shot harvest. They may be the rare imperial subject the Carryx cannot afford to consume.
- **The Amanthea.** Named in the same breath as the Dragons (Ref D) as a late mastery. Nothing else is said. The Amanthea may be a parallel infrastructure species (perhaps providing the "knowledge to lock a sky in place"); they may be the empire's other reality-warping organism. Whatever they are, they are co-equal in the Lothark's chronology with the Dragons.

## 5. Bottom Line

Void Dragons are the Carryx empire's central nervous system and its in-ship reality-warpers — a single species of organism, native to the edges of black holes, whose body-tendrils stretch through asymmetric space to scout enemy worlds and whose ship-borne members can constrict the geometry of local space in combat. They were acquired late in Carryx history (after the Lothark, before the Anjiin era), they require a permanent tending caste, they are conscious enough to suffer, and their suffering broadcasts directly into the minds of the Carryx librarians who command them.

In a series whose recurring image is the Carryx's making of empire from harvested life, the Void Dragons are the most sweeping example: an organism whose feeding behavior on the most extreme physical environment in the universe has been turned into the imperial communications backbone, the strategic surveillance system, and a battlefield-scale weapon — all at once.
