# Rickar Daumatin — Character Arc in *The Faith of Beasts*

*The Captive's War, Book 2: The Faith of Beasts — James S.A. Corey*

---

## Snapshot

Rickar enters *The Faith of Beasts* as one of the three surviving members of the Dyan research group — conscripted off the Carryx world-palace before the book opens, sent with Campar (and, Campar's new lover, Ghati) aboard a warship in *"the third dactyl of the seventh limb of the three hundred and fifty-second exploratory body."* He is the book's ship-board POV: the reader sees the Carryx war effort — its "overspill," its Budon prayer-singers, its Sinen overseers, its volumetric tactical displays — through his eyes.

He is also the novel's clearest portrait of **a man who has run out of hope** and is still, somehow, capable of small kindness. When the moieties are split and one half is sent to the disabled enemy command ship at Ashtin-Kah, Rickar **swaps himself into the reserve group** so that Campar and Ghati can stay together. The choice looks minor. It sends him to Jurupe instead, where the Carryx **bomb an inhabited world to glowing red ash** in front of him and Dervan — and where, in an evacuation pod routed out of the losing battle, a hidden cyst of the swarm broadcasts its accumulated intelligence by **vaporizing Rickar's body** on fifty overlapping frequencies.

Rickar does not know, at any point in the book, that he has been carrying the swarm. He never learns that his death is the event that ends the swarm's mission and opens the deathless enemy's line of sight onto the Carryx world-palace. He accepts his death lucidly, in a lucid-dream café with a woman who is not quite Else, and asks for a cigarette on the way out.

His arc, in one line: **he forgets what the Carryx are, remembers, and is used as a weapon against them without ever being asked.**

---

## Carried Over From *The Mercy of Gods*

Rickar arrives in TFOB with four facts the book builds on:

- He was the research group's Judas in Book 1 — took credit for the team's research. Campar thinks of this late in TFOB: *"I wish we'd been kinder to him… before"* (Ch 35). Back on Anjiin, they'd treated him *"like a traitor for his part in taking their research from them."*
- He was rehabilitated during their captivity on the prison world and became the group's gentlest member.
- He fell in love with **Dennia** on the prison world. She was slaughtered with the rest of Ostencour's rebels when the rebellion was betrayed — by Dafyd, who told the Carryx in order to spare everyone else. This is the knife Rickar carries for the whole novel.
- He started as a junior researcher who had planned *"a posting at Irvian that led to a senior position at Hausen Educational Medrey, and then an early and comfortable retirement back home at Dyan. Three decades."* (Ch 4) — a life he describes in TFOB as *"the absurd fantasies of a child."*

## Physical and Psychological Baseline

Corey's early TFOB descriptions establish a man who is **visibly unraveling but still functional**:

> "His clothes were the same tunic and trousers that all the humans wore, provided by their captors. His hair had grown longer and shaggy. He still shaved, but a few errant whiskers low on his neck and by his ear had escaped the scythe. His cheeks had grown gaunt, and there was a permanent darkness in the skin under his eyes."
> — TFOB Ch 4 (Campar's POV)

He grinds his teeth in his sleep so hard he's afraid he'll crack one:

> "One of these days, he'd clench his way into a broken tooth, and then he didn't know what would happen. There were only about a dozen humans aboard the Carryx warship, and he didn't think any of them were dentists."
> — TFOB Ch 4

He doesn't really sleep; he *lapses out of consciousness*:

> "Rickar didn't sleep anymore so much as lapse out of consciousness. There was no sense, when he came back to himself, of anything but the bone-deep tiredness that he'd carried to the pillow with him. The tension of knowing that he could die at any moment for no reason always at odds with the thought that, if he did, at least it would be over."
> — TFOB Ch 13

And he has a phantom craving for cigarettes he hasn't had in years — a tell Corey returns to repeatedly. Under stress he *pats at his breast like he was checking a jacket pocket he no longer had for a pouch of cigarettes they couldn't get* (Ch 4); at Jurupe, *"he was struck by an almost overpowering craving for a cigarette"* (Ch 22); and the craving sets up the book's single most devastating beat when the dying-dream Else gives him one at the café just before his body is unmade.

His dreams are a recurrent signal that he is living past his own capacity:

> "Rickar was at Irvian Research Medrey back on Anjiin, and he was trying to get across the city to Dennia before the attack started. That was wrong, of course. He hadn't met Dennia until after Anjiin fell. She had died on their prison world, slaughtered along with the rest of Ostencour's rebels. He knew that, but he was also running down streets that were almost the ones he'd known, trying to flag down transports that were always just out of his reach."
> — TFOB Ch 4 (opening of Rickar's first POV chapter)

The dream is not nostalgia. It is a running anxiety nightmare in which **he tries to save a woman he never met on that world** — a grief that has pulled itself out of linear time. He wakes from it with an aching jaw and goes looking for Campar.

The baseline interior monologue is pragmatist-approaching-fatalist:

> "Rickar had always thought of himself as a pragmatist, sometimes to the point of fatalism. They were powerless in the face of Carryx authority... The Carryx had taken his world. He'd lost Dennia in the collapse of Ostencour's rebellion. Hope had failed him too many times for him to lean on it again."
> — TFOB Ch 22

And a self-deprecating ethical mirror, in his read of Campar:

> "He was a sweet fucker, Campar. A better man than Rickar was. Probably an indication that he'd die in the war. Good people wore out faster than bastards, the universe using them up like it held a grudge against kindness."
> — TFOB Ch 13

He holds unexpected guilt about Jessyn — not betrayal, but contempt:

> "Sometimes he remembered when Jessyn had confessed that she needed medication to keep her mental illness from overtaking her. He'd actually felt a little pity for her at the time. He wondered how she was, wherever she was. He wondered if he'd ever see her again, ever have a chance to apologize for that pity and disdain."
> — TFOB Ch 4

## Arc by Chapter

### Ch 4 — Introduction on the Warship

Rickar wakes from the Dennia nightmare, reflects on his abandoned life plan, inventories the ship's geography and rules (animal-quarters bronze rooms; straying is death; Sinen orientation), and goes looking for Campar. Campar's cell is empty. In the common room he meets **Vaudai** for the first time.

The exchange is his first structural relationship of the book. It establishes:

- Vaudai is the only alien on the ship who will willingly speak to humans at length.
- Vaudai is *funny* — and Rickar, for the first time in a long time, is amused by something. *"A smile flickered over Rickar's lips."*
- Vaudai is carrying the mission-level information that no other captive has: *"I have determined that we are in the third dactyl of the seventh limb of the three hundred and fifty-second exploratory body."*

Rickar nicknames it "giant slug" and accepts "sticks-with-meat-on-them" in return. The friendship is the book's warmest cross-species bond (mirroring Campar's parallel friendship with Vaudai later); Rickar's access to Vaudai is what gives the reader access to Carryx tactics.

A few pages later he finds Campar. Campar tells him about Ghati; Rickar, low-key and off-balance, doesn't quite know what to do with the good news:

> "Be careful. Don't let yourself get hurt."
> — TFOB Ch 4

Campar later reflects that *"The Rickar sitting with him now was a very different animal"* — still recognizably Rickar, but hollowed out. Rickar stands, *pats his breast for the missing cigarettes*, and walks back to his cell. It is the first of three times he reaches for that pocket.

### Ch 13 — The First "Overspill"

The Budon of Luus sing; the Soft Lothark lie flat on the floor; the Carryx countermeasures absorb most of the enemy's field-weapons attack, but some of it bleeds through as *overspill* — field-weapons the defenses couldn't damp.

Rickar is knocked unconscious. When he comes back he finds his knee swollen, blood scabbed in his hair, and two Budon dead. He realizes something unexpected and laughs at it:

> "For the first time in a very long time, he felt joy. The enemy had tried to kill them and failed. Rickar felt giddy that he wasn't dead, and he laughed at the pleasure of the sensation."
> — TFOB Ch 13

The moment matters because it is the only on-page flash of uncomplicated happiness Rickar has in the novel, and because the reason for it is entirely negative: *he didn't die*. It's the inversion that defines the character — for Rickar, joy is an absence.

Afterwards he talks with Vaudai, who explains *overspill* and the Budon's role as quantum-flux sensors. Rickar thanks it and climbs to his cell.

### Ch 18 — "There Is No Plan"

Rickar has graduated from Vaudai's occasional conversation partner to Vaudai's **regular audience at the tactical display**. He has learned to follow the slug's narration: exclusion-and-control fields, fundamental decoherence zones, void tendril probability arcs. He can now watch the war.

Campar — wrecked by Ghati's depression — crosses the room to ask Rickar a question and gets the book's second-most-quoted answer. He wants permission to tell Ghati that *Dafyd has a plan* against the Carryx. Rickar tells him the truth:

> "You shouldn't tell him about Alkhor's plan. Alkhor doesn't have one."
> — TFOB Ch 18

And, pressed:

> "We were in shock. We were numb with grief. A bunch of our friends had been killed. Again. The fucking Carryx had just beaten one of their own to death in front of us and then told us we were being honored by getting assigned out through the universe. Alkhor felt guilty, and he said some shit he thought we'd want to hear. It didn't mean anything."
> — TFOB Ch 18

And:

> "Dafyd Alkhor is their favorite pet. He doesn't have power. None of us do. We're fucked. We're oak leaves in autumn, going where the wind blows us."
> — TFOB Ch 18

The two beats matter for the novel as a whole:

1. Rickar is **the book's only voice who states the nihilist reading of Dafyd aloud**. Dafyd himself, back at the world-palace, is actually running a long conspiracy; Rickar doesn't know that. To Rickar, Dafyd is the man who got Dennia killed and said comforting things afterward that nobody with power actually means. The reader cannot disprove Rickar's view within the text — it is only visible as wrong because the reader has Dafyd's POV chapters.
2. This is the belief Rickar dies believing. Nothing after this scene — including his own death — gives him evidence that resistance is possible. When he chooses to accept his death in the evacuation pod, he is choosing *something is better than nothing*, not *we are winning.*

The conversation is cut short when Vaudai pulls them both back to the display to watch the enemy command ship *fail to self-destruct* as predicted. Rickar has no idea what has just been handed to him — the one event that will cause the moieties to be split and sent to board the disabled ship, setting up his own transfer.

### Ch 22 — The Body Swap

The yellow-armored Carryx soldier enters the common room and sorts the captives by species. The four Sinen begin pulling individuals out of their cells. Rickar — bone-tired, shipwrecked, half-dissociating from a craving for a cigarette he hasn't had in years — watches the soldier physically separate **Ghati** and **Campar** into opposite groups.

Ghati is placed with the "exploration" team bound for the enemy command ship. Campar is placed in the "reserve" group that stays on the warship. Campar sees it happen and breaks:

> "Campar shook his head and said *No* again, but he wasn't saying it to himself. He was talking to the universe. He was begging."
> — TFOB Ch 22

Rickar had *"always thought of himself as a pragmatist, sometimes to the point of fatalism"* (Ch 22). Hope had failed him too many times. But he recognizes Campar's face — the face of hope dying — and the recognition does something to him. What he does next he does without planning. He walks casually into the space between the groups, calls Ghati over as if he's flagging a colleague across the quad at Dyan Academy, takes Ghati's elbow, and turns around him *"as gracefully as a dancer, propelling the other man in Campar's direction and continuing forward to the gap in the line that he'd left."*

> "Owe me one."
> — TFOB Ch 22 (to Ghati)

> "Sorry, sorry. I'll stay here. No trouble, no trouble."
> — TFOB Ch 22 (to the Soft Lothark)

> "One of us is just as good as another."
> — TFOB Ch 22 (smiling into the Soft Lothark's black eyes)

The guard puts a hand on his chest and confirms he will stay. He does. Across the room Campar gives Rickar the bear-hug relief of a man who has just had his lover handed back to him.

This is the hinge of Rickar's arc. He has been, up to this point, a man moving through a war that doesn't care about him. In this scene he **spends his life on a small mercy**. He does not know yet that the swap has sent him to Jurupe. He does not know yet that he is a walking transmitter. He does it *because Campar's face broke*.

Campar will later, posthumously, describe this as the single decision he can't forgive himself for accepting:

> "It's just that Rickar should have been with me. When they divided us up, he traded places with a man I've been seeing. I can't help wondering what would have happened if he hadn't."
> — TFOB Ch 35

### Ch 26 — The Sleeping World

After Vaudai and Campar are sent to Ashtin-Kah, Rickar's warship breaks asymmetric space in *Jurupe system*. The dactyl has divided; the librarian in command has chosen Jurupe as its prize.

Rickar inherits Vaudai's abandoned viewscreen and figures out how to work it by watching — a small, characteristic act of *dying-of-boredom* curiosity. He also inherits **Dervan**, the anxious fellow captive whose friends have been redistributed away from her:

> "In any other circumstance, he wouldn't have spent time with her. As it was her company or nothing, he sat with her a little under half the time."
> — TFOB Ch 26

He even extends her the small mercy of teaching her to see the dimly visible control dots on the display so she can change the view herself. The moment is understated but important: Rickar *keeps doing small human things for people who need them*, even when he is out of reserves for himself.

The sleeping world comes into the display — blue-green, lace of cloud, night side glowing gold with city lights *"like neural clusters reaching for each other across the darkness."* Dervan asks if Anjiin looked like that. Rickar says yes, *"but something had shifted in his gut. It was like part of him had seen into the future and didn't want to go. Like he was trying to swim against the current of time."*

The Budon of Luus begin to sing.

Rickar watches an **aurora of killing light** spread over the night side of the world. The city lights beneath it *"changed into a deeper, dimmer, more unified light. A dull red light. Fire through smoke."* The dactyl is sterilizing the planet — *not as a prize to be taken, but a resource to deny the opponents of the empire.* Dervan punches through the projection, screaming *"Make it stop. Stop it."* Rickar doesn't stop watching.

When it's over, he delivers the book's cleanest single-line distillation of his state of mind:

> "I had to work at it. I had to focus on other shit like Alkhor selling us out, but I managed. I did it, for a while."
> "Did what?"
> "Forgot. I made myself forget what the Carryx are."
> "And now?"
> "Now I remember why I made myself forget."
> — TFOB Ch 26

This is the thematic center of his arc. The warship, Vaudai, the viewscreen, the gallows jokes, the body-swap, Dervan's small company — all of it has been **an extended forgetting**. The sterilization of the blue-green world is the thing that ends the forgetting. Rickar is now looking directly at what he is enclosed inside of.

He has, maybe, eight chapters to live.

### Ch 29 — The Evacuation

The subjugator-librarian is preparing for a possibly-losing engagement against superior enemy forces incoming on Jurupe. It chooses to **evacuate its data-bearing assets ahead of the battle** — the Saren-hund, the nodes, and *"the other animals of service"* — not because they are endangered, but because if the enemy intercepts or fires on the pods, the dactyl gets free intelligence on enemy priorities.

Rickar is woken by a Sinen in his cell. He is processed through a hot, incense-and-sty-smelling section of the ship to a launching bay where *evacuation pods* lie open along the far wall like dissected fish. Along the way he notices, with the small pride of a long-erased man:

> "It had been so long since anything had used his name that Rickar felt disoriented hearing it. 'Yes,' he said. The idea that his identity might be important, might signify anything to his captors, felt almost like pride. 'I am Rickar Daumatin.'"
> — TFOB Ch 29

This is the last time he says his own name in the book.

The red-headed human from Abbasat who has never spoken to him in their entire captivity approaches him — *"We shouldn't let them. We ought to tell them all we won't do it. That we won't go."* Rickar agrees but does not argue. A Sinen leads the man away. A Sinen leads Rickar away to his assigned pod.

> "'This is your evacuation pod,' the Sinen at his side announced. 'When you take your place in it, you will be given a treatment that we expect to slow your metabolism. You will not resist it.'"
> "'Just "expect"?' Rickar asked. 'And what happens if you're mistaken?'"
> — TFOB Ch 29

Rickar climbs in. The pod closes. Something warm fills him. He closes his eyes.

And Else is there.

### Ch 29 — The Café

Not Else. A remnant-of-a-copy of Else. The swarm — Jellit's body now — is reaching backward through the cyst it had hidden in Rickar before he left the world-palace, and it is using the last seconds of his biological life to ask him to consent to being used as a transmitter.

The café is Anjiin. Sunset stripes on blue sky. The grown coral of the Common flooded with light and party-sound. Rickar is in his old tailored jacket. Else is in Gallantist black-and-gold mourning robes.

> "Else? Is that you?"
> "Sort of. A remnant of a copy, but a remnant of a copy of me, so sure. It's me."
> "Am I dreaming?"
> "Technically, I am. But I'm using your brain to do it."
> — TFOB Ch 29

He is gentle with her. He tells her — generously, unaccusingly — something he never got to say in life:

> "You're beautiful, you know. I never told you that, because you were with Tonner or Dafyd. But I always thought you were a beautiful woman."

She starts to orchestrate a sex dream; he waves it off — *"You're very kind, but it's all right. You don't have to do it this way."* They are back at the café, the sunset reddening.

She tells him he is going to die. She tells him it had to be someone. She tells him the broadcast will hurt the Carryx. He remembers Jurupe — the aurora, the red-glowing ruin. She remembers it because he does. She takes his dream-fingers and regrets. He gives her the defining line of his arc:

> "Everyone dies. But not every death matters. I'm grateful to fuck these assholes up on my way out."
> — TFOB Ch 29

And then, at the end, he asks for the thing he has been reaching for since Chapter 4:

> "Can you give me something? I mean, since it's all just a dream."
> She smiled, and he lifted the cigarette to his lips. The taste of the smoke, the brightening of the ember, even the small, subtle crackle of the paper as it burned. The illusion was perfect. He blew out a cloud of blueish smoke.
> "God damn I needed that."
> — TFOB Ch 29

The cigarette he has wanted the entire book. The Else he wishes he had spoken to. The chance, at the end, to turn his death into **signal**.

> "Let's do this thing, shall we?"

His body is unmade on fifty overlapping frequencies. The pod fails fifteen seconds later under the pressure of steam that had once been his blood. The swarm's intelligence reaches the deathless.

### Posthumous (Ch 29–36)

Rickar keeps appearing after his death.

- **Surur-Tlassen** sees the report of *"a pattern of anomaly"* around Rickar's evacuation pod cross the Sovran's desk (Ch 32). The Carryx learn nothing they can act on — only that something unscheduled happened to the Daumatin pod.
- **Ekur-Tkalal**, the keeper-librarian of the human moiety, summons Dafyd and asks if humans broadcast on death. Dafyd, blindsided, says no. Ekur-Tkalal notes Dafyd's denial into the archive. Dafyd privately absorbs the news that Rickar is dead: *"Irinna, Nöl, Synnia, Jellit, Else, Tonner, Rickar. Jessyn was in the field. Campar. They might be dead too, and the Carryx just hadn't bothered to tell him yet."* (Ch 32)
- Dafyd weeps in front of Uuya Tomos a few scenes later — *"Another one of my group died. Rickar Daumatin. He was in a battle and something went wrong with his evacuation pod. And now he's gone. And I don't know what to feel about it."* (Ch 33)
- **Campar**, recuperating at the end of the novel, learns in conversation with Dafyd that Rickar is dead. He gives him the line that is Rickar's epitaph within the moiety: *"I was sorry to hear about Tonner. And Rickar too. We became close on the trip out, before they separated us. I wish we'd been kinder to him… before."* (Ch 35)
- **Clae**, working with Dafyd on the conspiracy at book's end, pieces together independently that Rickar's death is what sent the deathless the surveillance image of the world-palace corridor near the human moiety's quarters. The deathless enemy does not know who sent it. The channel has closed with Rickar's body.

Rickar never learns that he ended the swarm's mission. Dafyd never tells the moiety how Rickar actually died. From the outside, Rickar simply *had an evacuation pod fail.*

## Themes

### Forgetting and Remembering

The arc's spine. TFOB builds a pattern in Rickar: **the forgetting is the survival strategy, and the remembering is the moral rupture.**

- Ch 4: he has *"made himself forget"* that he once planned a life.
- Ch 13: he has *"made himself forget"* enough to be briefly happy when he doesn't die.
- Ch 18: he tells Campar that *hope is the thing that keeps getting people hurt* — an attempt to make Campar forget too.
- Ch 26: the sleeping world dies. He remembers. The forgetting ends.
- Ch 29: he walks into his death *remembering*. The sterilization of Jurupe is what he uses to consent.

The remembering is what makes him usable by the swarm. If he hadn't re-seen what the Carryx were doing, he would likely not have agreed so cleanly at the café.

### Small Kindness Against Cosmic Violence

Rickar is the novel's most economical illustration of Uuya Tomos's long-horizon ethic: the Carryx cannot be defeated in our lifetime; what the human moiety can still produce is *good acts of their own*. Rickar:

- Forces himself to sit with Dervan half the time because she has no one else.
- Teaches her to work the display.
- Trades places with Ghati *because Campar is breaking in real time*.
- Is kind to the remnant-of-Else at the café — kind, the way he could never be to the woman herself while she was alive.
- Goes quietly when the pod closes on him.

His last line to anyone living is *"One of us is just as good as another."* The sentence is meant as a joke to the Soft Lothark guard. It is also the moral of his story.

### The Man Who Never Had a Plan

Dafyd's moiety is structured around Dafyd's conspiracy. Jessyn's moiety is structured around Jessyn's rescue plan. Rickar is the POV character whose arc is *not* organized by a plan — his own or anyone else's. He does not know the swarm has seeded itself in him. He does not know why he was conscripted. He does not know why he was evacuated. His death is organized by the *swarm* and the *subjugator-librarian* simultaneously and independently; Rickar is the vector that makes them meet.

This is the Corey move that makes the arc work. Rickar is right that *"Dafyd Alkhor doesn't have a plan,"* and he is wrong, and he dies without knowing he was wrong. He is also right that he himself has no plan, and he dies having been used exactly as a plan would have used him. The resistance succeeds **through** him, not **with** him.

### Dennia Unspoken

Dennia is not on the page. She is the pressure underneath every sentence. The opening dream establishes her — the lover Rickar met on the prison world, killed by the Carryx during the Ostencour purge that Dafyd betrayed. Corey does not bring her back explicitly; he lets Else take her place in the dying-dream. The café, the gentleness, the cigarette, the "it's all right, you don't have to do it this way" — that register is the register he would have used with Dennia, transposed onto Else because Else is the one the swarm can reach through.

It is Rickar's last piece of grace that he spends his dying kindness on the woman who is haunting someone *else*, not the one he wishes were there.

## Relationships

### Vaudai
His closest shipboard companion and the book's warmest cross-species friendship. "Giant slug" / "sticks-with-meat-on-them." Vaudai teaches him to read the Carryx tactical display; Rickar inherits its seat when it leaves. Vaudai never realizes Rickar has been reassigned — Rickar calls to it during the species-segregation scene and *"if Vaudai heard him, it didn't reply."* The friendship is real but asymmetric: Rickar loves Vaudai; Vaudai, in its own dry way, likes Rickar but goes on to love Campar and Ghati. The reader notes the continuity without Rickar getting to see it.

### Campar
Oldest living tie from the Dyan group. Campar carries the survivor's weight of the body-swap — *"I wish we'd been kinder to him… before."* Their final on-page interaction is Rickar gesturing across the common room that he's all right while Campar holds Ghati in a bear hug. Campar waves with his left hand because his right is holding Ghati's. Rickar waves back, and the big man is gone.

### Ghati
One word. *"Owe me one."* Ghati does — Campar's return home alive is Ghati's debt, and Rickar is never there to collect. The swap is the whole relationship.

### Dervan
A companion of necessity. Anxious, Gallantist, talkative. Rickar tolerates her company, teaches her the display, watches the genocide with her, lets her scream *"Make it stop"* without joining in. After Rickar is taken for evacuation, Dervan trots up and asks him what's happening. He tells her *"They're evacuating the ship."* He does not say goodbye. He doesn't know he needs to.

### Dafyd (in absentia)
The man who got Dennia killed. Rickar never sees him on-page in TFOB. He tells Campar *there is no plan*, and dies holding that conviction. Dafyd, receiving word of his death, grieves him honestly but without being able to tell the moiety why it mattered. They never reconcile. Their last exchange happened off-page before the novel began.

### Else (via the swarm)
The swarm's dream-interface wears Else's face because the swarm lived inside Else's body on Anjiin. To Rickar, this makes the dream gentle and recognizable instead of terrifying. He gives her the compliment he owed her in life; she gives him the cigarette he has wanted for the whole book; he consents. It is the single most tender scene between any two characters in the book.

### Dennia (absent)
The whole engine. Never on-page. Always in the dreams.

## Shape of the Arc, in Six Beats

1. **Wakes into grief** (Ch 4) — the Dennia dream, the teeth-grinding, the cigarettes he can't have.
2. **Finds something to like** (Ch 4/13) — Vaudai, the giant slug, gives him a voice to talk to and a reason to laugh.
3. **States his thesis** (Ch 18) — *there is no plan.* Hope has failed him too many times.
4. **Spends his life on a small mercy** (Ch 22) — trades places with Ghati so Campar isn't broken.
5. **Remembers** (Ch 26) — the genocide of the blue-green world returns him to what he'd been hiding from.
6. **Consents** (Ch 29) — accepts his death cleanly at the dream café, asks for the cigarette, and is used by the swarm as a one-shot transmitter.

He never knows he succeeded. Campar, Dafyd, Uuya, and Vaudai all mourn him without being able to say what he did. Only Clae, piecing it together later, understands — and tells only Dafyd.

Rickar's whole arc is a thesis that the book repeats in other registers: **in the Carryx war, the act that matters is the small human act done without knowing it will matter.** Rickar didn't know his body was a bomb; he knew he could move Ghati across a room. The first is how he is used. The second is what he is.
