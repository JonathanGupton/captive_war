# Vaudai — Character Analysis

*The Captive's War, Book 2: The Faith of Beasts — James S.A. Corey*

---

## Snapshot

A vast, blue-gray, slug-like alien "roughly the size of a side of beef" conscripted into the same Carryx exploratory body as Rickar, Campar, and Ghati. Its species studies the patterns of violence — a *moiety of war analysts* — and it wears its translation half-mind somewhere in the folds of its body rather than at a collar. Across *The Faith of Beasts* it is simultaneously the book's chief **exposition vehicle for Carryx strategy**, its most durable source of **gallows comedy**, and the voice that delivers the novel's bleakest thesis: the war cannot end, and the Carryx will not stop.

Vaudai is Rickar's closest shipboard companion, Campar and Ghati's mission commander aboard the dead enemy command ship at Ashtin-Kah, and — by book's end — Ghati's new partner on a six-thousand-year stellar-armature survey. It is the only non-human alien in either book (so far) that a human character openly loves.

Whether "Vaudai" is its personal name, its species name, or both, is never resolved in-text:

> "It called itself Vaudai, but Rickar didn't know if that was its name or its species. It didn't matter, because he didn't call it that."
> — TFOB Ch 4

Rickar calls it "giant slug" to its face. It calls him "sticks-with-meat-on-them." The affection is mutual and entirely unsentimental.

---

## Physical Description

Corey gives Vaudai more surface than most of the Carryx-menagerie aliens — because so much of its emotional register is carried in how its body moves and colors.

### Mass and build

> "a vast, blue-gray mass of flesh roughly the size of a side of beef lurched away from it."
> — TFOB Ch 4 (first sighting)

> "the couch-sized slug thing"
> — TFOB Ch 22

It is big enough that Campar can *lean against its side to get its attention*, but it is lower than a standing human; its translator box rests "in the folds of the alien body" rather than at a throat or chest. Elsewhere it is called a *"massive slug,"* *"the great slug,"* and *"that huge slug-like thing."*

### Color and skin

Its skin is the novel's most-described piece of alien physiology. Its default is gray or blue-gray, but it **shifts textures and colors constantly** — whether as speech, mood, exertion, or something untranslatable is never made clear, and Campar explicitly flags the ambiguity.

> "Vaudai shivered. Its flesh took on a nacreous sheen for a moment."
> — TFOB Ch 4

> "Vaudai shifted, its skin taking on a dancing mother-of-pearl sheen for a moment and then darkening back to gray."
> — TFOB Ch 18

> "Its skin was shifting textures quickly, but whether that was how it communicated or just an expression of excitement in the moment, Campar couldn't tell."
> — TFOB Ch 18

> "its body shimmering like oil in a puddle"
> — TFOB Ch 22

> "The great slug cycled through several shades of gray-green."
> — TFOB Ch 24

At Campar's door near book's end it is described as *"a nacreous blue-gray lump"* pressing in — the color is part of the character's signature.

### Locomotion

Vaudai has **one wide adhesive foot** and can reshape its body to fit its situation. It is slow on the deck in ordinary gravity and adheres to surfaces when it needs stability:

> "Yes," Vaudai agreed as it spread its body wider against the deck to adhere better."
> — TFOB Ch 13 (bracing for a field-weapons "overspill")

> "The great slug lurched across the floor, its wide single foot leaving a clean line behind it where it had adhered to the deck."
> — TFOB Ch 28

In microgravity it becomes a different creature entirely — fast, elegant, inchworm-like:

> "Vaudai taking the lead, its body stretching and contracting like an inchworm as it moved across the deck, its skin dancing with subtle colors."
> — TFOB Ch 24

> "Vaudai stretched its body out thin, grabbed into the wall, then thickened as it pulled itself forward. When it came to a corner, it hauled itself around, vanishing for a moment..."
> — TFOB Ch 28

> "the great slug had turned down a side corridor, its body stretching thin to reach ahead and then bunching together as fast as a running man."
> — TFOB Ch 28

It can also extend a portion of its body across a room like a probe: *"Vaudai extended itself into the room like a wide, grayish tongue"* (Ch 28) to investigate the deathless sample.

Under stress or injury, its body partially dissolves, leaving behind a white resin:

> "Vaudai was swelling up and pulling in, resuming its usual shape. Where its flesh let go, a hard, white resin stained the deck. Something smelled like acetate."
> — TFOB Ch 13 (after an overspill)

### Vacuum tolerance

Vaudai does not need the sprayed vacuum-sheath the humans wear on the command-ship boarding party; Campar speculates but is never told:

> "Campar couldn't tell whether the huge slug-like thing had also been covered in the false skin or if its kind didn't need interventions to withstand the vacuum of space."
> — TFOB Ch 24

The working evidence is that it tolerates hard vacuum natively — it leads the boarding, crawls through the cut door first, and is the only one of the three who doesn't require a breathing rig.

### Juveniles (or smaller kin)

During the species-segregation scene, when the Carryx soldier reshuffles the moieties, Rickar notices:

> "six more of the beetle-like aliens behind them and **a smaller version of Vaudai that Rickar had never seen before.**"
> — TFOB Ch 22

So there are at least two of Vaudai's kind on this warship — the "full-sized" Vaudai and a smaller individual — suggesting caste, life-stage, or simply two conscripts rather than one. The text does not clarify.

## Voice and Speech

Vaudai speaks through a translation half-mind of the same pattern every Carryx captive uses. Rickar notes the uncanny disconnect between what the box says and what the animal actually is:

> "'The battle is in essence already over,' Vaudai's translation half-mind said. Rickar was still uncertain what signals it was taking from the slug that became language. To him, the voice of the machine was the voice of the alien."
> — TFOB Ch 18

The half-mind is also imperfect. When Vaudai is emotional, it reaches for idioms the translator cannot handle, and the machine either hiccups or renders them as awkward literal English. These mistranslations are the book's most consistent running joke.

### The "painful-birthing" expletive

Vaudai's all-purpose curse word, apparently something deeply obscene in its own language. The translator hiccups every time:

> "Not a"—the translator hiccuped here for a moment—"painful-birthing clue."
> — TFOB Ch 4

> "I'm in a stillborn's milk room talking to badly made furniture instead of going to the command ship. I am painful birthing frustrated."
> — TFOB Ch 22

Rickar reads the half-mind's stumble as proof it is registering genuine emotion the translator can't dress up. Once he catches on, its mood becomes legible to him in a way very few of the other aliens' are.

### Names for humans

Vaudai has several interchangeable terms for humans, all of them variations on *limbs-on-a-stick*:

- **"sticks-with-meat-on-them"** — Ch 4, 18, and used throughout.
- **"meat-on-sticks"** — Ch 24 ("Come, come, come, meat-on-sticks").
- **"sticks-with-meat"** — Ch 28.
- **"animals as poorly designed as you"** — Ch 24 (said fondly).
- **"useful appendages"** — Ch 24 (to Campar and Ghati, about their hands).

Late in the novel it shifts registers when teasing them kindly: *"Well done, sticks-with-meat. You are not as inferior as most of your species."* (Ch 28).

### Other characteristic idioms

- **"badly made furniture"** — the Soft Lothark, from Vaudai's point of view (Ch 22).
- **"fossilized excrement"** — its more extreme description of them: "Speaking to these is like trying to teach grammar to fossilized excrement" (Ch 22).
- **"depositing half an egg"** — what stopping a job mid-task feels like to Vaudai (Ch 24).
- **"What is, is."** — closing line of its last conversation with Campar (Ch 35), and the Carryx's own catechism: Vaudai has taken on its masters' fatalism wholesale.

The cumulative effect is of a character who is **smart, impatient, bored, and extremely funny**, mediated through a translator that can't keep up.

## Role / Moiety Function

Vaudai's species is, in its own words, specialists in **the patterns of violence**:

> "The gift of my kind is to ponder the patterns of violence. If we live, I will provide our keeper-librarian with analysis of these battles. If we don't, it won't be my concern."
> — TFOB Ch 13

In practice this means it (a) **has and freely shares full Carryx tactical data**, (b) **runs the boarding action** on captured enemy command ships, and (c) **writes the after-action report** that filters up through the Carryx librarian hierarchy. Its attitude toward what a human would call military secrecy is dismissive:

> "Vaudai had access to the Carryx tactical data, and would share it if Rickar asked. They didn't seem to care about the concept of military secrets. Their strategy of *make a mistake and watch your species burn* was the only backstop they needed."
> — TFOB Ch 18

This offhand line contains Vaudai's whole worldview. Its species' security model is not *classify the data*; it is *serve the Carryx flawlessly because the alternative is species extinction*. Rickar is allowed to look at the tactical map because the Carryx do not consider him capable of acting on it, and Vaudai does not either.

Vaudai's working tool is a Carryx-style volumetric display that Rickar learns to operate by watching — tracking the twelve planets of the current system, the two asteroid belts, the Carryx and enemy ships, and "exclusion-and-control fields," "fundamental decoherence zones," and "void tendril probability arcs" (Ch 18). This is the viewscreen Rickar inherits after Vaudai and Campar are sent to the command ship, and the one Rickar uses in the book's climax when he detonates his own moiety in full view of the Carryx.

## Arc Across *The Faith of Beasts*

### Ch 4 — First meeting

Rickar encounters Vaudai at the food dispenser on the Carryx warship. The exchange establishes every formal element of the relationship in a page: the "giant slug" / "sticks-with-meat-on-them" mutual nicknames, the "painful-birthing" curse, the fact that Vaudai has mission intelligence the Carryx have not bothered to classify, and the fact that it has already told Rickar more about their situation than anyone else has bothered to:

> "I have determined that we are in the third dactyl of the seventh limb of the three hundred and fifty-second exploratory body."
> — TFOB Ch 4

This line is the reason Rickar — and the reader — can locate the *Captive's War* narrative in the larger scale of the Carryx empire at all.

### Ch 13 — First "overspill"

The first of several field-weapons battles. The Budon of Luus sing the alarm; the Soft Lothark brace themselves; Vaudai flattens itself against the deck and weathers the event, leaving behind the acetate-smelling white resin where its flesh partially dissolved. Afterwards it explains "overspill" to Rickar — the unavoidable bleed-through from damped Carryx countermeasures — and offers the book's first direct exposition of Carryx war doctrine:

> "Nothing is normal in war. It is always an experiment to discover what will undo the last encounter's defenses and what will defend against the newest angle of attack... Conflict is the engine of excellence, and war is the distillation of conflict. It is the grand unfolding that ends in the embrace of eternity. Also, it is like painful defecation and leaves me with an ache. I am going to rest now."
> — TFOB Ch 13

Two things land at once: Vaudai's species treats war as cosmic aesthetics, and Vaudai personally is tired.

### Ch 18 — The tactical display

Rickar is now a regular at the viewscreen. Vaudai explains the kill box being set for the enemy command ship. Campar — hollowed out by Ghati's depression — crosses the room to ask Rickar whether he should tell Ghati about "Dafyd's plan"; Rickar tells him there *is* no plan ("Alkhor doesn't have one"), and the conversation is interrupted by Vaudai calling them back to the display:

> "Sticks-with-meat-on-them, look here. It's happening as I said it would."
> — TFOB Ch 18

Vaudai predicts, to the cycle, the destruction pattern — and is then caught out when the enemy command ship, uniquely, fails to detonate:

> "Well. That's unexpected."
> — TFOB Ch 18

It is the first time in the book a Carryx-side analyst is visibly wrong, and the hinge-event that produces the next section of the plot.

### Ch 22 — Species-segregation and recruitment

The yellow-armored Carryx soldier separates the moieties and sends half to the command-ship boarding. Vaudai is the subject matter expert on this mission; it is being pulled away from the common-room viewscreen it loves, and it is incandescent about having to spend its time "sharing insight with fossilized excrement":

> "Interrupt me! Speaking to these is like trying to teach grammar to fossilized excrement. Anything else is better. Which one is your ignorant friend?"
> — TFOB Ch 22

It is during this scene that Rickar trades places with Ghati on the manifest — sending himself into the part of the moiety "held in reserve" (which turns out to be Jurupe, and his death) so that Campar and Ghati can stay together. Vaudai never registers the trade; to it, both are interchangeable sticks-with-meat.

### Ch 24 — The command-ship boarding

Vaudai leads Campar and Ghati onto the dead enemy ship. In zero-g its body becomes fluid and fast; on the deck it inchworms ahead, directing the men to set up the scanning-sheet poles that give it x-ray imagery of the ship's interior. Its enthusiasm is genuine:

> "the massive slug sounded transported by joy at the idea."
> — TFOB Ch 24 ("We will survey the internal shielding")

This is the first chapter in which Vaudai's **curiosity** is legibly separate from its loyalty to the Carryx. Its phrase for its own project — *"most importantly I will know. I will be the first to know. Most exhilarating!"* — is closer to an academic's pleasure than to a soldier's duty. And when the evacuation order comes and Vaudai insists on returning despite the battle, the justification is naked:

> "We are insignificant, the war is all that matters! **Plus which, it is interesting and I am bored!**"
> — TFOB Ch 24

This is the thesis sentence for the character. Vaudai serves the Carryx because the alternative is extinction; it *boards the deathless command ship* because it's *bored*.

### Ch 28 — The deathless soldier

The return trip. Campar, Ghati, and Vaudai cut through a shielded door, discover human corpses and five-tentacled alien corpses together on the bridge, and encounter a single badly-damaged "deathless" soldier reactivating as its fleet returns. Vaudai's investigative streak is as strong as ever — *"Intriguing to see firsthand,"* it says of the pursuit (Ch 28) — but it also rapidly constructs a triage plan to save at least one of them:

> "By the time it tracks and kills two of us, one may reach the skiff and escape."
> — TFOB Ch 28

When Campar flatly refuses — *"if I'm going to die, I'll die without abandoning my friends"* — Vaudai's response is characteristic:

> "I agree. This is a good plan. Bravery in client species is often noted by the Carryx."
> — TFOB Ch 28

The sentence is doing two things simultaneously: it is sincerely endorsing the plan, and it is sardonically reminding Campar that the Carryx reward bravery in animals *the way a human might reward a good dog*. Vaudai can mean both things at once without strain.

Vaudai survives the battle. Its flesh is partially dissolved by the field-weapon "overspill" along with Campar's and Ghati's; Ghati's later description is "Ghati's worst scar was an angry nerve... Vaudai's for that matter." (Ch 35, via Campar's reflection). The three of them go home damaged.

### Ch 35 — Parting

At Campar's quarters in the new compound, Vaudai arrives to collect Ghati for the next mission — a six-thousand-year-old stellar armature around a Carryx-controlled star. Ghati has just ended his relationship with Campar because he cannot survive watching him nearly die again. Vaudai enters through the doorway *"a nacreous blue-gray lump"* and opens with the question only a professional observer of grief would ask first:

> "Have you said the things? Has the grief passed?"
> — TFOB Ch 35

The scene is the most affectionate on-page exchange between a human and an alien in either book. Campar and Vaudai are, by this point, *friends*, and their parting is written as one:

> "I am going to miss you, you vast knot of mucus."
> "I expect to regret the absence of your grotesque odor as well, sticks-with-meat."
> — TFOB Ch 35

Vaudai's closing lines are the book's final statement on the Carryx:

> "The war will never end. It cannot. The Carryx will never cease their aggression, only redirect it."
> "Well, maybe they'll lose."
> "They will not. The enemy is in retreat. Their holdings are collapsing in five of the seven essential strategic zones."
> "No hope, then?"
> "Of peace? Absolutely none. The Carryx will be victorious, and then they will go on with the war at every edge of the empire. They have no choice. **What is, is.**"
> — TFOB Ch 35

"What is, is" is the Carryx's own motto. Vaudai has internalized it fully — and delivers it, as always, *cheerfully*. The last we hear of it, it is taking Ghati out of the war and into a six-millennium research project, and it has already told him the mission is *"Oh no. Not at all safe."*

## Temperament

Vaudai is the best-characterized non-Carryx alien in the novel because Corey is willing to give it a **distinct comic voice** rather than treating it as a biology problem.

- **Curious above all else.** It boards an enemy command ship in mid-battle because the door won't open and it wants to know what's behind it. Its stated reason is that it is *bored*.
- **Impatient.** "Is there a way to go faster?" (Ch 28). Ghati, who is hand-cutting shielded metal, has to keep answering no.
- **Openly contemptuous of its Carryx-side colleagues** — specifically the Soft Lothark, whom it calls "badly made furniture" and "fossilized excrement" in translator-muffled frustration. It is *not* contemptuous of the Carryx themselves.
- **Dryly affectionate with humans.** "If you were functional, I would enjoy risking your existence again" (Ch 35) is among the warmer things any alien says to any human in the series.
- **Fatalistic about its own death.** "We will. We are mortal. It is all right to eventually die. It would be concerning not to." (Ch 35)
- **Capable of genuine attachment.** Its request that Ghati — and, if possible, Campar — come with it on the next mission is a personnel decision driven by preference, and Ghati knows it: *"Apparently, the gooey little footstool likes working with us."*

It treats humans the way a brisk senior academic might treat capable grad students: dismissive nicknames, working impatience, a high tolerance for their slowness, and a clear preference for their company over that of most of its colleagues.

## Thematic Role

Vaudai does three jobs in the architecture of *The Faith of Beasts*:

### 1. It makes the Carryx war *comprehensible* without making it sympathetic.

Every Carryx-side tactical scene that the reader follows — the overspill, the projection fields, the command-ship kill box, the battle at Ashtin-Kah, the return-and-detonate pattern — is either explained by Vaudai or translated into lay terms for Rickar and Campar by Vaudai. The book's understanding of the war's *mechanics* is entirely mediated through its voice. This matters because the Carryx themselves are opaque; Rak-hund cannot explain; Sinen overseers only issue orders; the Soft Lothark are what Vaudai calls furniture. Vaudai is the only alien in the captive environment who is both well-briefed and willing to talk.

### 2. It delivers the novel's final statement on hope.

Most of the book has been Dafyd's conspiracy and Jessyn's deathless-alliance discovery — both of which point toward *eventual resistance*. Vaudai's closing monologue closes that door:

> "The Carryx will be victorious, and then they will go on with the war at every edge of the empire. They have no choice."
> — TFOB Ch 35

It is the prognosis of a specialist in the patterns of violence, not a propaganda line. The conspirators' work is therefore not "defeat the Carryx"; it is Uuya Tomos's *"a game to be left to the great-grandchildren of our great-grandchildren."* Vaudai's certainty about the present is what justifies Uuya's long horizon.

### 3. It models a non-Carryx species that has fully accepted domestication.

Vaudai is Corey's cleanest example of what Campar will propose in the book's final scene: *be the corn, be the chicken, be the species that the masters cannot stop feeding*. Vaudai's people have made that bargain already. They carry tactical secrets lightly because their value to the Carryx is in *analysis*, not in loyalty-through-ignorance; they send individuals onto suicide missions cheerfully because *"What is, is"*; they have become indispensable. And they are *flourishing* — Vaudai is curious, comfortable, funny, attached to friends, not visibly suffering. If Campar's proposal to Dafyd has a working model anywhere in the book, Vaudai is it.

That Vaudai is also the voice that tells Campar *"there is no hope of peace"* makes the domestication neither a criticism nor an endorsement. It is the tradeoff laid out in full.

## Relationships

### Rickar

The closest non-human friendship in either book so far. They spend the warship segment at the tactical display together; Vaudai teaches Rickar to interpret Carryx symbology, allows him to watch battles unfold, and shares intelligence with him as a matter of course. The relationship is built on **mutual irreverence** — the "giant slug" / "sticks-with-meat-on-them" nicknames, the running gag about Vaudai's expletives — and on Rickar's own despair: he is a man with nothing to lose making a dark-comic friend of the one alien who finds the situation as funny as he does.

When Rickar is reassigned to Jurupe, Vaudai does not notice. Their last interaction is Rickar calling *"Hey, giant slug, any idea what's going on here?"* across the common room and Vaudai ignoring him. Rickar inherits Vaudai's viewscreen in the "reserve" group — Dervan catches him using it and assumes Vaudai told him something (Ch 22); it hadn't. He'd just watched.

### Campar

Starts as professional — Campar is conscripted as Vaudai's "useful appendage" on the command-ship job — and becomes the book's single most affectionate cross-species bond. By the time they part, Campar is calling Vaudai *"you vast knot of mucus"* and Vaudai is responding in kind. Vaudai's request that Campar come on the stellar-armature mission is a personnel request, and both men understand that *it asked for them because it likes them.*

### Ghati

Begins their relationship afraid of Vaudai ("space slugs are a famously enthusiastic people," Campar jokes). Ends it as Vaudai's chosen human partner on a six-thousand-year mission. The relationship is never written as romantic on Ghati's part, but it is genuinely elective — Ghati is choosing six millennia of stellar-armature surveys over a pleasant compound on the Carryx world-palace, and Vaudai is a large part of why.

### Soft Lothark ("noble servants")

The half-mind's rendering. In private, Vaudai calls them "badly made furniture" and "fossilized excrement." It considers them unable to think at its level about patterns of violence and treats the Carryx's decision to station three of them by its viewscreen as a waste of everyone's time.

### The Carryx soldier

Vaudai abases itself appropriately and argues strenuously for permission to return to the command ship during the Ashtin-Kah evacuation. It gets its way. The exchange is conducted in Carryx song, untranslated; the half-mind does not bother relaying it to the humans. Vaudai has a working relationship with the Carryx themselves that it does not extend to their lesser client species.

### Other Vaudai (the smaller one)

Glimpsed once, during the species-segregation scene (Ch 22), and never referenced again. Likely a second conscript of the same species rather than a child, since the moieties appear to be configured by function rather than family, but the text does not say.

## Open Questions

- **Is "Vaudai" a name or a species?** Rickar never gets an answer, and the narrator doesn't either. The fact that a "smaller version of Vaudai" appears in Ch 22 without being named suggests the word may be the species — but Rickar uses it as a proper noun throughout.
- **Why doesn't Vaudai need a vacuum sheath?** Native vacuum tolerance is the working hypothesis; the text never confirms.
- **What is the white acetate-smelling resin?** Produced when overspill partially dissolves its flesh (Ch 13). Defensive encystment, metabolic byproduct, shed dead tissue — unclear.
- **Is the color-shifting language, mood, or both?** Campar explicitly flags the ambiguity and it is never resolved.
- **How old is Vaudai?** The stellar-armature it is assigned to survey has not been reviewed in "six thousand local rotations," and Vaudai expects to personally explore it. The mission lifespan is vastly longer than a human's; whether Vaudai's own is comparable, or whether it simply expects to die partway through and be replaced, is not addressed.
