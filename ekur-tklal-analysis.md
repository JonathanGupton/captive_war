# Ekur-Tkalal — Character Analysis and the Dafyd Relationship

*The Captive's War, Book 2: The Faith of Beasts — James S.A. Corey*

(The character's name is spelled **Ekur-Tkalal** throughout the source text. Filename retained per user request.)

---

## Snapshot

Ekur-Tkalal is a Carryx **keeper-librarian** — the direct master of the human moiety on the Carryx world-palace, Dafyd Alkhor's sole on-page Carryx superior, and the book's principal living face of the empire. It is a character we spend the whole novel in a room with yet almost never see from the inside; Corey gives us one full chapter (TFOB Ch 16) from its POV and otherwise keeps it behind the half-mind translator, reading as a looming silhouette whose moods must be inferred from leg position.

Across the book, its relationship with Dafyd moves through four distinct registers: (1) a raw, lethal authority issuing the breeding mandate in Chapter 1; (2) a weary administrator that gradually makes Dafyd its "factor," a sort of unwanted prosthetic librarian it half-consciously cannot bring itself to live without; (3) the single moment in Chapter 30 where it touches Dafyd's shoulder — the first recorded Carryx-to-human contact in either book that did not end in a human's death — and speaks the cryptic words "There is only one Sovran"; and (4) by Chapter 36, a routinized working partner whose casual disdain has become itself a form of stability. Ekur-Tkalal ends the novel exactly where it began — keeper-librarian of the humans, at the bottom of the Carryx chain of being — while Dafyd's conspiracy grows beneath a dissembled surface of perfect factor-like competence. The empire's tragedy, in miniature, is the Carryx officer who mistakes its growing affinity for a human for degradation, and so never once looks at him closely enough to see the plot.

## Physical and Biological Characterization

Dafyd's first-meeting description is the baseline:

> "Ekur-Tkalal shifted its abdomen on four thin legs while Dafyd finished his report. Its thorax and head stayed steady, the two massive black-and-red fighting arms planted against the floor. Which was good. As long as those arms stayed on the floor, it wasn't ready to kill him. Its four eyes moved independently as if each was distracted by a separate thought, and the mantis-like feeding arms in its chest unfolded and manipulated small shapes of floating light whose projectors Dafyd couldn't identify. Every now and then, the Carryx chirped or burbled to itself, but the half-mind at its throat didn't say anything. If there were words in its vocalizations, they weren't meant for him."
> — TFOB Ch 1

Body features recurring throughout:

- **Abdomen on four thin legs, steady thorax**, two massive **black-and-red fighting arms** kept planted on the floor during peaceful meetings; lifted only when ready to kill. A subtle shift back onto the abdominal legs is a pre-kill warning posture, as Dafyd observes in Ch 30.
- **Four independent eyes** that can focus together on a single point when making a deliberate gesture (Ch 30).
- **Mantis-like feeding arms** folded into the chest, used for fine manipulation — including of the light-shape "writing" that passes for Carryx formal documentation.
- A **beak** hidden beneath the flesh of the face; audible as a click.
- A **living voice like birdsong — deep, slow, and threatening** — speaking a language Dafyd can't produce. Translation is done by a half-mind device at its throat, whose output is "human and featureless" with a quality Dafyd notes "might only have been [his] prejudices" in reading as dismissive (Ch 1).
- **Olfactory reading of animal emotion**: Ekur can smell pheromonal relief on humans, and registers this as "repulsive" (Ch 16).
- **Scale**: Jessyn, seeing her own Carryx in the field, compares the world-palace keeper-librarian to the Sovran and notes it is "oddly simple. Only four eyes, and the plates of its armor made its body seem like it had been built out of fewer pieces than the others Jessyn had seen… a thumbnail sketch discarded when a better thought came along." Ekur is, even among Carryx, a low-prestige specimen.

The Carryx biology is caste-mutable: Ch 16 states that Ekur's "body had changed when it was given its new place, and because of that, its mind, but there was an echo of its old life that remained." The creature we meet is not a fixed organism but one whose morphology has been reshaped by its demotion into animal-handling.

## Caste, Status, and Personal History (the Ch 16 POV)

The novel's single most important passage for understanding Ekur-Tkalal is the opening of Chapter 16, which is narrated from its POV:

> "Ekur-Tkalal lived now at the bottom of a great chain of being. Keeper-librarian of the human moiety, it directed all things that touched upon the animals under its control, as it was itself directed by Moqid of the cohort Tlannan, who was directed by Dalkin of the cohort Astur, who was directed by the supervisor-librarian of animal assets. And so on, up to Surur-Tlassen, regulator-librarian, and through him — him — to the Sovran herself with whom Ekur was honored to share a planet."
>
> "Ekur's place was more honored and honorable than its former role as subjugator-librarian. Then, it had been a librarian to soldiers, and a soldier was the lowest caste. There was nothing less than to be a Carryx who died at the hands of animals. To interact with animals was fundamentally degrading, but at least here, it was not facing them as equals as it had in battle. Ekur's body had changed when it was given its new place, and because of that, its mind, but there was an echo of its old life that remained. It had been almost a soldier, but it had been high among subjugator-librarians of its dactyl, coordinating violence and spending the lives of Carryx who mattered even less than it did. Now the only things beneath it were the humans of Anjiin, and it had half-unconsciously made one of them its factor, putting the human's frail body and unpleasant, squeaking voice in the place that the lesser librarians had once filled. Ekur had seen other animals make effigies of those they had lost as a kind of comforting doll, and it was disgusted to see some version of that impulse in itself."
>
> "But to spend more time directly with the animals would have been worse. This way, even though its factor was acting as a perverse parody of a Carryx librarian, there was only one that Ekur had to meet with on a regular basis."
> — TFOB Ch 16

Everything about Ekur's characterization descends from this paragraph:

- **Former caste**: subjugator-librarian. A military-administrative role: it supervised Carryx *soldiers*, the lowest Carryx caste. It was "high among" such subjugator-librarians "of its dactyl" (a Carryx organizational unit) and "coordinat[ed] violence and [spent] the lives of Carryx who mattered even less than it did." It has personally ordered its own species into deaths.
- **Current caste**: keeper-librarian of the human moiety. Reports directly to **Moqid-Tlannan**, who reports to **Dalkin-Astur**, who reports to the **supervisor-librarian of animal assets**, who reports (with several rungs skipped in Ekur's own summary) to **Surur-Tlassen, regulator-librarian**, who reports to the **Sovran**. Ekur is approximately six steps down from the empire's apex, and the humans are the only thing below it.
- **Promotion or demotion?** Ekur treats the move as an upgrade ("more honored and honorable than its former role") — because now the animals under it are not its fellow Carryx. The shame of having commanded Carryx into dying is gone. But the novel frames the move, through Ekur's own tells, as a sideways demotion: it has been put out to pasture at the bottom of a chain that doesn't matter, with a body that has been physically remolded to fit the new role. The "echo of its old life that remained" is the novel's signal that this creature is, underneath, a veteran killer.
- **Ambition**: Ekur harbors a private plan to challenge Moqid-Tlannan in the Carryx physical-combat succession custom and take its superior's place. It notes that the gravity-survey permission, if successful, would produce enough status-flow of "hormones and growth factors" to get it into fighting trim for the challenge (Ch 16). This ambition is never revisited in the novel. By the time the Sovran dies in Ch 36, Ekur is still a keeper-librarian.
- **The factor impulse**: This is the key psychological fact. Ekur has made Dafyd its *factor* — an informal proxy librarian it uses to avoid direct animal-handling — and it is **disgusted with itself** for doing so. It explicitly compares the habit to an animal making "effigies of those they had lost as a kind of comforting doll." Ekur knows the behavior is beneath a Carryx; it can't stop. This is the novel's most surprising characterization move: the Carryx officer is capable of ambivalent self-disgust, of knowing that its own instincts toward Dafyd are incorrect by its species' standards, and of nevertheless continuing to indulge them because the alternative — more direct animal contact — would be worse.

This last point is load-bearing for the relationship arc. Ekur-Tkalal is not a pure hierarchical operator. It is a veteran officer on a degrading assignment who has, against its own values, started to rely emotionally on the one animal it deals with most. The book is the story of that reliance deepening without Ekur ever naming it to itself — and of Dafyd slowly learning to exploit it.

## Arc of the Dafyd–Ekur Relationship

### Phase 1 — Ch 1: The Breeding Mandate

Their first on-page meeting in the novel is not a first meeting in continuity; it's a routine audience. Dafyd submits a report, kneels when corrected, and absorbs the order that will shape the rest of the book. The scene establishes three enduring facts:

1. **The relationship is entirely one-way in surface register.** Ekur issues directives; Dafyd complies. Dafyd is on his knees with palms flat on the floor.
2. **Ekur has contempt for Dafyd's output as animal scrawl.** "I have no use for animal scratchings. You will submit your reports in proper archival form." The spatial glyph system Ekur mandates will be Dafyd's single most exhausting daily labor.
3. **Ekur is the messenger, not the author, of the breeding mandate.** When Dafyd protests that humans are only three thousand and children grow slowly, Ekur answers with what amounts to a shrug of the empire: *"The will of the Sovran compasses eons. Begin now. Have them ready when we have use for them."* Ekur is not being cruel. Ekur is relaying the schedule.

The Carryx is already reading Dafyd as its factor in this scene — "Your moiety has been found of interest by the Sovran. You will prepare for greater use in the empire" — but neither party names it.

### Phase 2 — Ch 7: Ekur as Investment Manager

After Ver Cannedan knocks Dafyd unconscious with a single uppercut, Ekur-Tkalal assigns a hundred Soft Lothark guards to the moiety and a personal rak-hund bodyguard to Dafyd himself. Dafyd learns this through the Soft Lothark, not from Ekur:

> "You have suffered violence that inconveniences the Carryx. A hundred of our — clan group are now assigned to your moiety to help you maintain physical security. We were also to escort this one which is assigned to protect your person in particular."
> — TFOB Ch 7

The framing is cold ("inconveniences the Carryx"), but the action is large. Ekur is protecting its investment in Dafyd with a level of resource commitment — a hundred guards, a bred weapon — that would be overkill for a disposable animal. This is the first external evidence that Dafyd has already become something more than replaceable.

### Phase 3 — Chs 2–15: The Grind

Between the breeding mandate and the Ch 16 POV chapter, the relationship is experienced by Dafyd as administrative labor. Dafyd drafts reports in the spatial glyph language until his eyes bleed, submits them, receives adjustments, submits more:

> "The writing wasn't really a language so much as a drawing in space of the information it meant to share. The shapes each seemed to have a hundred different meanings depending on where they were placed in space, what shapes they interacted with, and the context of the he'd-call-it-a-document in which they appeared… Using it was like trying to write poetry in advanced mathematical notations that he didn't know."
> — TFOB, Dafyd's POV (early chapters)

Dafyd's reading of Ekur during this phase: "the librarian's disdain for working with animals might also be a disdain for working at all." Dafyd notices Ekur "seemed happy to drop all of it on his shoulders, and Dafyd had encouraged it to. The more he knew, he figured, the more options he could discover." Dafyd is consciously cultivating the factor relationship Ekur is unconsciously indulging. Each is exploiting the other's weakness, and neither is aware of the other's plan.

### Phase 4 — Ch 16: The Gravity Survey (the Ekur POV)

The most intimate chapter of the relationship is one Dafyd never gets to read. Dafyd and Jellit (actually the swarm) request permission to fly a transport out over the planet to conduct a "gravity survey" — cover for the swarm to trace a Carryx archive signal. From Dafyd's side, this is a calculated risk petition to his master. From Ekur's side, it is the novel's most revealing internal deliberation:

> "Ekur-Tkalal's feeding arms plucked at each other as it thought. There were animals of service throughout the world-palace, but they were selected and bred for their functions. If the humans of Anjiin stumbled into places where their presence was noxious, they would be killed. If its factor and the lead of its research group both died, that could reflect poorly on Ekur. But if the information led to a technology useful to the Sovran, it would certainly elevate its status. Ekur felt ambition awake in long-dormant parts of its body."
> — TFOB Ch 16

Ekur grants the permission because it sees a chance at personal advancement. The protection of Dafyd and Jellit is a secondary consideration — a reputational risk to manage. And yet the chapter also registers Ekur's **smelling their relief** when it says yes, and finding that relief **"repulsive."** The Carryx cannot bear to watch humans be grateful. It approved the request but cannot tolerate the intimacy of their reaction to its approval. This is the texture of the relationship in two sentences: Ekur has the instincts of someone taking care of Dafyd and the professed aesthetics of someone who finds taking care of him disgusting.

### Phase 5 — Ch 30: Rickar's Death, the Misstep, and the Touch

This is the pivot. Dafyd arrives for a routine meeting; Ekur opens it by informing him of **Rickar Daumatin's death** in an escape pod leaving a distant battle. Dafyd is gutted. Ekur, with what reads as genuine curiosity, follows up on something it has read in human recordings:

> "There are references in your recordings to a spirit or soul that leaves the body at death, but no such phenomenon has been observed in other deaths by your kind."
>
> "Yes, it's a religious story. It's spiritual. It's meant to describe the change between being alive and having awareness to being dead."
>
> "You will clarify that this spirit is made of information."
>
> "It isn't made of anything. It's just a story that tries to comfort us in the face of something we find terrifying and inexplicable. It makes it so we can stand the idea of dying."
> — TFOB Ch 30

Ekur logs the answer into its glowing-glyph archive. The keeper-librarian is not mocking Dafyd's grief — it is doing fieldwork. An observation Ekur will pass up to its superiors, where it will travel all the way to the Sovran, is that the human "soul" is not an information structure but a coping device. This is one of the few places in the book where we see how the Carryx empire's knowledge of humans is actually assembled: by a mid-level administrator asking questions of its pet native informant in the moments between reports.

Then Dafyd, grief-distracted, makes the most dangerous mistake of his tenure. He reports disappointing lab results, gets critiqued ("You have given no reason for this failure"), and then — fatally — phrases a team request in a way that positions animals as giving orders to the Carryx: "The Soft Lothark and Rak-hund guards? The biological group wants them to be removed." Ekur responds with a pre-kill posture:

> "The keeper-librarian's response was subtle enough that he would have missed it before. A shifting of weight back toward its abdominal legs, a change in the angle at which its massive fighting legs met the floor. It hadn't gone as far as lifting its fighting arms. The threat hadn't been made, but a chill ran down Dafyd's spine all the same."
> — TFOB Ch 30

Dafyd — physically pulling his shoulders in as if protecting little kittens on them — reframes: "Neither. We make no request. It's information I have had from the new leader of the team, and which I have presented to you." Ekur's weight shifts forward again. The kill has been averted.

Then, unprecedentedly:

> "'As it should be.' Ekur-Tkalal leaned forward on its fighting arms and reached out with its feeding arms in a gesture Dafyd had never seen before. It touched Dafyd's shoulder, and all of its eyes looked at him as if to mark the importance of the moment. 'There is only one Sovran.'
>
> 'I understand,' Dafyd said, even though he was fairly sure he didn't.
>
> The Carryx drew itself back to its customary position, but Dafyd could still feel the place where it had touched him. It was the first time he could remember a Carryx doing that that hadn't resulted in the death of the human."
> — TFOB Ch 30

**What the touch is.** Dafyd does not know. The novel does not tell us. The phrase "There is only one Sovran" appears elsewhere in the book both as a Carryx political maxim (only the Sovran is irreplaceable; every other Carryx is fungible) and as a private note the swarm uses while trying to understand Carryx command structure (Ch 23). Read against Ch 16's factor confession, the most defensible gloss is: *I have almost made you my lesser librarian, but you are an animal, and there is only one real authority. Don't forget that.* The gesture is a correction Ekur is issuing to itself as much as to Dafyd — a reassertion of hierarchy right after it has indulged the species-crossing impulse of its own behavior.

But it is also the first physical contact across the boundary, and Dafyd registers it as such. Later in the same chapter, Korham claps Dafyd on the shoulder in the same place, and the human gesture and the Carryx gesture sit on top of each other. The two species have briefly used the same vocabulary without agreeing on the grammar.

The chapter ends with Dafyd rattled and self-critical; he confesses the misstep to Uuya ("I challenged it, and the best I could have hoped for was it broke my leg. It probably would have killed me"), receives the wisdom-myths lesson, and is pulled back to work. It is striking that the novel's worst-ever moment in the Dafyd–Ekur relationship (a near-kill) and its most intimate (the touch) occur inside the same meeting, five paragraphs apart.

### Phase 6 — Ch 36: The Normal Meeting, the Sovran's Song

By the final meeting in the book, the dynamic has settled. Dafyd is reporting on nutrient densities in experimental grasses. Ekur is satisfied. Dafyd even receives a compliment for his team:

> "These results are much improved. Your research group deserves whatever recognition is appropriate to your kind."
>
> "I will see to it," Dafyd said.
>
> He had a sense that the librarian's vocabulary in human languages had started to improve. He didn't know if that was a sign that the half-minds had gathered enough samples of human interaction to grow sophisticated or if it was something specific to Ekur-Tkalal as an individual, but it was interesting. Maybe concerning, maybe not.
> — TFOB Ch 36

Dafyd is now a proficient reader of the glyph archive, concealing his fluency — "The more the keeper-librarian underestimated him, the better." Ekur is now offering him strategic questions — "You will draw up analysis of which individuals can be assigned out with the least disruption" — that Dafyd immediately recognizes as an opportunity to shape the field-agent roster into the backbone of his conspiracy. The relationship has become a working professional one in which both sides are quietly exploiting the other.

Mid-meeting, a vast choral song fills the air. Ekur rises up onto its back legs, arms outstretched, and sings along for a full minute before shuddering and returning to itself:

> "Several of Ekur's eyes flickered toward him and then away. 'It was not meant for you. The Sovran has died. The new Sovran has announced herself. The consonance is not for animals. It does not affect your duties.'"
> — TFOB Ch 36

The Sovran has been killed by her heir (we learn this from the Surur-Tlassen chapter immediately preceding), and Ekur is being biologically retuned to the new Sovran in real time. For a Carryx, this is the species's most central religious experience. For the novel's reader, the telling detail is what Ekur says to Dafyd: **"The consonance is not for animals."** The relationship that began with "no use for animal scratchings" ends with "the consonance is not for animals" — the same framing, unmoved. Whatever Ekur has come to feel for Dafyd across 35 chapters, it still cannot name him as anything but an animal. And yet Ekur has interrupted itself, from a moment of species-ecstasy, to explain the event to him. The courtesy is real. The classification is unchanged.

> "And when it was over, Ekur dismissed him with the same casual disdain as always."
> — TFOB Ch 36

## The Factor Relationship (the Core Dynamic)

The term *factor* — Ekur's own word (Ch 16) — is the key to the relationship, and it is worth being precise about what the word does.

A Carryx keeper-librarian would ordinarily manage the animals under it by working through lesser Carryx librarians as intermediaries. Ekur, demoted out of the military side, no longer has access to such subordinates. So it has made Dafyd — a human — stand in for the lesser librarians it used to command. Dafyd drafts the archival reports, handles personnel, transmits orders downward, collects data, and performs the ritual of kneeling on arrival. From Ekur's POV this is not a relationship with Dafyd; it is an *effigy* of its lost subordinate class, hung around a convenient human-shaped armature.

The emotional content of the factor relationship, as described in Ch 16, is:

- **Functional dependence**: Ekur cannot stomach more direct animal-handling than is needed, so the factor is necessary.
- **Self-disgust**: Ekur recognizes the impulse to treat Dafyd as a substitute-Carryx as the same kind of grief-displacement other animals exhibit ("effigies of those they had lost as a kind of comforting doll"). Ekur is embarrassed.
- **Suppression**: Ekur doesn't stop. It *does* minimize: "there was only one that Ekur had to meet with on a regular basis." One animal meeting scheduled into its week.

From Dafyd's POV, the factor relationship is exploitable in exactly three ways, and he uses all three:

1. **Information**: the more Ekur offloads, the more Dafyd sees. Dafyd actively cultivates being overloaded. By Ch 36 he has enough fluency in the glyph archive to read reports going up the chain and conceal that fluency from his master.
2. **Access**: the factor can ask for unusual things. The gravity-survey flight in Ch 16 — a journey Dafyd and the swarm use to tap the Carryx archive itself — is possible only because Ekur grants permissions inside its own authority.
3. **Underestimation**: Ekur's need to keep Dafyd an animal prevents it from ever looking at Dafyd closely enough to detect the conspiracy. The self-disgust reinforces this; Ekur's shame at the factor relationship manifests as refusing to inspect it.

These three exploits are the scaffolding of Dafyd's entire hidden campaign. The conspiracy is possible because of the factor relationship, and the factor relationship is possible because of Ekur's demotion out of military command.

## What Ekur Never Learns

By the end of TFOB, Ekur-Tkalal has not discovered:

- That Jellit Kaul — whom it authorized for the Ch 16 gravity survey — is not Jellit. The swarm used Ekur's permission to penetrate the Carryx archive.
- That Tonner's memorial was a narrative-management operation run by Uuya Tomos, not a grief ritual.
- That Uuya Tomos is assembling a multigenerational subversion curriculum for the moiety's children, attributed to herself as editor of *Myths of Origin*.
- That Dafyd's growing fluency in the glyph archive is concealed from Ekur on purpose.
- That Jessyn Kaul has made contact with the Carryx's "deathless" enemy and that humans may themselves descend from that enemy.
- That the Ch 36 field-agent roster Ekur just commissioned is the exact selection apparatus Dafyd will use to seed his conspiracy across the thousand worlds.
- That Dafyd has an explicit goal: the destruction of the Carryx.

The only human-related intelligence Ekur is ever shown collecting personally is the Ch 30 data point about souls — which it characterizes accurately as a human coping device and passes up the chain. Its fieldwork on the species is both real and narrow. It knows what humans *say about* dying. It does not know what humans *plan*.

## Thematic Function

Ekur-Tkalal is the book's argument that empires rot at the administrative level.

The Carryx, as a species, are portrayed throughout the Captive's War books as ancient, disciplined, and essentially uniform in their commitment to hierarchy. Book 1's Carryx were glimpsed at the top or in combat; they were terrifying precisely because they presented as a single intent. Book 2 takes us into an office at the bottom of their bureaucracy and finds an officer who is (a) demoted, (b) privately ambitious, (c) too proud to do its own paperwork, (d) self-aware enough to be embarrassed by its own instincts, and (e) slowly softening toward the one animal it has to talk to every week. None of these traits is a flaw the Carryx worldview admits. Every one is visible to the reader.

What Ekur does to itself — deputizing Dafyd as a factor because it can't stand direct animal-contact — is structurally identical to what the empire as a whole is doing by using conquered species as its hands: governing through proxies it can't respect. Ekur is a miniature of the Carryx method, and in Ekur's administrative laziness the empire's whole vulnerability becomes legible. The Sovran dies in Ch 36 and is replaced within a page; Ekur sings along; the empire continues. But in the same room, Dafyd is memorizing the glyph grammar. The empire has no handle on him.

The Ch 30 touch is the book's image for this. A Carryx officer, for the first time in recorded interspecies contact, lays a feeding arm on a human shoulder — and uses it to tell the human that the human does not matter, that there is only one Sovran. The gesture says: *you are nothing, and also, I have noticed you.* This is what Corey seems to mean by the empire's disease. The people who carry it out have to stay precisely blind to the people they're carrying it out on, and they can't, quite, all the way. Ekur-Tkalal is the officer in whom that almost-seeing is happening. And because Ekur can't let itself finish the thought, Dafyd is still alive to end the empire.

## Where They End

The book's final Dafyd–Ekur scene is the Ch 36 Sovran-succession meeting. No confrontation happens. No secret is exposed. Ekur compliments the grass team, assigns Dafyd the next roster task, explains the Sovran's death as not-for-animals, and dismisses him "with the same casual disdain as always." Dafyd walks out of the room with more power than he arrived with and more awareness that Ekur does not know this.

The relationship is intact, working, and entirely misunderstood by one of its parties. That is where TFOB leaves it — and what makes Ekur-Tkalal the most dangerously useful ally the conspiracy has.
