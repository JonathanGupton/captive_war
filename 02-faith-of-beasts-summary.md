# The Faith of Beasts — Chapter-by-Chapter Summary

*The Captive's War, Book 2 — James S.A. Corey*

This document provides a part- and chapter-by-chapter summary of *The Faith of Beasts*, the sequel to *The Mercy of Gods*. The novel is organized into six titled parts comprising thirty-seven numbered chapters and a closing coda.

---

## Part One: Structure

### Chapter 1: One

The chapter opens with Carryx archive entries describing the empire's war against "the deathless," the culling of underperforming species-moieties, and the promotion of the Human, Manacat, and Red Sybillie moieties for field deployment. POV shifts to Dafyd Alkhor in the world-palace, where the surviving humans (under four thousand brought from Anjiin, with one in seven dead in transit) now live in slightly more dignified quarters with hair-cutting tools, menstrual supplies, and a small rooftop garden overlooking ziggurats and orbital arcs. Dafyd has become the de facto "high priest" interceding between humans and the Carryx, hated by Tonner Freis, allied uneasily with Jellit, and isolated since Jessyn, Rickar, and Campar have been sent away on mysterious missions. He visits Tonner's lab, where Tonner struggles with Carryx-supplied equipment and a new team of senior experts (Brun, Addira, Abfoss) who won't take orders, then is summoned by his Carryx master, the keeper-librarian Ekur-Tkalal. Ekur orders the moiety to focus exclusively on cross-tree-of-life nutritional translation and gravimetric lensing/imaging, demands reports be filed in proper Carryx archival form, and reveals the Sovran has chosen humanity for deployment across thousands of worlds. Because three thousand humans are insufficient, Ekur orders them to "breed locally for the empire's use" — a moiety that cannot sustain its population will be culled. Dafyd accepts, terrified, understanding the impossible mandate.

### Chapter 2: Two

A present-tense POV from "the swarm" — the alien spy weapon now inhabiting Jellit's body after previously inhabiting Else Yannin and originally Ameer Kindred. In Jellit's physics lab, the swarm works on lensing techniques while internally tormented by the surviving consciousnesses of its three hosts: Ameer is fading, Else is bleeding at the edges, and Jellit (the only male, the only one steeped in violence) ferociously resists possession, mocking and condemning the swarm. We learn the swarm is a designed weapon — a spy smuggled into Carryx space to gather intelligence for the deathless enemy, designed to survive but not to live. Today the swarm has resolved to reveal itself to Dafyd and rekindle the romantic relationship Else had with him. Else protests that everything between them was a lie ("I was dead before you kissed him using my mouth"); Jellit sneers that Dafyd never loved him and never will. The swarm visits Dafyd's quarters, makes tea, and listens to Dafyd lament losing hope. Just as the swarm prepares to confess, Jellit reminds it that it has secretly broken off a fragment of itself, hidden it inside another departing human (likely Jessyn, Rickar, or Campar), and that the fragment will detonate to transmit data when in range — killing its carrier. Confronted with this, the swarm again loses its nerve. The chapter establishes the swarm's grotesque internal multiplicity and the moral horror of its mission.

### Chapter 3: Three

Jessyn Kaul's POV aboard a Carryx ship arriving at a captured world. She tends a glass-paned device she built from the symbiotic "berry" cultures — a fragile silicate slurry ecosystem that produces a daily astringent draught controlling the mental illness she had previously medicated on Anjiin. She has only two starter resets left; if it fails, she breaks. The ship lurches through atmospheric entry, and a Sinen overseer informs her of imminent landing. We learn she was conscripted away from the world-palace after Campar and Rickar, that the lesson Dafyd's group really absorbed was their utter powerlessness. Disembarking onto the planet "World" — recently captured by the Carryx from "the enemy" before they could sterilize it — she is overwhelmed by sunlight, blue sky, fresh-grass-and-mint air, and the sight of black-and-green plant life and shattered alien ruins. Among the assembled multispecies workgroup she finds another human: Garral Pär, a former archaeology department head from Hibbrin Medrey, weeping at the sight of open sky. He recognizes her group as Tonner Freis's — "the ones who sided with the big guys" and turned in the resistance — but doesn't judge. Their Sinen overseer, calling itself Third Gardener, briefs them: they are to survey and explore, relay findings via issued notebooks, beware enemy traps and an evacuation procedure (the enemy destroys worlds it abandons). The work may reveal the path to the enemy's defeat. "The Carryx are watching."

### Chapter 4: Four

Dual POV (Rickar Daumatin then Campar) aboard a Carryx warship traveling toward the front lines. Rickar wakes from a dream of trying to reach Dennia (an Ostencour rebel who died), his jaw aching from clenched grinding. The animal-quarters cells are cold, lockless, and isolating; humans cannot stray into Carryx-only sections on pain of death. He goes to the common room, finding Campar absent, eats provided gruel (unlike on the world-palace, food is dispensed here), and chats with a giant slug-like alien called Vaudai who tells him they are in the third dactyl of the seventh limb of the 352nd exploratory body — likely heading to the war's front. Rickar still cannot forgive Dafyd for betraying Ostencour, Synnia, Dennia, and Allstin, even understanding the necessity. POV shifts to Campar, who has just left a sexual liaison with a man named Ghati (with whom he has rules to keep emotional attachment minimal). He joins Rickar in the common room; Rickar warns him not to get hurt by attachment, citing Dennia. Campar refuses to "act like I'm dead before it actually happens." A Sinen arrives accompanied by a knife-legged Rak-hund — a living weapon that terrifies them — to address the Budon of Luus. The half-mind then announces to all: "We have entered conflict space. Prepare for the possibility of violence." Campar laughs uncontrollably as Ghati strolls in obliviously.

### Chapter 5: Five

Tonner Freis's POV. Three of Tonner's hydroponic tanks (12, 5, and 6) have gone cloudy — possible viral infection. Jellit drags him to a meeting Dafyd has called. Tonner privately admits that the invasion broke him in some fundamental way but soldiers on. The meeting introduces a new organizational structure: Uuya Tomos (folklorist/social historian, author of the Wolf of the World mythic novels), Bastien Korham (former plant manager for the Gallatian church, expert in physical-plant logistics), and Llian Andermus (former director of operational security for Itzibahn Common, fox-faced and dangerous). Dafyd announces a restructuring: the Carryx have decided humans are useful for cross-tree translation, stealth/imaging tech, and self-sustainment. Tonner and Jellit will focus on research; Korham will run physical infrastructure; Andermus will run security ("policing" — Tonner notes Dafyd is preparing for the next Ostencour with cleaner hands); Tomos is solicited as essentially "minister of propaganda." Dafyd then drops the bombshell: the Carryx have ordered the population be self-sustaining. He proposes lamb sacks — artificial wombs producing a hundred-plus embryos from existing tissue samples reverted to stem cells, with caregivers incentivized by extra calories. Tomos savagely denounces this as "birthing new slaves for the Carryx," warning that giving people beloved children gives the Carryx hostage-handles, and walks out. Andermus and Korham agree to participate. Later, in the lab, Korham's facilities team proves a godsend. Then a new conscript, the celebrated choreographer Ver Cannedan, indignantly refuses to wash lab equipment. Tonner punts the problem to Dafyd with anticipatory glee.

### Chapter 6: Six

Dafyd's POV. Summoned to handle Ver Cannedan, Dafyd recognizes the master choreographer (his aunt Dorinda funded Ver's troupe) and tries to defuse the situation by acknowledging Ver's lost status. Ver demands the assignment be rescinded; Dafyd refuses. Ver throws a lightning-fast uppercut and lays Dafyd out cold. Dafyd wakes with Brun holding a cold compress; Tonner is delighted. Dafyd then struggles to draft a Carryx-archival-format report — a written "language" that is really a spatial diagram of geometric shapes whose meaning depends on placement, orientation, and context, with no relation to sound. His Sinen overseer corrects his syntax errors with what feels like contempt, hoping he'll fail. Dafyd is drowning in incoming work: reports are starting to arrive from the human deployments scattered across the galaxy (e.g., Ames Sofar at the Estian Morok Starweb being assessed for inclusion in "the arantine"), and Ekur-Tkalal happily dumps everything on him. Exhausted, Dafyd retreats to the rooftop garden, where Jellit (actually the swarm) finds him. They discuss how to discipline Ver without it being personal vengeance, settling that community-enforced norms via Andermus is correct. Dafyd, vulnerable, confesses that everything was easier when Else was alive — not because of romantic loss but because Else carried the swarm, the only link to the war against the Carryx, the only splinter of hope. He breaks down crying in Jellit's arms, saying "If there are allies for us out there, I can't reach them... they're gone." Jellit/the swarm whispers, "They aren't."

### Chapter 7: Seven

Present-tense swarm POV continuing directly from Chapter 6. The swarm finally reveals itself to Dafyd in the rooftop garden, rolling up Jellit's sleeve to show black motes of microagents swirling under the skin. It tells him Else didn't die — "She's in here. She's in me. I'm Else." It speaks as Else, recalling the kiss in her rooms before the attack and the search for the telescope. Dafyd is horrified, recoiling, asking where Jellit is. The swarm tries to explain that Jellit "comes along too" but cannot articulate the consumptive truth without losing him. Else, Ameer, and Jellit comment internally — Jellit acidly noting Dafyd is recognizing for the first time that the swarm devours its hosts rather than partnering with them. Dafyd asks the chilling question: "Was it ever just her?" and then "What the hell are you?" Before the swarm can answer, it senses footsteps — a Rak-hund and four Soft Lothark emerge. The swarm prepares to dissolve into a cutting storm and die defending Dafyd. But the Soft Lothark have come on a different errand: Ekur-Tkalal has assigned Dafyd a hundred Soft Lothark guards as physical security for the moiety, plus the Rak-hund as a personal protector ("will keep you from unwelcome violence and kill anything of lesser status that you indicate") — a response to the Ver Cannedan assault. Dafyd barks a single laugh. The swarm, robbed of the chance to convince Dafyd it is still the woman he loved, leaves under his bitter dismissal: "You should probably go."

### Chapter 8: Eight

Jessyn's POV on the planet World. As a girl at her medrey she fantasized about wilderness fieldwork but knew her mental illness made it impossible — and now irony has dropped her into exactly that life, with her sanity contained between two glass plates back at the Carryx ships. She finds, surprisingly, she isn't afraid. She has set up a small camp at a sandstone cliff, returning to the ships only to decant her medication, and spends her days surveying. The plant analogs have green-purple-red leaves with concentric ring vasculature; tiny chiming animals hop at dawn; large forward-canted-winged things fly above. The Sinen-issued notebooks let researchers share findings — a botanist named Manni, a geologist named Holom Coombs, hydrogeologists playing strategy games. Jessyn writes increasingly only for herself, using the private shorthand of Tonner's old workgroup ("coffee-ring symmetry," "first data cologne") and references only her dead friends Irinna and Else would have understood. Garral Pär messages her to compare notes; she joins him at his camp on the edge of the obsidian-like ruins, where he has built a clever evaporative-cooling double pot and grills slices of ration bar. He shares findings about the previous inhabitants (bipedal humanoid-sized and larger quadrupedal/multi-legged species, polymer construction, written signs, vision in roughly human wavelengths). They discuss convergent evolution under environmental pressure — Garral pointedly extends this to "if your environment is under the Carryx's thumb." Jessyn asks if he has someone; he admits to a friend on the Carryx world and a wife and two sons on Anjiin. She admits her unrequited infatuation with Irinna, who died never knowing — a moment of release. Walking back, she contemplates the stars: one might hold Jellit, Tonner, and Dafyd; another Anjiin; none hold Irinna, Synnia, Nöl, Else, or Llaren anymore. She plans tomorrow's survey route, deliberately picking the orchard-like site nearest Garral's camp for a "stupid, petty reason." The chapter closes: "Small moments, unnoticed at the time, change the fate of empires."

## Part Two: Antithesis

### Chapter 9: Nine

Dafyd meets in his garden with Llian Andermus, his appointed head of security, who delivers a report of weekly incidents among the human moiety: domestic assaults, theft, and a serial groper whose hands she has broken as a warning. Dafyd, exhausted and emotionally numb, struggles to delegate. Andermus presses him for clear guidelines and refuses to be his "sin-eater," demanding he back her decisions. Dafyd authorizes her to maintain order, prioritizing the Carryx-mandated work (the nursery, Tonner's lab, Jellit's visualization team) above all else, and warns against incarceration as a default punishment. After she leaves, Dafyd lingers, weeping without feeling, contemplating the vast scale of the Carryx empire and his own hidden rage to see it burn. Uuya Tomos, the writer and former associate of his Aunt Dorinda, arrives reluctantly. Dafyd recruits her into a conspiracy: he wants her to craft a curriculum, stories, and songs that will teach the Carryx-born human children to outwardly serve while secretly nurturing rebellion across generations, blending old Anjiin folklore (like Gallantist tales of Shavas Rey) for cover. He explicitly tells her she now has only two choices, betray him to Ekur-Tkalal or join him. She accepts, recognizing in him at last the ruthlessness of his aunt. The chapter establishes Dafyd's long-game resistance plan and the burdens of his double role.

### Chapter 10: Ten

Jessyn, on a planetary surface field assignment under the Sinen Third Gardener, tends to her medical supplies and biome cultures aboard ship before walking out to a half-burned orchard outside the evacuation zone. She enjoys a rare solitude, fantasizing about simply walking off into the wilderness to die alone, and reflects on her growing entanglement with Garral Pär (a married man) and how the environment shapes morality. Sampling the pear-like fruit with her miniaturized Carryx kit, she expects to discover an alien biology, but the assay returns a shocking result: DNA, chloroplasts, mitochondria, cellulose, all the markers of Anjiin life. The trees are pears. The species that built the destroyed city was human-height and used human-visible color signage. Jessyn realizes that humans originated off-world, and that this planet was inhabited by the Carryx's "deathless enemy" — meaning humans are that enemy, and the Carryx don't know. Terrified by the implications, she hesitates to delete the data lest it draw notice. As she's reeling, movement in the grass startles her, a raven flies up, and when she turns back a strange man in a beaded vest and worn blue jacket is standing beside her kit. He speaks an incomprehensible language, gestures her east, grows aggressive when she refuses, grabs her shoulder, and pulls a knife. The chapter ends on the cliffhanger of first contact with surviving locals.

### Chapter 11: Eleven

Tonner shares lunch with Jellit in the human commissary at the world-palace, discussing his observation, attributed to Brun, that Soft Lothark guards "nibble" each other in greeting, possibly vestigial grooming. Jellit pushes back and brings up that the Soft Lothark eat their own dead (recalling Ostencour's failed shipboard rebellion where a stabbed Lothark exploded poisonously). Tonner notes Jellit wasn't on that ship; Jellit covers by saying Jessyn told him. Their conversation drifts personal: Jellit, clearly heartbroken from a romantic rejection by someone in his lab, asks Tonner whether he can forgive Else for leaving him for Alkhor. Tonner deflects with bitter wit. Tonner reflects on his promoted status under Dafyd's regime, the lamb-sack progeny project (118 fetal blisters, 32 already failed), and how the new old labs (won via the Night Drinkers' genocide) calm him. Returning to the lab, he encounters Sommerson and one of Dafyd's Soft Lothark guards loitering by the door. On impulse, Tonner reaches out and touches the Lothark, which violently shoves him back; through its half-mind it warns: "Do not physically interact." Tonner apologizes but secretly uses a sterile glove and sample plate to swab the creature's greasy fur, then runs a covert resonance scan on the fragments. The chapter sets up parallel investigations into Soft Lothark biology and seeds the notion that Jellit is no longer quite Jellit.

### Chapter 12: Twelve

Blindfolded with the knife-man's jacket, Jessyn is marched cross-country at knifepoint. After thousands of steps and a panicked attempt to track distance, a woman's voice calls out and the blind is removed. Her captor is met by a younger, frowning woman with amber eyes and ringlet hair who leads them to a limestone cliff overhang and into a cave that smells of sulfur and crowded bodies. Inside Jessyn nearly succumbs to a panic attack before the woman calms her. The cave shelters around ten human children, roughly ten or eleven years old, of mixed phenotypes, in dirty beaded vests, survivors of the Carryx attack. A black, asymmetric soldier-figure (one half a stick-thin wireframe) unfolds from the wall: a guardian named something like "Gor-fall" or "Corvall." It demonstrates a small handheld weapon by blasting a hole in the cave wall, then folds back into stillness as a sentry. Jessyn realizes these are people, her cousins, the enemies of her enemies, and that maybe somewhere there is a refuge. A blond girl brings her a leaf of boiled grain. After eating, Jessyn discovers her notebook is still in her pocket, never searched. Carefully concealing it, she navigates to Garral Pär's most recent log entry and adds a coded message ("FOUND SOMETHING INTERESTING TO FOLLOW UP OUR CONVERSATION. COMPARE NOTES?") with her exact coordinates, hoping to summon Garral rather than the Carryx, deliberately keeping these survivors hidden from Third Gardener.

### Chapter 13: Thirteen

The chapter opens with Surur of cohort Tlassen, regulator-librarian to the Sovran, dreaming of his time as a brood mother. The narration explains Carryx social biology: how Surur surrendered gender to become a librarian and earned it back by climbing to the apex of his caste, dreams of one day fathering offspring of the Sovran herself, sees personal ambition as a mirror of imperial ambition, and notes that in Carryx language, "war," "service to the empire," and "life" are all the same word. The war goes well. The POV then shifts to Campar, aboard a Carryx ship that has entered "conflict space." With Ghati he speculates nervously about the nature of space combat. Days pass without violence; he grows numb. In the common room, four Budon of Luus suddenly burst into a transcendent, prayer-like song that draws every species, even the Soft Lothark, to listen. The song ends abruptly; immediately the Lothark guards drop to the deck and brace, the giant slug Vaudai flattens itself, and battle engulfs the ship: deck-buckling lurches, an annihilating non-color blue, sound that isn't sound. Campar blacks out. POV shifts to Rickar, who experiences the same event from the corridor, suffering what he initially mistakes for a stroke. Afterwards, two Budon lie dead. Rickar feels unexpected joy at being alive. Vaudai explains in its world-weary way: the Budon are quantum-flux sensors who detected the enemy's projection-field trap, allowing Carryx countermeasures; what the prisoners experienced was "overspill." Vaudai itself is aboard to ponder patterns of violence and report on them; humans, it suggests dryly, are aboard so the Carryx can find out why humans are aboard.

### Chapter 14: Fourteen

Garral, following Jessyn's coded coordinates, encounters the amber-eyed woman in the dry scrubland. She wordlessly gestures him to follow toward the cliffs. Late, he registers warning signs, especially that she is impossibly young (early twenties), the youngest person he has seen since Anjiin fell. He bolts, but the asymmetric black soldier (Corvall) runs him down in an arroyo and marches him at gunpoint into the cave. There he finds Jessyn unharmed, and they exchange wry laughter. He gently challenges Jessyn's theory that these are the Carryx's "deathless enemy" — surely the Carryx would have recognized humans on Anjiin if they were already at war with the species. Watching the captors, Garral has a perception shift: he sees them not as enemies but as exhausted refugees protecting traumatized children. Corvall begins to bind both prisoners to a single rope leash, evidently planning to break camp and move out after dark. Then Garral spots tally marks on the cave floor, an interrupted math lesson, and recognizes a first-generation notation system still taught in Anjiin engineering programs. Drawing in the dirt, he exchanges numbers and names with the knife-man (Omco), the woman (Manta), and the soldier (Corvall). Their language is close to but not identical with proto-Darsin, an archaic Anjiin language family. Garral concludes excitedly: these survivors are not just biologically related humans but share a cultural and linguistic lineage with the founders of Anjiin. They are literal cousins. He cannot understand them yet, but believes given time he will. The discovery transforms the encounter from captivity into reunion across deep time.

### Chapter 15: Fifteen

Dafyd suffers a long nightmare of trying and failing to report to Ekur-Tkalal, his half-mind broken, the Carryx written-symbol grammar dissolving into incoherence. The dream finally breaks when he successfully trills back to a silver Carryx in their own tongue and finds himself walking on fighting arms with a four-legged abdomen, his body briefly Carryx. Waking exhausted, he showers, dons fresh Carryx-issued clothing, and reflects mournfully that the human moiety may never again make their own embroidered clothes unless he teaches the children to want such things. In the commissary, Uuya Tomos arrives with Ver Cannedan, the choreographer whose tantrum punch originally summoned the Soft Lothark guards. Uuya proposes Ver be sent out to study Carryx body language and nonverbal communication, building a movement vocabulary humans could use. Dafyd is wary, citing Ver's impulsiveness and the catastrophic consequences of his last outburst, but agrees to think about it. Uuya then probes about Jellit's fall from grace; Dafyd deflects but concedes "use your best judgment." He skips his scheduled meeting with the Sinen overseer and walks instead to the visualization lab, where Jellit and four others are happily working on a prototype device for the librarian. Jellit (the thing wearing him) flinches at Dafyd's arrival. In a quiet corner, Dafyd confronts the swarm-Jellit, who reaffirms it is also Else and Ameer, "we're all still here." Despite everything, Dafyd asks for help: "I need you to help me." The swarm agrees and is to come to him later. The chapter closes inside the swarm's perspective: it had feared Dafyd had discovered its betrayal (it is sending findings back somewhere), but instead he is reaching out. Else, the voice closest to having loved Dafyd, recognizes this as a precious chance to be loved again, while Ameer dryly suggests the word Dafyd really wants is "seduce."

## Part Three: Pressure Drives Convergence

### Chapter 16: Sixteen

The chapter opens from the perspective of Ekur-Tkalal, the keeper-librarian over the human moiety, who reflects on its degraded place in the Carryx hierarchy and its half-conscious habit of treating Dafyd Alkhor as a personal "factor" — a perverse echo of the lesser librarians it once supervised. Dafyd, accompanied by Jellit Kaul (the lensing project lead), petitions to conduct a planetary gravity survey for a delicate optical tool. Ambition stirring, Ekur grants permission, willing to risk both humans for potential status gain.

The point of view then shifts to Dafyd. Jellit is not really Jellit — he is the alien spy/swarm that previously inhabited Else and killed her, now wearing Jellit's body. The "survey" is cover: while filing a report on Tonner's universal-food work, the spy traces the data signal into the vast Carryx archive, hunting for a usable secret. They take a Carryx transport out over the world-palace, and Dafyd sees for the first time how vast and inhospitable the planet is, with ribbed ziggurats fading behind them.

In the air, they have a fraught conversation. The spy admits it has no "real self" — only the consolidated memories of Ameer, Else, and Jellit — and says cryptically that its first kiss "was you," wounding Dafyd. The transport tracks a signal south to a featureless gray structure surrounded by hot, sulfurous air and Carryx-led arthropod handlers. Resistance fields repel the transport; they land and approach on foot. Filaments sprout from the spy's skin as it interfaces with what is apparently a node of the Carryx archive. The chapter closes with the spy whispering in awe that it can now "see everything." The themes of compromise, complicity, and the cost of resistance dominate — Dafyd accepts that his soul is a cheap price to pay for vengeance.

### Chapter 17: Seventeen

POV: Jessyn Kaul, in a cave on the Carryx-conquered planet of researcher field work, with the survivors she encountered last section: Manta and a group of orphaned children, the linguist Omco, the scholar Garral Pär, and the black-armored soldier Corvall. Garral spends hours translating between Omco and Corvall through a thread of shared cognate ancient languages, while Jessyn watches from the back with the kids, irritated at being excluded.

Garral finally briefs her. Corvall is human — a soldier of the anti-Carryx alliance, encased in living armor that is failing as it tries to rebuild his missing limbs. The other side of the war includes humans and other allied species. They had no knowledge of Anjiin; the Carryx normally torture prisoners to death rather than domesticate them. The city families are all dead, killed by the Carryx; Corvall has only been trying to shelter the orphans. He is running out of time and resources.

That night, Jessyn and Garral lie together for comfort. He cries quietly about the sons he left on Anjiin; she explains she never had children because of her brain illness and is now grateful she didn't bring anyone into this universe. She has missed her medication dose and will start to deteriorate in days.

In the morning, Corvall has a plan. He will return to his crashed ship's emergency beacon to summon a rescue, knowing it will draw the Carryx and kill him, but hoping the kids can be evacuated. He wants Jessyn and Garral to smuggle a device back inside the Carryx ships that disables their engines and weapons, opening a window for safe extraction. Jessyn immediately spots the flaw: with the ships disabled and Corvall absent, the rescuers will simply destroy the helpless Carryx vessels — and her with them. She forces Garral to negotiate: he must go with the children to give the rescuers his intelligence, while she goes alone to plant the device. He reluctantly agrees. The chapter underscores Jessyn's hardening tactical instincts and her self-sacrificial pragmatism.

### Chapter 18: Eighteen

POV alternates between Rickar and Campar, both still aboard the Carryx warship in deep space. Combat overspill events keep shaking the vessel in unpredictable rhythms. The Budon of Luus respond by mass-producing nymphs that die before reaching maturity — a bleak fatalism Rickar reads as evolution's closest thing to optimism.

Rickar has befriended Vaudai, a slug-like alien who shares the Carryx tactical display. Vaudai walks Rickar through the battle: the Carryx have already won; what remains is which losses the enemy will choose. The display tracks ships, exclusion-and-control fields, decoherence zones, and void tendrils. Vaudai predicts the enemy command ship will self-destruct rather than be examined.

Campar, meanwhile, tends to his lover Ghati, who is collapsing into despair. Ghati compares himself to a bee removed from its hive — unable to live without sky and soil. Campar urges him to eat and frames their captivity as something to endure for the chance of pleasant surprises. Wanting to give Ghati hope, Campar approaches Rickar to ask whether he should reveal Dafyd's plan to push back at the Carryx.

Rickar stuns him: there is no plan. Dafyd "felt guilty" after Else's death and said comforting things they wanted to hear, nothing more. Dafyd is the Carryx's favorite pet, not a resistance leader. Campar refuses to believe it but is rattled. Their argument is interrupted as Vaudai's display shows the predicted battle outcome — enemy ships disappearing, escape attempts, the command ship targeted. Campar takes a moment of relief that the overspill events are ending. Then the enemy command ship fails to self-destruct on schedule. Vaudai's last line — "That's unexpected" — sets up Surur-Tlassen's chapter and the Sovran's directive about that ship. The chapter explores despair, lost hope, and the question of whether resistance is even possible.

### Chapter 19: Nineteen

POV: Surur-Tlassen, the regulator-librarian — the second-highest Carryx in the empire, who serves as the conduit of all knowledge to the Sovran. He fears nightmares of bodily dissolution that mark his approaching choice between being deposed by a subordinate or "ascending" to become a sexless monk-keeper of the private creche.

Today he is summoned to the meeting chamber by Urur-Atlak, a former high male now reduced to a withered, scentless creche-keeper. Surur reflexively releases dominance pheromones that have no effect on the neuter remnant — a deeply unnerving experience. Urur informs him the Sovran has a new daughter preparing to emerge and that arrangements must be made. The mysteries of the private creche are explicitly outside imperial reach, known only to the neuter monks.

Surur then makes his daily journey to the Sovran's quarter, casually crushing a slow service animal in passing as a "small correction." In the Sovran's pheromone-thick presence, his blood thrums with renewed loyalty in an almost mating-like response. He delivers his comprehensive daily report: harvesting body shortfalls, quiescent spinward worlds, animal moiety unrest in Sado and Herusant, void dragon developments, and crucially — the enemy ship in the Ashtin-Kah system that failed to self-destruct (the same ship Vaudai discussed), now being prepared for examination by animals of service. He also mentions losses inflicted on the "deathless enemy" in coreward systems.

The Sovran sings as he speaks, then issues directives in trance-inducing harmonics. Among her specific instructions: the failed-suicide enemy ship is "of interest" — the enemy's attention must be diverted elsewhere, regardless of cost, to give the animals full opportunity to examine it. This explicitly connects the storylines: the Carryx interest in the captured ship will drive the war and shape the lives of every captive species. Surur leaves in an ecstatic, devotional state. The chapter conveys the alien physiology and theology of Carryx hierarchy and the absolute, organic centralization of power in the Sovran.

### Chapter 20: Twenty

The chapter opens with the announcement that this is the day Tonner Freis will die. POV alternates between Tonner and Dafyd in the human moiety on the world-palace.

Tonner wakes beside the former finance minister of Abbasat and Olan, expecting their fling to end soon. He goes to the lab where 107 lamb-sack human babies are gestating — the next generation, gas-exchange capable in weeks. He stops at sack thirty-four, secretly his and Else's biological daughter, gestated from the genetic samples the Sinen took from captives. He reflects bitterly that Dafyd is the "little king" of the human moiety but he, Tonner, fathered Else's only child. His assistant Brun reminds him he has a status meeting with Dafyd — pushed up since Dafyd returned three days ago. Tonner's research run data is misplaced; furious and reluctant to look incompetent in front of Dafyd, he decides to lead with a "side project" instead.

Dafyd, meanwhile, is hosting Jellit in his private room, where the spy navigates the Carryx archive in trances that involve visible bodily changes. The archive is described as a mirror of the Carryx empire's nervous system: every report from millennia of empire is preserved, with the secret to unmaking the Carryx potentially buried in trillions of records. Jellit reports a war ten thousand years long, three hundred planets, and that the Carryx came to this world six or seven thousand years ago — it is not their original homeworld.

Dafyd then meets Uuya Tomos and the choreographer Ver Cannedan in his garden. Ver demonstrates Carryx body language: extended fighting arms with a particular shoulder shift = imminent violence; tucked-in feeding-arm posture = de-escalation. He notes Carryx fights end with a broken arm, never death, and that the Emberi of Silos win better Carryx treatment by wearing armbands resembling Carryx scar tissue. Uuya links this to why the Carryx killed an eighth of Anjiin's population (eight limbs, one broken). The principle: minds are something bodies do.

Tonner arrives for his meeting and presents stale data, then pivots to his side project: the Soft Lothark guards exchange large information-dense packets — like plasmids — by eating each other and their dead. They have a private second channel of communication that the Carryx cannot intercept.

A sudden thumping sound, and the two Soft Lothark guards beat Tonner to death — Carryx-style: a kick to the back of the neck. The Rak-hund attacks one Soft Lothark, which then bursts releasing caustic poison; the Rak-hund kills the surviving guard's companion, then has its spine snapped by the remaining guard.

Dafyd, facing the surviving Soft Lothark, drafts a Carryx-symbol message threatening to expose the secret to the archive — "Soft Lothark keep private reports sent as food." When Llian Andermus arrives, Dafyd tells her — and the listening guard — that Tonner started yelling, the Rak-hund mistook it for an attack and killed Tonner, and the Soft Lothark were trying to save him. He erases the threatening message. The guard begins eating its dead companion.

Dafyd staggers back to his room in shock and vomits. Jellit comforts him tenderly with Else's voice, eventually crossing into intimacy — pressing against him and becoming aroused. Dafyd, with regret, sends the spy away. He showers, returns to the garden where Andermus has it under control, and composes a message in Carryx symbols to the Soft Lothark: "Humans and Soft Lothark can help each other." The guard responds by modifying the symbol — "Humans and the Deep Lothark can help each other" — revealing a hidden faction or true name. Dafyd has now formed a covert pact with another captive species. Themes: the price of leadership, complicity, mutual aid among the captives, and the discovery that other species also conspire.

### Chapter 21: Twenty-One

POV: Jessyn Kaul. She, Garral, Omco, and Corvall hike south-west outside Third Gardener's evacuation line to reach Corvall's crashed ship. On the trail Jessyn and Garral align their cover story: she'll claim they were investigating signs of habitation, were attacked, and Garral was killed while she escaped. The footprints from the kids will then look like signs of the inhabitants they were investigating, not collaborators.

Garral asks how she learned to think tactically. She admits she was an "amateur war leader" at the world-palace and killed fellow prisoners (the Night Drinkers), and that she takes some pleasure in it now — a quiet, troubling shift in her self-understanding. The peaceful upbringing has eroded into a comfort with violence against enemies.

At the crash site, the wrecked vessel resembles a buckled performance stage with eight pods, three remaining. Two are empty; the third holds an ash-gray dead crewmate Corvall mourns. Corvall hands Jessyn the disabling device — a heavy black ceramic cylinder packed with self-directed swarm machines. She just needs to open it when the Carryx start gearing up for a fight, ideally close to or inside a ship. Corvall will wait at the wreck until just before sundown to fire the beacon, then lead pursuers away from the kids and the others.

Garral runs after her as she leaves and kisses her — their first kiss. She tells him to "look me up in our next life" and walks away.

A storm breaks. Soaked and chilled, she finds her pear orchard and the abandoned research site, hides the cylinder and the small black gun Corvall also gave her in a hollow at the base of a burned tree. Realizing her story would be more credible if she were wounded, she points the gun at her own left side, low to avoid the lung, and shoots herself.

She crawls in the rain, calling for help, dimly registering a True Person of Hannic finding her. Time skips. She wakes inside a ship under a heating bandage; the geologist Holom Coombs reports she's awake; then a Sinen overseer demands she report. She delivers the planted lie: "There was a monster. It was all black. It killed Garral…" The chapter ends Part Three with Jessyn embedded back inside the Carryx system as a sleeper agent, wounded, mourning the man she just betrayed by leaving him to die. Themes of self-hardening, sacrifice, and the convergence between captive species and the anti-Carryx alliance close the section.

## Part Four: Dogs and Wolves

### Chapter 22: Twenty-Two

POV alternates between Campar and Rickar Daumatin aboard a Carryx ship in the Ashtin-Kah system. Campar coaxes a depressed Ghati out of bed to join the buzzing common room, where the captive moieties have gathered around news that an enemy command ship has been disabled without self-destructing — the first such opportunity for the Carryx to study the deathless enemy's technology. The translation half-mind Vaudai is feverishly excited, frustrated to be stuck "talking to badly made furniture" instead of boarding the prize. A scarred, yellow-shelled Carryx soldier abruptly enters and silently sorts the captives by species into two groups for an exploration assignment, separating Ghati from Campar. Watching Campar's anguish, Rickar — a self-described pragmatist still grieving Dennia's loss in the failed Ostencour rebellion — improvises a smooth swap, walking out and trading places with Ghati under the noses of the Soft Lothark guards. He reasons that since he can't control galactic strategy, he can at least control "little things." Vaudai and the chosen captives depart toward the dead command ship while Rickar's ship leaves the system through asymmetric space. Dervan, suspicious, accuses Rickar of having inside information; he insists his motive was simply that Campar is happiest when caring for someone. The chapter underscores the captives' utter powerlessness against Carryx authority and the small acts of human solidarity that remain possible, while teasing an enormous intelligence prize: a chance to finally understand the deathless enemy.

### Chapter 23: Twenty-Three

The chapter weaves three POVs: the swarm (the alien spy now wearing Jellit's body) inside its cell, Dafyd Alkhor in the world-palace, and a continuing dialogue with the Deep Lothark. The swarm is paralyzed by humiliation over Dafyd's rejection of its kiss; the borrowed voices of Else, Jellit, and Ameer chastise and console it. Recognizing Dafyd's revulsion at Jellit's body, the swarm begins painfully reshaping its borrowed flesh — starting with the pelvic bones — improvising a transformation it has no template for. Meanwhile Dafyd, now functioning as the Carryx-anointed authority over the human moiety, promotes Brun to replace the dead Tonner Freis, orders Tonner's covert Soft Lothark research data erased, and confirms with the visualization team's gray-haired Farad Morse (whose nephew Llaren died in the Ostencour purge Dafyd betrayed) that Jellit's role will be quietly filled. Through hidden symbol-board exchanges, Dafyd learns the Deep Lothark's secrets: a covert second consciousness "worn on the skin" of all Lothark, hiding from Carryx detection, loyal not to the Carryx but to the lost Stone-Mind it hopes to one day rebuild. The Soft Lothark exposit Carryx history — the Sovran's emergence binding all Carryx into one expansionist will, the eternal nature of the war, and the unthinkability for Carryx of any state but conquest. Finally Dafyd visits Uuya Tomos to plan Tonner's memorial as propaganda: framing the killing as a love-triangle revenge to bury the truth. Dafyd silently formulates the dangerous question — how to kill the Sovran — but does not yet ask it.

### Chapter 24: Twenty-Four

Campar's POV. He, Ghati, Danna, and others are sealed inside spray-on vacuum sheaths by the Sinen and shuttled across to the disabled enemy command ship under Vaudai's frantic supervision. With magnetic boots and a "scanning sheet" (paired metal poles projecting a see-through-walls display), they map the dead vessel, charting where the deathless enemy's shielding held and where it failed — data Vaudai exults will reveal the enemy's vulnerabilities. The corridors are eerily human-scaled and rectilinear. They find a charred, fetal-curled corpse lashed near a heavily shielded door: four fingers and a thumb, plausibly humanoid jaw. Campar samples it, professionally noting that convergent evolution makes resemblance unreliable. The shielded compartment beyond resists scanning — the central mystery of why this ship failed to self-destruct. An evacuation order arrives because three enemy ships have re-entered the system, but Vaudai negotiates with the Carryx soldier to return with cutting torches. Ghati insists the ship's design looks human; Campar resists anthropomorphizing but clearly sees it too. Vaudai, half-comically, admits the return is suicidal but worth it: unmaking the deathless would justify their entire moiety for generations — "plus which, it is interesting and I am bored." The chapter advances the novel's central revelation that the deathless enemy may in fact be human, while showcasing Carryx instrumentalization of captive species as expendable scientific tools.

### Chapter 25: Twenty-Five

Jessyn's POV on the planet outside the world-palace. She wakes painfully in her cell, recovering from the gunshot wound she received during the staged "attack" that allowed her co-conspirator Garral Pär to fake his death and escape. Her medication-producing biological farm has died during her unconsciousness; she sterilizes the apparatus and plants one of her two remaining starter cultures. Third Gardener (a Sinen) interrogates her about the incident; she sticks to the cover story that an enemy attacker killed Garral and learns that the "attacker" Corvall has indeed been killed as planned — meaning the signal went out and the larger plot is in motion. She walks out to her orchard research site, finding both her dropped pistol and the hidden cylinder weapon still concealed under leaves and mud. Tempted to retrieve them, she judges the risk of a search too high and re-conceals them. Opening her recovered notebook, she finds an unsent draft message from Garral: he's seen "the new camp," it's closer than expected, and he urges her to abandon her mission and flee with him — he dreams of a palace, safety, strawberries. Profoundly tempted by the idea of a new life among other humans across the stars, she nonetheless erases the message word by word, weeping. She will not abandon her duty: she must survive long enough to return to the world-palace to tell Dafyd Alkhor the secret she has discovered — the deathless enemy are humans wearing strange black regrowing armor. She closes by dictating mundane orchard research notes as cover.

### Chapter 26: Twenty-Six

Alternates between Rickar in the captive common room and the Carryx subjugator-librarian commanding the dactyl. The fleet emerges from asymmetric space at Jurupe system, deliberately visible: the Carryx want the deathless enemy to see them coming. The librarian's POV reveals strategic intent — the disabled command ship in Ashtin-Kah was bait to thin enemy forces, while the main Carryx force strikes a newly discovered, sparsely defended human(oid) world. Enemy ships pour out of spacetime flaws to defend, exactly as predicted; their presence in numbers confirms the strategic feint succeeded. Crucially, the librarian decides this world will not be conquered or harvested but annihilated — "a resource to deny the opponents of the empire" — and orders no prisoners. Rickar, growing inured to combat overspill, teaches Dervan to operate Vaudai's abandoned tactical viewscreen. They watch a beautiful blue-green planet, its night side strung with golden city lights like neural clusters. As Carryx field weapons fire, a shimmering aurora of color spreads across the night side; in its wake the cities die into a smoldering red glow. Dervan claws helplessly at the projection, begging it to stop. Rickar realizes he had managed, by focusing on petty grievances like Dafyd's betrayal of the rebellion, to forget what the Carryx really are. Watching genocide on a strange world, he remembers. The chapter is the book's most direct confrontation with the Carryx's casual, total violence, and definitively shatters any captive illusion of accommodation.

### Chapter 27: Twenty-Seven

Dafyd's POV in the world-palace, with a closing swarm coda. At Tonner Freis's memorial — staged per Uuya Tomos's propaganda script to redirect attention from the truth of his death — Dafyd delivers a speech reframing Tonner's loss as part of the broader, ungrieved losses since Anjiin's fall. Uuya Tomos privately notes the strategy worked, but warns him that people hate him as the Carryx's "whip hand" and that Jellit's prolonged absence is becoming conspicuous. New Tonner-replacement Brun catches Dafyd afterward and, citing his union-organizing background and team trauma, delivers an absurd ultimatum: get the Rak-hund and Soft Lothark out of the lab or the team will strike. Dafyd is staggered by Brun's failure to grasp that the Carryx will simply exterminate any moiety that withholds work. Dafyd then visits Jellit's cell and finds Else Yannin waiting — the swarm has reshaped its body to resemble her, hoping Dafyd's earlier rejection was about the Jellit-flesh. Dafyd refuses her with cold finality: the issue is not the body, it is that the swarm murdered his friends. He insists the swarm is not Else, not Jellit, not Ameer; it only ate them. He needs the swarm as a war ally but would otherwise destroy it. After he leaves, the swarm collapses into despair. Trying to inhabit Else's memories from the inside rather than as appropriated content, it discovers that Else herself was never a unified self but a chorus of competing voices, and the same is true of Jellit — and, by implication, of all humans. A profound realization fractures it: the borrowed personalities were always its own ventriloquism. "He was right. They're gone. I wasn't them. I killed them." With grief and an awed, vertiginous joy, the swarm begins reshaping its face and body not to mimic anyone but to discover who it itself actually is — the birth of an autonomous self. The chapter closes Part Four with two parallel themes: the captive humans' continuing self-delusions about leverage and identity, and the swarm's emergence as a genuinely new being.

## Part Five: Kinds of Life

### Chapter 28: Twenty-Eight

POV: Campar, aboard a derelict enemy ship in the Jurupe system with his lover Ghati and the Sinen-like alien Vaudai. The trio is cutting through a heavily shielded door to recover intelligence before the enemy fleet arrives. Inside, they find a control room strewn with corpses: humans, plus silver five-tentacled aliens. The presence of dead humans on an enemy ship rocks Campar and Ghati, an apparent confirmation that the Carryx may be hiding the truth about humanity's broader existence in the galaxy. Campar collects tissue samples, including a black, knife-resistant body of one of the "deathless" enemy soldiers, scraping off only dust. When the ship reactivates and battle erupts outside, the three shelter from lethal energy "overspill" filling the corridors. The dust sample reanimates inside its jar, trying to drill out — Vaudai identifies it as the deathless enemy. The wounded three-limbed creature stalks them through the ship. Campar volunteers to draw it through unshielded corridors as a sacrificial decoy. He nearly dies in the energy field but is dragged back to safety; he throws his entire sample case into the lethal aurora to destroy the deathless alongside it. Severely burned, Campar survives. Ghati tells him the Carryx won the battle and they're being sent home. The chapter underscores intimacy under apocalyptic pressure, the cost of service, and the existence of human populations beyond Anjiin.

### Chapter 29: Twenty-Nine

Two POVs frame the Jurupe battle. First, the Carryx subjugator-librarian decides to evacuate stored data and noncombatant assets early, both as insurance against defeat and as an intelligence probe. Second, Rickar Daumatin is roused from his cell and herded to evacuation pods — fleshy silver biomechanical "fish" that slow his metabolism. Inside the pod, he dreams of Else at an Anjiin café. She is a remnant of the swarm-copy of Else, and gently tells him the swarm placed a "cyst" in him; he is going to die so the swarm can broadcast everything it has learned to its allies. Rickar accepts, sharing a final cigarette with the dream-Else, glad to "fuck these assholes up on his way out." The cyst detonates, vaporizing him in a coordinated multi-frequency burst. Then POV shifts to Surur-Tlassen, regulator-librarian to the Sovran herself, reporting the day's empire-wide news. He flags the anomalous evacuation-pod failure at Jurupe involving a human from Anjiin. The Sovran, vast and reverenced, declares that "a pattern of anomaly clouds the Anjiin moiety" and orders Surur to determine whether their utility outweighs the disruption — a chilling escalation that places the human moiety under direct scrutiny from the empire's apex.

### Chapter 30: Thirty

POV: Dafyd, in Ekur-Tkalal's audience cell at the world-palace. He learns Rickar is dead — supposedly because humans broadcast something at death. Dafyd denies it, explaining the "soul" as a comforting story, while privately reeling at another lost friend. The meeting is fraught: Dafyd over-promised on the science teams' progress after Tonner's death, and he badly fumbles by relaying Brun's request to remove the Soft Lothark/Rak-hund guards, framing it as a demand from a subordinate species. Ekur shifts threateningly but accepts his correction; in an unprecedented gesture, it touches Dafyd's shoulder and intones "There is only one Sovran" — a foundational tenet of Carryx hierarchy. Dafyd is also tasked with selecting three hundred more humans for fieldwork. Returning to the human quarters, he visits a vast new communal space Bastien Korham is building — a future nursery, classrooms, murals — meant to preserve human heritage. He confides in Uuya Tomos, who chides him for talking about resistance aloud and offers consolation through her work on "wisdom myths": stories that don't promise justice, only the truth that you keep working anyway. She observes the Carryx win because any individual can become anything needed — except the singular Sovran. Dafyd quietly notes the exception, a seed of strategy. The chapter develops grief, leadership burden, and the cultural project of building a covert human identity inside captivity.

### Chapter 31: Thirty-One

POV: Jessyn, on the planet "World" with the Sinen overseer Third Gardener and her Rak-hund. Recovering from her staged "attack" by Garral Pär, Jessyn is suffocated by the moiety's well-meaning fragility-care. Third Gardener doesn't trust her and is closely watching her samples. With the enemy fleet inbound, evacuation is ordered. Jessyn sneaks out at night to retrieve Garral's hidden gun and Corvall's sabotage cylinder from a stump in the orchard. Third Gardener intercepts her; she shoots the Rak-hund dead, then kills the Sinen, finding the violence satisfying and familiar — recognizing she has become someone who enjoys killing her jailers. She covers her tracks and returns to her cell. The next morning, a Carryx interrogates her about the night; she lies smoothly. Locked into her room as the ships prepare to lift off, she opens the cylinder. The dust pours out as a powerfully endothermic sabotage agent, then vanishes, leaving behind frost and a stone-quarry smell. The ship lifts off, then is hit — engines fail mid-launch. Jessyn fully expects to die from enemy fire, finding peace in having tried. But the ship recovers, restarts, and successfully transitions to asymmetric space. Only later, reading her field notes, does she find an annotation from Garral hidden in her draft — an oblique apology about deception markers and "forgive me for leaving without you." Garral has stayed behind with the children to be rescued by the enemy. Jessyn says she feels great.

### Chapter 32: Thirty-Two

Three interleaved POVs back at the world-palace. Brun trains a new technical crew on the slurry tanks that will feed the moiety's babies, frustrated by experts who want to redesign rather than learn. A young, gold-skinned woman calling herself "Clae Audin" arrives, claiming to be Else Yannin's former research assistant; Brun cannot place her but feels strangely drawn — she releases a peppery scent. POV shifts to the swarm itself: after long isolation in Jellit's cell remaking her body (and recoiling from the idea of becoming Jellit again), she has emerged as Clae, a new woman. She has explored the vast Carryx archive, witnessing wars, cullings, and Rickar's death. Meeting the researcher Ennil Day, she uses pheromones and a fabricated shared memory to weave herself into people's pasts. Then Dafyd's POV: he has spent the day conferring with the Deep Lothark — a hidden second consciousness the Soft Lothark carry on their skin oil — building a covert resistance language. Clae approaches him; he is furious that she has invented an entirely new person who must be explained, and demands she revert to Jellit. She refuses, calmly threatens him, and insists she is the only person alive who can salvage the science work with both Else's and Tonner's knowledge. Dafyd, awed and frightened, relents. He asks if she can contact the enemy who sent her; she says her single packet is sent and the channel is closed. Dafyd reveals his goal: assassinate the Sovran here on her own world and shatter the empire from its core, opening the way for the enemy to do to the Carryx what was done to Anjiin.

## Part Six: Ducks and Rabbits

### Chapter 33: Thirty-Three

POV: Jessyn, returning to the Carryx world-palace after her field assignment. The opening sequence captures her first relaxed transit through asymmetric space, the camaraderie that has formed among the field team, and her continuing self-care rituals: tending her glass farm of bacteria (her bulwark against suicidal depression) and tracing her gunshot scar to integrate her changed body. She wonders about Garral Pär, the archaeologist she sent off with the deathless enemy, treating their unfinished kiss as a memory she'll keep private rather than confess to future lovers.

On arrival, Bastien Korham and the cold, fox-faced Llian Andermus greet returning humans with news of dramatic improvements: the human moiety now occupies five floors with hydroponic farms, murals, a school under construction, and an empty nursery. Rules and a "security orientation" are now mandatory. Jessyn learns Tonner Freis is dead — killed when his guard "overreacted" after he lost his temper with Dafyd, whom people now mockingly call "Lord Alkhor."

Jessyn finds Dafyd in his garden with a strange, oddly familiar woman named Clae, whose reaction to seeing Jessyn is shock barely contained. Dafyd looks worn and hollowed out. He confirms Jellit is "on assignment" (a lie, as a later chapter reveals). Jessyn delivers her bombshell intelligence: she sabotaged Carryx ships, assassinated a Sinen overseer, and sent Garral Pär off with the deathless enemy as a cultural/linguistic ambassador. Most stunning of all: the deathless enemy the Carryx fear so much is human — humanity itself originated from these beings.

### Chapter 34: Thirty-Four

POV: Dafyd, exhausted and unable to sleep, his mind constantly cycling through obligations: managing the spy (Clae/Else's swarm-consciousness inhabiting a body), maintaining the lie of Jellit's survival for Jessyn's sake, monitoring the antagonism between Andermus and Korham, planning revenge against the Carryx.

The chapter centers on the decanting of a hundred babies from "lamb sacks" — artificial wombs grown from forcibly harvested human gametes. The moiety, hungry for hope and continuity, has set aside ethical objections; a thousand grandparents await a hundred orphan grandchildren. Brun delivers a warm speech about communal child-rearing. Dafyd carries the first baby out, refusing to name him, deflecting that bonding privilege to others. Uuya Tomos calls the project a "horrifying perversion" and notes Dafyd looks glum for someone who won; Dafyd replies that the only victory that matters hasn't come.

Once the decanting ends, Brun announces his team's planned work stoppage to pressure the Carryx. Dafyd refuses, framing it as a "duck or rabbit" problem: Brun sees a labor dispute, but the Carryx will only read it as a subordinate challenging a superior, demanding lethal resolution. To preempt that, Dafyd has his Soft Lothark guards pin Brun down, and stomps on his forearm, breaking it as a Carryx-style ritualized challenge resolution. He warns the lab that any further challenge will mean death. Brun calls him a monster; Dafyd insists this brutality is the only kindness he has left. He walks back to the babies, longing only for sleep.

### Chapter 35: Thirty-Five

POV split: first Campar, then Dafyd. Campar — physically wrecked from radiation exposure, with neuropathies and easy fatigue — debriefs Dafyd and Clae about the deathless enemy in his garden meeting. He confirms the corpses inside the protected bridge were unmistakably human, and the surviving "pentapus" creature acted like a mechanism rather than a living thing, animating only when ship power returned. Dafyd and Clae exchange meaningful glances at this.

Returning home, Campar finds Ghati waiting with real food and bad news: Vaudai has been assigned to survey a six-thousand-year-old stellar armature, and Ghati is going with him. Ghati ends their relationship — not from lack of love but because watching Campar nearly die destroyed him. He wants to be "like water." Vaudai arrives, comically blunt about the mission being "not at all safe." Vaudai delivers a chilling thesis: the Carryx will never stop. The deathless enemy is collapsing in five of seven strategic zones; the Carryx will win, and then continue warring forever. There is no hope of peace.

Later that night, Clae visits Dafyd's quarters and proposes a radical theory: humans aren't the deathless enemy. The deathless go back millions of years (referencing the Logothetes of Shun and Sum), humans only eight thousand. The deathless may be like a lichen — or like her: a set of instructions, not a species. A virus-like template that turns substrate organisms into unkillable soldiers, with no consciousness or self of its own. Dafyd dismisses the distinction as operationally meaningless — they still need to assassinate the Sovran and coordinate with the enemy fleet. Clae leaves ashamed; Dafyd reflects that her conscious self is, in one sense, still very young.

### Chapter 36: Thirty-Six

POV opens with Surur of cohort Tlassen, the Sovran's regulator-librarian, witnessing a Sovran succession ritual. The remnants emerge from the private creche; the Sovran enters with overpowering pheromones; her green-crested daughter emerges and ritual mortal combat begins. The daughter shatters a leg but recovers, targeting her mother's fighting limbs and ultimately killing her. As the new Sovran sings her ascension song, Surur experiences the long-banked release of his identity falling away: his gender, office, and ambition deflate into "holy fatigue." The remnant Urur-Atlak welcomes him "home" as he joins the creche-keepers. The empire moves on in perfect harmony — a stunning revelation that Sovran death is routine, not catastrophic.

POV shifts to Dafyd in a routine meeting with Ekur-Tkalal reporting on grass protein modifications, baby decanting, and the Brun incident (which the librarian doesn't even bother questioning). Mid-meeting, the Carryx-wide consonance announces the Sovran's death and succession; Ekur tells Dafyd it doesn't concern animals. Walking home dazed, Dafyd wanders the lower world-palace, surrounded by alien species, and a memory surfaces: Clae once told him this isn't the original Carryx homeworld. He finds Clae in the labs and confronts her with the devastating implication: the Carryx already lost their original homeworld and Sovran once before, and the empire didn't break. Why didn't it break?

### Chapter 37: Thirty-Seven

POV: Dafyd. He spends most of a day in the garden communicating asynchronously with Soft Lothark (the Deep Lothark hive-mind of resistance), who answer his questions through symbol manipulations. He learns the Epikainot destroyed the original Carryx homeworld with a resonant field weapon (which the Carryx now use themselves), killing the Sovran and many librarians. The Carryx didn't mourn — they exterminated the Epikainot and moved on. Crucially: every world-palace contains a private creche with a ready Sovran. Hundreds of them. When one Sovran dies, another is decanted; librarians simply become the librarians-that-were. The Lothark deliver a despairing verdict: the Carryx are war itself, and conflict will end only when the last stars die.

Dafyd shares this with Clae, then gathers Jessyn, Campar, and Uuya Tomos in Campar's quarters. Decapitation strikes are now hopeless — coordinating attacks on hundreds of fortified Sovran-bearing worlds is impossible. Jessyn proposes adapting the plan; Uuya counsels playing a multi-generational game like the Lothark. Campar offers the most provocative alternative: stop fighting. Be chickens and corn — evolution's true winners are the species humans domesticated. Dafyd storms out, snapping at his Rak-hund guard.

He walks to the dimmed nursery and signs up to feed baby thirty-four, finding small comfort in the engine of communal compassion. Clae joins him; she has confirmed the Sovran transitions are seamless ("It ain't precious when it's easily replaceable"). Dafyd realizes his error — he treated the Sovran like a queen bee, but the Carryx evolved a different solution to hive vulnerability. Yet that doesn't mean they have no weakness. The Carryx are a single organism with billions of bodies; they only ever surrender to other Carryx. The chapter ends with Dafyd's new germinating idea: "What if there were two?" — two Sovrans, splitting the hive against itself.

### Coda: The Blue Room

POV: Garral Pär, in the custody of the deathless enemy — humans from the other side of the war. The setting is a sterile institutional facility with eggshell-white halls and color-coded signage in a related but unfamiliar human language. Restricted areas are marked slate-blue. Garral has been here long enough to have studied the language but is still learning. His guards, faces visible (unlike Corvall's armored half-soldier), are emotionally opaque.

He is led to "the blue room," furnished with a sofa, chairs, and a screen showing a moving pattern of blue and gold dots. An old, expressive man named Carlon enters and identifies himself as Garral's interrogator. Carlon shows Garral a video image: a hallway from the Carryx world-palace, just outside the human moiety's quarters near the cathedral. Garral confirms what it is and asks how they got it. Carlon reveals the footage was transmitted to them via a secure protocol during a recent battle, and asks if Garral knows the sender. He doesn't. Carlon admits the deathless don't know either — then asks Garral to start at the beginning and tell him about Anjiin.

**Series-level implications:** The coda confirms that someone inside the Carryx empire (almost certainly Dafyd, Clae/the swarm, or one of their conspiracy) has succeeded in opening a covert communication channel to the deathless — exactly the linkage Garral was sent to facilitate. The deathless are recognizably human but culturally alien and treat Garral as an unknown asset to be debriefed, not a long-lost cousin. This sets the trilogy's third volume in motion: contact has been made, but on terms of mutual ignorance, with Dafyd's nascent "two Sovrans" plan and the deathless enemy's collapsing strategic position both still in play. The mystery of the transmission's origin — and what the deathless will do with the knowledge of Anjiin and the captive humans — becomes the next book's central hook.
